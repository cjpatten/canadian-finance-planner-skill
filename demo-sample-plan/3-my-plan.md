This plan gets them from "surviving" to "building wealth" in 3 phases.

## Phase 3: Accelerate (2+ years, after debt-free)

**Goal:** Maximize wealth-building. Target retirement at 60/58.

### Monthly Allocation After All Consumer Debt Paid Off (~May 2028)

Freed-up cash: $921/month (from debt payments) + continued spending discipline

| Priority | Allocation | Monthly | Why |
|----------|-----------|---------|-----|
| 1 | Sarah's RRSP | $400 | Lower her taxable income, boost CCB, build retirement outside HOOPP |
| 2 | Marcus's TFSA | $300 | Tax-free growth, no OAS clawback impact in retirement |
| 3 | Sarah's TFSA | $200 | Max contribution room, tax-free growth |
| 4 | Kitchen renovation fund | $200 | $25K goal, ~10 years or use windfall |

**Why Sarah's RRSP first:**
- Sarah's marginal rate: ~29.65% (federal 20.5% + Ontario 9.15%)
- $400/month RRSP contribution = $4,800/year = ~$1,423 tax refund
- That tax refund also lowers family net income, potentially increasing CCB by ~$200-400/year
- Reinvest the refund into the RRSP or TFSA for compound effect

**Why NOT Marcus's RRSP:**
- Marcus has HOOPP — one of the best DB pensions in Canada
- His pension + CPP + OAS will likely put him in a similar or higher bracket in retirement
- Better to use TFSA (tax-free withdrawals won't trigger OAS clawback)

### Spousal RRSP Consideration
- Marcus has $22,400 in RRSP room he's not using
- If Marcus contributes to a Spousal RRSP for Sarah: HE gets the tax deduction at his higher marginal rate (~33.9%), SHE withdraws in retirement at her lower rate
- Recommendation: Once Phase 2 is complete and there's cash flow, contribute $200/month to a Spousal RRSP in Sarah's name, in addition to her own RRSP contributions. After the 3-year attribution period, the funds are fully Sarah's.

**Bridge years (age 58-65):** HOOPP pension starts at 60 for Marcus. Sarah needs 7 years of bridge income (age 58-65) from TFSA and RRSP drawdowns before CPP/OAS kick in.

**Target retirement income:** 70% of pre-retirement = ~$110K gross → ~$88K net. The projection shows they're close but may need to either work to 62/60 instead of 60/58, or increase savings in Phase 3.

**Retirement gap:** ~$22K/year shortfall in early retirement years (before CPP/OAS). This can be closed by:
- Working 2 extra years (retiring at 62/60 instead of 60/58)
- Increasing TFSA contributions by $150/month starting in Phase 3

## Mortgage Renewal Strategy (June 2026)

Current: $412,000 at 4.89% fixed, $2,340/month, renews June 2026.

**Options to evaluate at renewal:**
1. **Fixed rate (current market ~4.5-5.2%):** Predictability, easier budgeting. Best if rates stay flat or rise.
2. **Variable rate (current market ~4.0-4.8%):** Historically saves money over full term. Riskier month-to-month.
3. **Shorter amortization:** If they can handle higher payments post-debt-payoff, reducing from 25 to 20 years saves significant interest.

**Recommendation:** At renewal, strongly consider accelerating payments if the budget allows. Even an extra $100/month on a $412K mortgage at ~5% saves ~$18,000 in interest and cuts 2+ years off the amortization.

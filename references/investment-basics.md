common investment vehicles — not specific investment advice.

## 1. Investment Options Overview

Present these in order from simplest/safest to more growth-oriented:

| Option | Risk Level | Expected Return | Liquidity | Best For |
|--------|-----------|----------------|-----------|----------|
| High-Interest Savings Account | Very Low | 2–3% | Instant | Emergency fund, short-term savings |
| GICs (Guaranteed Investment Certificates) | Very Low | 3–4% | Locked (1–5 years) | Money you won't need for a set period |
| HISA ETFs (e.g., CASH.TO, PSA.TO) | Very Low | 2–3% | Daily (sell on TSX) | Parking cash in a brokerage account |
| Conservative All-in-One ETF (40/60) | Low-Medium | 5–7% long-term | Daily | Near-retirees, cautious investors |
| Balanced All-in-One ETF (60/40) | Medium | 6–8% long-term | Daily | Mid-career, moderate risk tolerance |
| Growth All-in-One ETF (80/20) | Medium-High | 7–10% long-term | Daily | Long time horizon (10+ years), comfortable with dips |
| All-Equity ETF (100/0) | High | 8–12% long-term | Daily | Very long horizon (15+ years), high risk tolerance |

**Key message to users:** "You don't need to pick stocks. You don't need to watch markets.
A single all-in-one ETF gives you instant diversification across thousands of companies
worldwide. You buy one thing and you're done."

## 2. All-in-One ETFs — The Easiest Way to Invest

These are the most popular options in Canada. Both Vanguard and iShares (BlackRock) offer
nearly identical products at nearly identical costs. Either family is a great choice.

Match the ETF to their risk profile based on age, timeline, and comfort with volatility:

| Profile | Equity/Bond Split | Vanguard | iShares | Who It's For |
|---------|------------------|----------|---------|-------------|
| Conservative | 40% / 60% | VCNS | — | Within 5 years of retirement, very risk-averse |
| Balanced | 60% / 40% | VBAL | XBAL | 10–15 years to goal, moderate risk tolerance |

**Why these and not others?**

- One purchase gives you exposure to 13,000+ stocks and bonds worldwide
- Automatic rebalancing — the fund maintains the target equity/bond split for you

fully by mid-2023 and went on to see 20%+ gains. The lesson: don't sell during dips."

## 5. How to Pick the Right Option

Use this decision tree when advising users:

```
Need the money within 1 year?
  → YES → High-interest savings account (EQ Bank, Saven, etc.)
  → NO ↓

Need it in 1–3 years?
  → YES → GIC or GIC ladder at an online bank
  → NO ↓

Need it in 3–5 years?
  → YES → GIC ladder OR conservative ETF (VCNS)
  → NO ↓

Need it in 5–10 years?
  → YES → Balanced ETF (VBAL/XBAL) or Growth ETF (VGRO/XGRO)
  → NO ↓

Won't need it for 10+ years?
  → YES → Growth ETF (VGRO/XGRO) or All-Equity (VEQT/XEQT)
```

**Risk tolerance adjustment:** If the user says they'd panic and sell during a -20% year,
move them one step more conservative regardless of timeline.

## 6. Investment Growth Comparison (for Dashboard Chart)

This powers the "Investment Growth Comparison" chart in the dashboard. The chart shows how
a fixed monthly investment grows over time across different vehicles.

### Chart Specification

**Title:** "How Your Money Grows: Investment Comparison"

**Type:** Line chart (Chart.js) with multiple datasets

**X-Axis:** Years (1 to 30)

**Y-Axis:** Portfolio value ($)

**Inputs from user's plan:**
- Monthly investment amount (from their suggested budget allocation)
- Current age and target retirement age (determines time horizon)

**Datasets (lines on the chart):**

| Line | Label | Annual Return | Colour | Style |
|------|-------|--------------|--------|-------|
| 1 | Big 5 Savings Account | 0.75% | Grey | Dashed |
| 2 | Online HISA | 2.75% | Light Blue | Dashed |
| 3 | GIC Ladder | 3.50% | Blue | Dashed |
| 4 | Balanced ETF (VBAL/XBAL) | 6.5% | Green | Solid |
| 5 | Growth ETF (VGRO/XGRO) | 8.5% | Orange | Solid |
| 6 | All-Equity ETF (VEQT/XEQT) | 10.5% | Red | Solid |

**Growth formula (compound monthly):**
```
FV = PMT × [((1 + r/12)^(n×12) - 1) / (r/12)]
```
Where PMT = monthly contribution, r = annual return, n = years.

**Example callout on chart (annotation):**
For $500/month over 25 years:
- Big 5 Savings: $158K (you contributed $150K — only $8K in growth)
- Online HISA: $210K
- GIC Ladder: $231K
- Balanced ETF: $378K
- Growth ETF: $487K
- All-Equity ETF: $630K

**Key insight to display below chart:**
"The difference between a Big 5 savings account and a balanced ETF over 25 years is
$220,000 — on the same $500/month. That's the cost of not investing."

### Important Notes for Chart Generation

- Use the user's ACTUAL monthly investment amount from their plan, not examples
- Highlight the line that matches their risk profile (from the decision tree above)
- ETF return assumptions should use long-term historical averages, not recent years
- Always include disclaimer: "Returns shown are historical averages. Actual returns will vary
  year to year. ETF values can go down as well as up."
- The savings/GIC lines are guaranteed; the ETF lines are projections based on historical data

## 7. Where to Buy These

Don't recommend specific platforms by name, but describe the categories:

| Type | Cost | Best For | Examples (mention generically) |
|------|------|----------|-------------------------------|
| Online discount brokerage | $0 per trade | DIY investors, all-in-one ETFs | "Most major banks and several independent platforms offer commission-free ETF purchases" |
| Robo-advisor / managed platform | 0.25–0.50% + MER | "Set and forget" investors | "Several Canadian platforms will invest for you automatically based on your risk profile" |
| Bank financial advisor | 1–2% MER (mutual funds) | People who want hand-holding | "Convenient but costs 5–10x more than DIY — show the math" |

**Key message:** "If you're comfortable buying something online, you can buy an all-in-one
ETF yourself for $0 commission. It takes about 15 minutes once your account is set up.
If you'd rather not think about it, a managed investing platform charges about 0.25–0.50%
on top of the ETF fee — still much cheaper than a bank mutual fund."

**The fee math (always show this):**
On a $100,000 portfolio over 25 years at 8% return:
- DIY ETF (0.20% MER): ~$632K final value — $50K in fees
- Managed platform (0.70% total): ~$582K — $100K in fees
- Bank mutual fund (2.00% total): ~$462K — $220K in fees

"The difference between 0.20% and 2.00% in fees is $170,000 on $100K over 25 years."

## 8. Common Questions

**"Is my money safe in an ETF?"**
"ETFs hold real stocks and bonds on your behalf. If the ETF provider went bankrupt, your
assets are held separately by a custodian — you don't lose them. This is very different
from a company going bankrupt. That said, the value of an ETF goes up and down with the
market — there's no guarantee on the value like a GIC."

**"Should I invest in my RRSP or TFSA first?"**
Refer to the main plan — this depends on their income, tax bracket, and goals. Generally:
- Income under ~$55K → TFSA first (low marginal tax rate, RRSP deduction less valuable)
- Income over ~$55K → RRSP first (higher marginal rate, bigger tax refund)
- If employer matches RRSP → always RRSP first (free money)

**"What about individual stocks?"**
"This skill doesn't recommend individual stocks. An all-in-one ETF already holds thousands
of companies — Apple, Amazon, Royal Bank, Shopify, and thousands more. Picking individual
stocks is gambling unless you're willing to do serious research. Most professionals can't
beat the index long-term."

**"What about crypto?"**
Refer to `references/scenarios-and-coaching.md` for crypto guidance.

**"I only have $50/month to invest — is it worth it?"**
"Absolutely. $50/month in a growth ETF for 30 years at 8.5% becomes ~$82,000. That's
$18,000 contributed and $64,000 in growth. Start small and increase as you can."

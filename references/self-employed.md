## 2. Business Structure Decision Tree

### Sole Proprietorship
- **Simplest structure.** No separate legal entity — you ARE the business.
- Business income reported on your personal T1 return (Form T2125).
- Unlimited personal liability (your personal assets are at risk).
- No separate tax filing or corporate maintenance.
- All business income taxed at your personal marginal rate.
- **Best for:** Side hustles, freelancers earning under $50K-$70K, testing a business idea.

### Partnership
- Two or more people sharing business income/losses.
- Each partner reports their share on their personal T1 return.
- Partnership files an information return (T5013) but doesn't pay tax itself.
- Can be general (shared liability) or limited (limited liability for limited partners).
- **Best for:** Professional practices (law firms, accounting firms), joint ventures.

### Incorporation (CCPC)
- Separate legal entity. The corporation earns income and pays corporate tax.
- You're an employee/shareholder — take salary, dividends, or both.
- Limited liability (personal assets generally protected from business debts).
- More complex: separate tax return (T2), annual filings, legal/accounting costs ($2,000-$5,000/year).
- Access to Small Business Deduction (9% federal on first $500K active income).
- **Best for:** Businesses earning $70K+ net profit, need liability protection, want tax deferral.

### Professional Corporation
- Ontario, BC, Quebec, Alberta allow professionals (doctors, lawyers, accountants, engineers,
  dentists) to incorporate.
- Same tax benefits as regular CCPC (SBD, salary/dividend flexibility).
- Professional liability is NOT limited by incorporation (malpractice still personal).
- Ownership restrictions: only the licensed professional can own voting shares (family members
  can hold non-voting shares in some provinces).
- **Check provincial rules** — each province has different requirements for professional corps.

### Decision Framework
```
Net business income < $50K/year → Sole proprietorship (keep it simple)
Net business income $50K-$70K → Consider incorporation (run the tax math)
Net business income > $70K → Likely incorporate (SBD savings meaningful)
Need liability protection → Incorporate regardless of income
Multiple business owners → Partnership or incorporated partnership
Professional (doctor/lawyer/etc.) → Professional corporation
```

## 3. When to Incorporate (Tax Math)

The key question: **does the corporate tax rate save enough to justify the cost of incorporation?**

### The Math (Ontario Example, 2025 — verify)

**Sole proprietor earning $150K net profit:**
- Personal tax (federal + Ontario): ~$38,000-$42,000
- Net after tax: ~$108,000-$112,000

**Incorporated CCPC earning $150K, paying $75K salary + retaining $75K:**
- Corporate tax on $75K retained: ~$9,150 (12.2% combined)
- Personal tax on $75K salary: ~$14,000-$16,000
- Total tax: ~$23,000-$25,000
- **Savings: ~$15,000-$19,000/year** (tax deferral — you'll pay personal tax when you eventually
  take the retained earnings as dividends)

**But factor in costs:**
- Accounting/tax prep: $2,000-$5,000/year
- Legal/annual filings: $500-$1,000/year
- Bookkeeping: $1,200-$3,600/year (if not DIY)
- **Total overhead: $3,700-$9,600/year**

**Net benefit of incorporation:** $5,000-$15,000/year at $150K income.
At $70K income: marginal benefit after costs. Below $50K: costs exceed savings.

### Key Concept: Tax Deferral, Not Tax Elimination
Corporate tax is lower than personal tax, so you **defer** tax on retained earnings. But when
you eventually take the money out as dividends, you pay personal tax on it. The "integration"
system means total tax (corporate + personal) is designed to be roughly equal to what you'd
pay as a sole proprietor. The advantage is **timing** — you control WHEN you pay the personal
portion.

**The real savings come from:**
1. Leaving money in the corp to grow (tax-deferred investing)
2. Taking money out in low-income years (lower marginal rate)
3. Income splitting with family members (within TOSI rules)
4. Accessing IPP (higher pension contributions than RRSP)
5. Using the LCGE on sale ($1,250,000 exemption)

## 4. Salary vs Dividends Optimization

This is the most common question from incorporated business owners. There's no universal
answer — it depends on their specific situation.

### Salary Advantages
- Creates RRSP contribution room (18% of salary, up to ~$32,490 limit, 2025 — verify)
- Builds CPP credits (employee 5.95% + employer 5.95% = 11.9% on earnings up to YMPE $71,300)
- Qualifies for EI (employee 1.58% + employer 2.21%, 2025 — verify) — useful for parental leave
- **Mortgage qualification:** Lenders strongly prefer T4 income. Salary makes qualifying easier.
- Corporate deduction is immediate (reduces corporate taxable income)
- Childcare expense deduction based on earned income (salary counts, dividends don't)

### Dividend Advantages
- No CPP contributions (saves ~$4,200 employee portion, but lose pension credits)
- No EI premiums (saves ~$1,050 employee portion, but no EI benefits)
- Simpler admin (no payroll, no T4, no source deductions)
- Dividend tax credit makes effective tax rate roughly equivalent to salary (integration)
- Flexible timing (declare dividends when beneficial, not on a payroll schedule)
- Can pay dividends to family shareholders (within TOSI rules — see below)

### The Blend Strategy (Most Common Recommendation)
Pay yourself a **salary up to the RRSP limit threshold** (~$180,000 salary generates max
$32,490 RRSP room), then take additional income as **dividends.**

**Example (Ontario, 2025):**
- Corp earns $200K net profit
- Pay $90K salary: creates $16,200 RRSP room, builds CPP, qualifies for mortgage
- Declare $60K in eligible dividends: lower effective tax rate via dividend tax credit
- Retain $50K in corporation: taxed at 12.2%, available for future use
- **Total personal income: $150K.** Total tax: ~$32,000-$36,000.

### Tax on Split Income (TOSI) Rules
Since 2018, paying dividends to family members (spouse, adult children) who don't meaningfully
contribute to the business is heavily penalized — dividends are taxed at the top marginal rate.

**Exceptions (dividends NOT subject to TOSI):**
- Spouse age 65+ (pension income splitting equivalent)
- Adult child who works 20+ hours/week in the business
- Family member who contributed significant capital
- Reasonable salary for actual work performed (salary is NOT subject to TOSI)

**Bottom line:** You can still pay your spouse a reasonable salary for actual work. You can't
just sprinkle dividends to family members who don't contribute.

(Source: canada.ca/TOSI, taxtips.ca)

## 5. Small Business Deduction by Province

The SBD reduces the corporate tax rate on the first $500K of active business income for CCPCs.
(Some provinces have increased this limit — see below.)

### Combined Federal + Provincial Rates on SBD Income (2025 — verify)

| Province | SBD Rate | General Corporate Rate | SBD Limit |
|----------|---------|----------------------|-----------|
| Ontario | 12.2% | 26.5% | $500,000 |
| Quebec | 12.2% | 26.5% | $500,000 |
| BC | 11.0% | 27.0% | $500,000 |
| Alberta | 11.0% | 23.0% | $500,000 |
| Manitoba | 9.0% | 27.0% | $500,000 |
| Saskatchewan | 9.0% | 27.0% | $600,000 |
| Nova Scotia | 11.5% | 29.0% | $700,000 |
| New Brunswick | 11.5% | 29.0% | $500,000 |
| Newfoundland | 12.0% | 30.0% | $500,000 |
| PEI | 10.0% | 31.0% | $600,000 |
| Yukon | 9.0% | 27.0% | $500,000 |
| NWT | 11.0% | 26.5% | $500,000 |
| Nunavut | 12.0% | 27.0% | $500,000 |

(Source: KPMG corporate tax rates, taxtips.ca — verify all)

### The SBD Cliff
Above the SBD limit, income is taxed at the **general corporate rate** (23-31% depending on
province). This is a sharp increase — going from 12% to 27% on the next dollar.

**Planning:** If your business is approaching $500K, consider:
- Accelerating expenses to reduce income below the limit
- Paying bonuses/salary to bring active income under the threshold
- If well above, the cliff doesn't matter — you're paying general rate regardless

### Passive Income Grind
If your CCPC earns more than $50,000 in passive investment income (interest, dividends,
capital gains on investments), the SBD limit is reduced. For every $1 over $50K in passive
income, the SBD limit drops by $5. At $150K passive income, the SBD is eliminated entirely.

**Impact:** If you've accumulated investments inside your corporation, the passive income may
cost you the SBD on your active business income. This is the "grind" — it grinds down your
SBD limit.

**Strategy:** Keep passive investments in personal accounts (TFSA, RRSP) when possible.
If investments must be in the corp, consider a holding company to separate passive from active.

(Source: canada.ca, KPMG)

## 6. Business Expense Deductions

### Fully Deductible Expenses
- **Advertising & marketing:** Website, social media ads, business cards, signage, SEO
- **Professional fees:** Accountant, lawyer, consultant, bookkeeper
- **Office supplies:** Paper, pens, printer ink, postage
- **Software & subscriptions:** Accounting software, CRM, cloud storage, professional tools
- **Insurance:** Business liability, professional liability (E&O), business interruption
- **Interest on business debt:** Loans used to earn business income (fully deductible)
- **Rent:** Office or workspace rent (not home office — see Section 7)
- **Wages & salaries:** Employees, subcontractors (must issue T4s or T4As)
- **Bank charges & merchant fees:** Business account fees, credit card processing
- **Professional development:** Courses, conferences, certifications related to your business
- **Business licenses & permits:** Annual renewals, professional dues
- **Bad debts:** Amounts owed to you that are uncollectable (must have been included in income)

### 50% Deductible
- **Meals & entertainment:** Business meals, client entertainment, team lunches
  - Must be for business purposes (document the business reason)
  - Keep receipts with notes: who, where, business purpose
  - Does NOT include personal meals while working late (that's personal)

### Capital Expenses (CCA — Depreciated Over Time)
- **Equipment:** Computers, printers, machinery → Class 50 (55% for computers) or Class 8 (20%)
- **Furniture:** Desks, chairs, shelving → Class 8 (20%)
- **Vehicles:** → Class 10 (30%) or Class 10.1 for luxury vehicles
- **Leasehold improvements:** → Class 13 (straight-line over lease term)
- **Buildings:** → Class 1 (4%)

**Half-year rule:** In the year you acquire an asset, you can only claim CCA on 50% of the
net addition to the class (unless accelerated CCA applies — verify current rules).

**CCA is optional:** You can claim any amount from $0 to the maximum. Useful in low-income
years (save the deduction for high-income years).

### NOT Deductible
- Personal expenses (groceries, personal clothing, personal travel)
- Mortgage principal payments (interest is deductible, principal is not)
- Capital improvements (add to CCA class, depreciate over time)
- Fines and penalties (CRA, regulatory, parking tickets)
- Club memberships (golf, social clubs — even if used for business meetings)
- Political contributions (separate tax credit, not business deduction)

### Record-Keeping Requirements
- Keep ALL receipts and invoices for **6 years** (CRA audit standard)
- Digital copies acceptable (scan or photograph receipts)
- Vehicle mileage log is **mandatory** if claiming vehicle expenses
- Home office usage records (square footage, hours of use)

(Source: canada.ca/business-expenses, T4002 guide)

## 7. Home Office Deduction

### Eligibility
You can deduct home office expenses if your home office is:
- Your **principal place of business** (you work from home more than 50% of the time), OR
- Used **exclusively** for earning business income AND used on a regular and continuous basis
  for meeting clients/customers

### Calculation Methods

**Method 1: Detailed (Higher deduction, more records)**
Calculate the percentage of your home used for business:
- Square footage of office / total home square footage = business use %
- OR: Number of rooms used / total rooms = business use %

Apply this percentage to eligible expenses:
- Rent (if renting)
- Mortgage **interest** (not principal — if you own)
- Property tax
- Utilities (hydro, gas, water, internet)
- Home insurance
- Maintenance and repairs (proportional)

**Example:** 150 sq ft office in 1,500 sq ft home = 10%
- Rent: $2,000/month × 10% = $200/month = $2,400/year
- Internet: $85/month × 50% (shared use) = $510/year
- Utilities: $300/month × 10% = $360/year
- **Total: $3,270/year deduction**

**Method 2: Flat rate (Simple, no receipts needed)**
- $2/day for each day worked from home, max 250 days = **$500/year**
- This is the CRA "simplified method" — much smaller deduction but no record-keeping.
- **Only use this if your actual expenses are minimal or you don't want to track.**

### Important Warnings
- **Sole proprietors:** Home office deduction CANNOT create a business loss. Can only reduce
  business income to $0 (carry unused portion forward).
- **Incorporated:** The corporation can pay you rent for use of your home office. This is
  taxable to you but may be more tax-efficient depending on your situation. Consult your CPA.
- **CRA audit risk:** Claims over 30-40% of home raise red flags. Keep detailed records.
- **Capital gains impact:** If you claim CCA (depreciation) on the home office portion of
  your home, that portion loses the principal residence exemption. **Most accountants recommend
  NOT claiming CCA on a home office for this reason.**

(Source: canada.ca/home-office, T2125)

## 8. Vehicle Expenses

### Tracking Requirements
CRA requires a **vehicle mileage log** showing:
- Date of each trip
- Destination and purpose (client name, business reason)
- Kilometres driven (start and end odometer readings)
- Total business km / total km for the year = business use %

**Apps like MileIQ, Drivvo, or a simple spreadsheet work.** CRA accepts digital logs.

### Deductible Vehicle Costs (Business % of)
- Fuel
- Insurance
- Maintenance and repairs
- Licence and registration
- Loan interest (capped — see below)
- Lease payments (capped)
- CCA (depreciation on owned vehicle)
- Parking (business-related, 100% deductible — not subject to business %)
- Car washes (business %)

### CRA Limits on Luxury Vehicles (2025 — verify)
- **CCA limit:** Maximum cost for CCA purposes: ~$37,000 + tax (Class 10.1). If vehicle costs
  more, you can only depreciate on the capped amount.
- **Lease limit:** Maximum deductible lease payment: ~$950/month + tax.
- **Interest limit:** Maximum deductible interest on vehicle loan: ~$300/month.

### Employee vs Self-Employed
- **Self-employed:** Deduct actual costs × business %, OR use CRA per-km rate
- **Employee (with T2200):** Deduct actual costs × business %, OR use CRA per-km rate
  ($0.72/km first 5,000 km, $0.66/km after, 2025 — verify)
- **Corporation-owned vehicle:** Corporation deducts all costs. Personal use is a taxable
  benefit (standby charge + operating cost benefit). Complex — consult CPA.

(Source: canada.ca/vehicle-expenses, T4002)

## 9. HST/GST Registration & Input Tax Credits (ITCs)

### $30,000 Registration Threshold
- **Mandatory registration:** If your revenue exceeds $30,000 in any rolling 4 consecutive
  calendar quarters (or single calendar quarter), you MUST register for GST/HST.
- **Voluntary registration:** You CAN register below $30,000 if it benefits you.
- **Exempt supplies:** Some services are exempt from GST/HST (medical, financial, educational).
  If you only provide exempt supplies, you don't register.

### When Voluntary Registration Makes Sense
- You buy significant equipment/supplies with GST/HST → claim ITCs and get money back
- Your clients are businesses who expect invoices with HST (B2B)
- You expect to exceed $30K soon and want to start claiming ITCs now

### When Voluntary Registration Does NOT Make Sense
- All your clients are consumers (B2C) and you're under $30K → HST increases your prices by 5-13%
- Your expenses are low (not much ITC to claim)
- You provide exempt supplies

### How ITCs Work
- You collect HST on sales (13% in Ontario, 15% in Atlantic, 5% GST-only provinces)
- You pay HST on business purchases
- **Net HST = collected - paid (ITCs)**
- If ITCs > collected: you get a **refund** from CRA
- File quarterly (or annually if revenue < $1.5M)

### Quick Method (Simplified Accounting)
- For businesses with revenue < $400K (goods) or < $200K (services)
- Instead of tracking every ITC, you remit a flat percentage of revenue
- Rates vary by province and type of business (typically 3.6-8.8%)
- Often results in LESS HST remitted than the regular method (you keep the difference)
- **Example (Ontario, services):** Collect 13% HST, remit 8.8% under quick method = keep 4.2%
  as a bonus. On $100K revenue = $4,200 extra.

### Common Mistakes
1. **Not registering when hitting $30K** — CRA will back-assess HST plus penalties
2. **Collecting HST but not remitting** — this is a trust issue, CRA treats it seriously
3. **Not claiming all ITCs** — every business purchase with HST is claimable (keep receipts)
4. **Registering too early for B2C businesses** — you're just adding cost for your customers
5. **Missing the quick method** — many small businesses would benefit but don't know about it

(Source: canada.ca/gst-hst, RC4022 guide)

## 10. Quarterly Tax Installments

### Who Must Pay
Required if you owe **more than $3,000** in net tax for the current year AND either of the
two prior years. (Quebec threshold: $1,800 to Revenu Québec.)

### Due Dates
- **March 15, June 15, September 15, December 15**
- These are HARD deadlines — CRA charges compound daily interest on late payments.

### Calculation Methods (CRA allows you to choose)
1. **Prior-year method:** 1/4 of last year's net tax owing × 4 payments. Simplest if income
   is stable.
2. **Current-year estimate:** 1/4 of what you expect to owe this year. Better if income dropped.
3. **CRA-suggested method:** Based on your Notice of Assessment. CRA sends reminders but
   they're not always accurate.

### Interest & Penalties
- CRA charges **compound daily interest** at the prescribed rate (~7-10%, verify quarterly)
  on late or insufficient installments.
- If your installments fall short of what was owed, interest accrues from the installment due
  date, not the filing date.
- **Set up automatic quarterly payments** or calendar reminders — there's no grace period.

### Self-Employed Specific
- Self-employed income is reported on your personal T1 but doesn't have source deductions
  (no employer withholding tax). This means your tax bill at filing time can be large.
- Quarterly installments prevent the year-end shock.
- File by **June 15** (self-employed filing deadline) but taxes owing are still due **April 30.**
  Interest starts April 30, not June 15.

(Source: canada.ca/installments)

## 11. Business Startup Planning

### Runway Calculation
Before launching, calculate how long your savings can sustain you:

```
Personal monthly expenses (rent, food, insurance, debt payments, etc.)
+ Business monthly fixed costs (rent, software, insurance, marketing)
= Total monthly burn rate

Savings available for business / monthly burn rate = months of runway
```

**Rule of thumb:** Have 6-12 months of runway before launching. 12-24 months for businesses
with longer sales cycles (consulting, SaaS, manufacturing).

### Cash Flow vs Profit
- **Profit** (accounting): Revenue - expenses. Can be positive on paper.
- **Cash flow** (reality): Money in - money out. Can be negative even when profitable.
- **Why it matters:** If clients pay in 60 days but you pay suppliers in 30 days, you need
  working capital to bridge the gap. Many profitable businesses fail due to cash flow problems.

### Break-Even Analysis
```
Fixed costs per month (rent, salaries, insurance, software)
÷ Contribution margin per unit (sale price - variable cost per unit)
= Break-even units per month

OR for service businesses:
Fixed costs per month ÷ (hourly rate × billable hours per month) = months to break even
```

### Financing Options
- **Personal savings:** Simplest, no debt. Risk: your savings are at risk.
- **HELOC:** Low interest (~prime + 0.5-2%), secured by home. Risk: your home is collateral.
- **Business Development Bank (BDC):** Government-backed loans for Canadian small businesses.
  Typically higher rates than banks but more flexible approval.
- **Canada Small Business Financing Program (CSBFP):** Government-guaranteed loans up to
  $1.15M for equipment, leasehold improvements, and real property. Banks are more willing to
  lend because the government guarantees 85%.
- **Friends/family:** Document everything. Use a promissory note. Set interest rate and
  repayment schedule. Don't mix friendship with business informally.
- **Credit cards:** Last resort. 20%+ interest makes this the most expensive financing option.

### Pre-Launch Tax Items
- Choose a fiscal year-end (Dec 31 most common for sole proprietors — required by CRA.
  Corporations can choose any date.)
- Register for GST/HST if expected revenue > $30K
- Set up a separate business bank account (not legally required for sole props, but strongly
  recommended for record-keeping)
- Get a business number from CRA (required if incorporating, hiring employees, or registering
  for HST)

(Source: canada.ca/starting-business, BDC.ca)

## 12. Business Succession & Sale

### Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE)
The most valuable tax benefit available to Canadian business owners.

- **Amount:** $1,250,000 per person (2025, indexed for inflation starting 2026 — verify)
- **Applies to:** Sale of Qualified Small Business Corporation (QSBC) shares
- **Tax savings:** At 50% capital gains inclusion and ~33% marginal rate, the LCGE can save
  up to ~$206,000 in tax per person.
- **Per person:** Both spouses can each claim $1,250,000 if they both own shares. Total
  family exemption: $2,500,000.

### QSBC Share Requirements (All Must Be Met)
1. Corporation is a **Canadian-Controlled Private Corporation (CCPC)**
2. At time of sale: **90%+ of fair market value** of assets are used in active business
   (not passive investments)
3. During the 24 months before sale: shares were held by the seller (or related person) AND
   50%+ of assets were used in active business
4. Shares were not held by a non-resident or public corporation at any time

### Share Sale vs Asset Sale

| Factor | Share Sale | Asset Sale |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| LCGE available? | YES | NO |
| Tax to seller | Capital gains (50% inclusion) | Depends on asset type |
| Buyer inherits liabilities? | YES (all) | NO (only chosen) |
| Simplicity | Simpler for seller | Simpler for buyer |
| Buyer preference | Usually dislikes (hidden risks) | Usually prefers (clean start) |
| Double taxation? | NO | YES (corporate tax on asset gain + personal tax on distribution) |

**Negotiation:** Sellers prefer share sale (LCGE + simplicity). Buyers prefer asset sale
(control + clean start). Often resolved with a price adjustment.

### Estate Freeze
If you want to pass the business to the next generation while locking in your current value:

1. You exchange your common shares for **preferred shares** with a fixed value (= current FMV)
2. Your children (or a trust for them) subscribe for new **common shares** at nominal value
3. All future growth accrues to the common shares (children's shares)
4. Your preferred shares can be redeemed over time (you get your money out gradually)

**Result:** You lock in today's value for your estate planning. Future growth is in the next
generation's hands, potentially multiplying the LCGE ($1.25M per child).

**Requires:** Lawyer + accountant. Cost: $5,000-$15,000.

### Buy-Sell Agreements
If you have a business partner, document what happens if one of you:
- Wants to leave the business
- Dies
- Becomes disabled
- Gets divorced (spouse claims business interest)

**Funded by insurance:** Each partner owns a life insurance policy on the other. Death triggers
the buyout using insurance proceeds. Cost: depends on partner ages and coverage amount.

(Source: canada.ca/LCGE, taxtips.ca, BDO corporate tax planning)

## 13. Employee vs Contractor Classification

### CRA's Classification Factors
CRA uses a multi-factor test. No single factor is decisive — they look at the overall relationship.

| Factor | Employee | Contractor |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| Control | Payer controls what, how, when | Worker controls their own method |
| Tools & equipment | Payer provides | Worker provides their own |
| Financial risk | Payer bears profit/loss risk | Worker can profit or lose |
| Integration | Work is integral to payer's business | Worker has own business identity |
| Multiple clients | Works only for one payer | Has multiple clients |
| Invoicing | Receives T4 (payroll) | Sends invoices, receives T4A |

### Misclassification Penalties
If CRA determines a "contractor" is actually an employee:
- **Employer liable for:** Unpaid CPP (employer + employee portions), EI premiums, income tax
  that should have been withheld
- **Penalties:** Up to 10% of the unpaid amounts + interest
- **Example:** 3 "contractors" at $50K each for 2 years = ~$30K-$50K in back-assessed CPP/EI
  + $5K-$10K penalties + interest

### Protecting Yourself
**As the hiring business:**
- Use a written contract specifying scope, deliverables, payment terms
- Contractor should invoice you (not be on payroll)
- Contractor should have multiple clients (not just you)
- Don't control their schedule or methods
- Don't provide tools/equipment
- Contract should have a termination clause (not an employment-style dismissal)

**As the contractor:**
- Have a business number, HST registration (if applicable), and business insurance
- Issue invoices (not timesheets)
- Have multiple clients
- Provide your own tools
- Deduct your own business expenses

(Source: canada.ca/employee-or-self-employed, RC4110)

## 14. SRED Tax Credits

### What Is SRED?
Scientific Research and Experimental Development — a federal + provincial tax incentive for
Canadian businesses doing R&D. One of the most generous R&D incentive programs in the world.

### Federal Credit Rates (2025 — verify, recent changes)
- **CCPCs (enhanced rate):** 35% refundable credit on first $4.5M of qualified expenditures
  (increasing to $6M — verify). Expenditures above the limit: 15%.
- **Other corporations:** 15% non-refundable credit.
- **Maximum refundable credit (CCPC):** Up to ~$2.1M annually at the 35% rate.

### Provincial Credits (Selected — verify)
| Province | Rate | Refundable? |
|----------|------|------------|
| Ontario (OITC) | 8% | Yes |
| Ontario (ORDTC) | 3.5% | No |
| Quebec | 14-30% | Yes (varies by size) |
| BC | 10% | Yes (for CCPCs) |
| Alberta | 10% | Yes |

**Combined federal + provincial:** 43-65% effective credit rate for qualifying CCPCs.

### Eligible Activities
- Developing new software (novel algorithms, new architectures — NOT standard coding)
- Designing new products or processes
- Experimental development to resolve technological uncertainty
- Testing hypotheses through systematic experimentation

### NOT Eligible
- Routine software development, maintenance, or updates
- Market research, sales, or marketing
- Quality control or testing (unless part of an experiment)
- Social science or humanities research
- Prospecting or exploring for minerals

### Documentation (Critical)
CRA denies many SRED claims due to poor documentation. You need:
- Project descriptions (what technological uncertainty were you resolving?)
- Timeline of work (dates, hours spent by each person)
- Records of hypotheses tested and results (including failures — these strengthen claims)
- Salary records for employees who worked on SRED projects
- Materials and supplies used for R&D

### Professional Help
Many firms specialize in SRED claims. They typically charge 15-25% of the credit obtained
(contingency fee). Worth it if you're unfamiliar with the program — a well-prepared claim
can be worth $50K-$500K+ per year for tech companies.

(Source: canada.ca/sred, thinkaccounting.ca)

## 15. IPP (Individual Pension Plan)

### What Is an IPP?
A defined benefit pension plan set up by an incorporated business owner for themselves.
Allows higher tax-deductible contributions than an RRSP, especially for people 40+.

### When It Makes Sense
- **Age 40+** (contributions increase with age — the older you are, the bigger the advantage)
- **Incorporated** with stable T4 income of $100K+
- **Already maxing RRSP** and want to shelter more income
- **Plan to stay incorporated 10+ years** (need time to recoup setup costs)

### Contribution Comparison (2025 — verify)
| Age | Max RRSP | Approx IPP Contribution | IPP Advantage |
|-----|---------|------------------------|---------------|
| 40 | $32,490 | ~$28,000 | None (RRSP is better at this age) |
| 45 | $32,490 | ~$33,000 | ~$500/year |
| 50 | $32,490 | ~$41,000 | ~$8,500/year |
| 55 | $32,490 | ~$51,000 | ~$18,500/year |
| 60 | $32,490 | ~$65,000 | ~$32,500/year |

**The advantage grows significantly with age.** At 55+, the IPP allows nearly double the RRSP.

### Costs
- **Setup:** $2,500-$3,500 (actuarial design)
- **Annual administration:** $800-$1,200
- **Actuarial valuations:** $2,000-$3,000 every 3 years
- **Total annual cost:** ~$1,500-$2,200/year

### Payback Analysis
At age 50, extra $8,500/year contribution × 43% marginal rate = ~$3,655/year tax savings.
Costs ~$1,800/year. **Net benefit: ~$1,855/year** + tax-deferred investment growth.
Payback period: ~1 year. Over 15 years to age 65: ~$27,000+ in extra tax savings.

### Restrictions
- Must have T4 income (salary, not dividends) to fund the IPP
- Contributions are a corporate deduction (corporation pays into the plan)
- Benefits are locked-in at retirement (can't access early like RRSP — similar to LIRA)
- Past service contributions possible (can fund for years of service before the IPP existed)
- Spousal benefits limited compared to RRSP flexibility

(Source: canada.ca, taxtips.ca/ipp)

## 16. Shareholder Loans & Year-End Planning

### Shareholder Loans (Section 15(1))
If you borrow money from your corporation, CRA may deem it a taxable dividend.

**The rule:** If a shareholder loan is NOT repaid within **one full taxation year** after the
year it was advanced, the full amount is included in your income as a deemed dividend.

**Example:**
- Corp fiscal year: January 1 – December 31
- You borrow $50,000 on March 15, 2026
- Must repay by December 31, 2027 (one full year after the end of the year the loan was made)
- If not repaid: $50,000 is deemed a dividend on your 2026 personal tax return

**Proper documentation:** Promissory note, reasonable interest rate (at least CRA prescribed
rate), repayment schedule, both parties sign.

**Legitimate uses:** Short-term bridge loans between salary/dividend payments. Always repay
within the deadline.

### Year-End Planning Strategies

**Bonus Declaration (Tax Deferral):**
- Declare a bonus on December 31 (corporate deduction for year X)
- Pay the bonus in January-February (personal income for year X, within 180 days)
- Corporation gets the deduction in year X, you report income in year X
- **Must be paid within 180 days of corporate year-end** or the deduction is denied

**Dividend Timing:**
- Declare and pay dividends before corporate year-end to extract surplus cash
- Or declare in December, pay in January — personal income shifts to the following year

**RRSP Deadline Coordination:**
- Personal RRSP deadline: 60 days after calendar year-end (March 1 or March 2)
- If paying yourself a bonus in January, contribute the RRSP amount before the March deadline
  to offset the personal tax on the bonus

**Passive Income Check:**
- If corporate passive income is approaching $50,000, consider extracting investments as
  dividends before year-end to avoid the SBD grind next year

(Source: canada.ca, ITA section 15(1), ITA section 78)

## 17. Gig Economy & Freelancer Issues

### Income Volatility Management
- Budget based on your **lowest-earning month**, not your average
- Emergency fund: **6-12 months** of personal expenses (higher than T4 employees who need 3-6)
- Track income monthly — if 3 months below average, adjust spending
- Consider a "paycheck" system: transfer a fixed amount to personal account monthly, let
  business account absorb the peaks and troughs

### No Employer Benefits
Gig workers have NO employer-provided:
- Health/dental insurance → buy individual plan (Blue Cross, Green Shield, professional
  association group plans)
- Life insurance → buy personal term life if dependents
- Disability insurance → **CRITICAL** — buy individual own-occupation disability. You have
  zero income replacement if you can't work.
- Retirement savings → no employer match, no pension. Self-directed RRSP/TFSA only.
- EI → self-employed can opt into EI Special Benefits (maternity, parental, sickness, caregiver)
  for ~$1,000/year premium (2025 — verify). Regular EI (job loss) is NOT available.

### CPP for Self-Employed
- Self-employed pay **BOTH** the employee AND employer portions of CPP
- Total: ~11.9% on net self-employment income between $3,500 and $71,300 (2025 — verify)
- Maximum annual CPP contribution: ~$8,068 (both portions combined, 2025 — verify)
- This is a significant cost — but it builds retirement pension credits
- Claimed as a deduction (50% on personal return)

### Multiple Income Sources
Common for gig workers: Uber + Airbnb + freelance design + part-time employment
- Track EACH source separately
- Employment income: T4 issued by employer (tax already withheld)
- Self-employment income: reported on T2125 (no tax withheld — quarterly installments needed)
- Rental income: reported on T776
- All flow into your personal T1 return

### CRA Scrutiny
Gig workers face **higher audit risk** because:
- Income reporting relies on self-reporting (no employer T4)
- Platforms (Uber, Airbnb, DoorDash) now report earnings to CRA
- Home office and vehicle deductions are commonly over-claimed
- **Protect yourself:** Keep meticulous records, use accounting software (Wave, QuickBooks,
  FreshBooks), separate business and personal banking

(Source: canada.ca/self-employed, T2125)

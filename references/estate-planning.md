## 2. Why Estate Planning Matters

- **52% of Canadian adults don't have a will** (2025 — verify). Without one, the province
  decides who gets your assets, who raises your children, and who manages your estate.
- A will costs $500-$1,500 with a lawyer. Court-appointed administration without a will
  costs $5,000-$15,000+ and takes months longer.
- Powers of Attorney are arguably MORE important than a will — they cover what happens if
  you're alive but can't make decisions (stroke, dementia, accident).
- **Estate planning is not just for wealthy people.** Anyone with a home, registered accounts,
  children, or a spouse needs basic estate documents.

## 3. Wills: Types, Costs & What Happens Without One

### Types of Wills in Canada

| Type | How It Works | Cost | Pros | Cons |
|------|-------------|------|------|------|
| **Formal will** (lawyer-drafted) | Prepared by a lawyer, signed with 2 witnesses | $500-$1,500 (simple), $2,000-$5,000 (complex) | Legally robust, customized, professional advice | Most expensive |
| **Holographic will** (handwritten) | Entirely in your handwriting, signed, no witnesses needed | Free | Free, can be done immediately | Easy to challenge, may miss critical provisions, not valid in all provinces (not valid in BC) |
| **Online will** (platforms like Willful, Epilogue) | Guided online process, printed and signed with witnesses | $100-$300 | Affordable, convenient, better than nothing | Limited customization, no legal advice, may not cover complex situations |
| **Notarial will** (Quebec only) | Prepared and signed before a notary | $800-$2,000 | No probate required, strongest legal standing, self-proving | Quebec only, more expensive |

### When to Use Each
- **Simple situation** (married, kids, straightforward assets): Online will or simple lawyer will
- **Complex situation** (blended family, business, multiple properties, trusts): Lawyer-drafted
- **Quebec:** Always use a notarial will — it avoids probate entirely ($0 probate fees)
- **Emergency** (no will at all, need something NOW): Holographic will as a stopgap until
  you see a lawyer. Write it entirely by hand, sign it, date it.

### What Should Be in Your Will
- **Executor appointment** (who manages the estate)
- **Alternate executor** (if first choice can't or won't serve)
- **Asset distribution** (who gets what — be specific)
- **Guardianship** for minor children (see Section 18)
- **Trust provisions** for minor beneficiaries (children don't inherit directly — a trustee
  manages until they reach a specified age)
- **Funeral wishes** (burial vs cremation, preferences)
- **Specific bequests** (particular items to particular people)
- **Residual clause** (everything not specifically bequeathed)
- **Digital assets** clause (see Section 16)

### When to Update Your Will
Update after ANY of these life events:
- Marriage (in some provinces, marriage automatically revokes a prior will)
- Divorce or separation
- Birth or adoption of a child
- Death of a named beneficiary or executor
- Significant change in assets (bought/sold property, inheritance, business)
- Moving to a different province (estate law varies)
- Change in wishes about distribution

(Source: provincial Succession Law Reform Acts, lawdepot.ca)

## 4. Intestacy Rules by Province (No Will)

If you die without a will, your province's intestacy rules determine who inherits.
**Common-law partners are NOT automatically included in most provinces.**

### Ontario (Succession Law Reform Act, updated 2025)
- **Spouse only, no children:** Spouse inherits everything
- **Spouse + 1 child:** Spouse gets preferential share ($350,000) + 50% of remainder.
  Child gets 50% of remainder.
- **Spouse + 2+ children:** Spouse gets $350,000 + 1/3 of remainder. Children share 2/3.
- **No spouse:** Children inherit equally. If no children: parents. If no parents: siblings.
- **Common-law partners:** NO automatic inheritance rights in Ontario. Must make a
  dependant's relief claim or have a cohabitation agreement.

### British Columbia (WESA)
- **Spouse only, no children:** Spouse inherits everything
- **Spouse + children (all shared):** Spouse gets $300,000 + household furnishings + 50% of remainder
- **Spouse + children from prior relationship:** Spouse gets $150,000 + household furnishings + 50% of remainder
- **Common-law partners (2+ years):** Same rights as married spouses in BC

### Alberta (Wills and Succession Act)
- **Spouse, no children:** Spouse inherits everything
- **Spouse + children (all shared):** Spouse gets $150,000 + 50% of remainder (verify — recently changed)
- **Adult interdependent partner:** Same rights as married spouse

### Quebec (Civil Code)
- **Married spouse + children:** Spouse gets 1/3, children share 2/3
- **Married spouse, no children:** Spouse gets 2/3, parents get 1/3
- **Common-law partners (de facto spouses):** No automatic rights UNLESS in a "parental
  union" (new as of June 30, 2025 — verify)
- **Notarial will avoids probate entirely** — this is a major Quebec advantage

### Key Takeaway
**Common-law partners are at serious risk without a will** in most provinces (except BC and
some others that have expanded rights). A couple that's been together 20 years, owns a home
together, and raises children can see the surviving partner inherit NOTHING if there's no will.

(Source: provincial succession legislation, ig.ca, lawdepot.ca)

## 5. Powers of Attorney (Property + Personal Care)

### Two Types (Both Are Essential)

| Type | What It Covers | Who Decides | Terminology |
|------|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| **POA for Property** | Financial decisions: banking, investments, real estate, tax | Your chosen attorney | "Mandate for property" in Quebec |
| **POA for Personal Care** | Health and living decisions: medical treatment, housing, care | Your chosen attorney | "Mandate for person" in Quebec; "Representation Agreement" in BC |

### Provincial Terminology
| Province | Property | Personal Care |
|----------|---------|--------------|
| Ontario | Power of Attorney for Property | Power of Attorney for Personal Care |
| BC | Enduring Power of Attorney | Representation Agreement |
| Alberta | Enduring Power of Attorney | Personal Directive |
| Quebec | Protection mandate (mandat de protection) | Same document covers both |
| Other provinces | Enduring Power of Attorney | Health Care Directive / Advance Directive |

### When to Create POAs
- **NOW.** You must create POAs while you have mental capacity. Once you lose capacity
  (stroke, dementia, accident), it's too late — your family must apply to the court for
  guardianship/trusteeship ($5,000-$15,000+, takes months).
- Every adult 18+ should have both POAs, regardless of age or wealth.
- Couples should each have their own POAs (naming each other, with alternates).

### Choosing Your Attorney
- Must be someone you trust completely with your finances and health decisions
- Doesn't have to be the same person for both types
- Can be a family member, trusted friend, or professional (lawyer, trust company)
- Name an alternate in case your first choice can't serve
- Consider: will they follow your wishes? Can they handle the responsibility? Are they
  geographically accessible?

### Immediate vs Springing
- **Immediate:** Takes effect as soon as it's signed. Your attorney can act now (useful if
  you travel frequently or want someone to manage finances while you're away).
- **Springing:** Only takes effect when you become incapacitated (requires a medical
  determination of incapacity). More common for younger people.

### Cost
- DIY (online templates): $50-$200
- Lawyer-drafted (recommended): $300-$800 for both POAs
- Usually done at the same time as your will: $800-$1,500 for will + both POAs

(Source: provincial legislation, canada.ca)

## 6. Incapacity Planning (Distinct from Death)

### What If You're Alive But Can't Make Decisions?
This is different from estate planning (which covers what happens after death). Incapacity
planning covers the period when you're alive but unable to manage your own affairs.

**Common causes:** Stroke, dementia/Alzheimer's, traumatic brain injury, severe mental
illness, extended coma, advanced age with cognitive decline.

### The Documents You Need
1. **Power of Attorney for Property** — who manages your money, pays your bills, handles
   your investments, files your taxes
2. **Power of Attorney for Personal Care** — who makes medical decisions, chooses your care
   facility, consents to treatment
3. **Advance Directive / Living Will** — your specific wishes about medical treatment:
   - Do you want life support? Under what conditions?
   - Do you want CPR if your heart stops?
   - Do you want tube feeding?
   - Organ donation preferences
   - Pain management preferences (comfort care vs aggressive treatment)

### What Happens WITHOUT POAs
If you become incapacitated with no POAs:
- **No one can access your bank accounts** (even your spouse, unless joint)
- **No one can sell your home** (even if needed to fund care)
- **No one can make medical decisions** for you beyond emergency care
- Your family must apply to the court for **guardianship/trusteeship:**
  - Cost: $5,000-$15,000+ in legal fees
  - Timeline: 3-6 months minimum
  - Court decides who manages your affairs (may not be who you'd choose)
  - Ongoing court oversight and reporting requirements
  - **This is completely avoidable** with $300-$800 in POA documents

### Long-Term Incapacity Considerations
- **Financial:** Who pays for care? Long-term care costs $2,000-$15,000/month. Your POA
  attorney needs authority to access investments, sell property, manage income.
- **Medical:** Your personal care attorney needs to know your wishes about aggressive
  treatment, palliative care, DNR orders.
- **Family dynamics:** Incapacity can last years (dementia averages 8-10 years). This is
  emotionally and financially draining on caregivers. Plan for it.

(Source: provincial legislation, alzheimer.ca)

## 7. Executor: Role, Duties, Compensation & Liability

### What an Executor Actually Does
Being named executor is a significant responsibility — typically 6-18 months of work.

**Immediate tasks (first 2 weeks):**
- Locate the will and review it
- Arrange the funeral/memorial
- Secure the deceased's property (change locks if needed, notify insurance)
- Obtain multiple death certificates (10+ copies)
- Notify banks, employers, government agencies, insurance companies

**Administrative tasks (months 1-6):**
- Apply for probate (if required — see Section 8)
- Identify and inventory ALL assets (bank accounts, investments, real estate, vehicles,
  personal property, digital assets)
- Identify ALL debts and liabilities
- Open an estate bank account
- Notify creditors (publish advertisement for creditors — typically required for 4-6 weeks)
- File CPP death benefit application ($2,500)
- Cancel CPP/OAS/GIS and other government benefits
- File the deceased's final tax return

**Distribution tasks (months 6-18):**
- Pay all debts, taxes, and estate expenses
- Obtain tax clearance certificate from CRA (confirms all taxes are paid)
- Distribute assets according to the will
- Prepare estate accounting (detailed record of all transactions)
- Obtain releases from beneficiaries (protects executor from future claims)

### Executor Compensation
Executors are entitled to compensation for their work. Rates vary by province:

| Province | Guideline | On $500K Estate |
|----------|----------|----------------|
| Ontario | Up to 5% of estate value (Trustee Act) | Up to $25,000 |
| BC | Up to 5% of estate value (Trustee Act) | Up to $25,000 |
| Alberta | "Fair and reasonable" (Surrogate Rules) | Typically 3-5% |
| Quebec | No compensation unless specified in will | $0 (unless will says otherwise) |
| Saskatchewan | Up to 5% | Up to $25,000 |
| Manitoba | "Fair and reasonable" | Typically 2.5-5% |

**Note:** Compensation may be set in the will (overrides provincial guidelines). Professional
executors (trust companies) typically charge 3-5% + annual fees.

### Executor Liability
Executors can be **personally liable** for:
- Distributing assets before paying all debts (creditors can come after the executor)
- Not filing tax returns (CRA can assess the executor for unpaid taxes)
- Mismanaging estate investments (beneficiaries can sue)
- Not following the will's instructions
- Distributing to the wrong people

**Protection:** Get a tax clearance certificate from CRA before distributing. Get signed
releases from all beneficiaries. Keep detailed records of every transaction. When in doubt,
consult an estate lawyer.

### When to Decline Being Executor
- The estate is complex and you're not comfortable managing it
- There's family conflict that will make distribution contentious
- You live far from the deceased and can't manage local affairs
- Consider hiring a professional executor (estate lawyer or trust company): $5,000-$25,000+
  depending on estate complexity

(Source: provincial Trustee Acts, clearestate.com, moneysense.ca)

## 8. Probate: What It Is & How It Works

### What Is Probate?
Probate is the court process that:
1. **Confirms the will is valid** (properly signed, not revoked, the deceased had capacity)
2. **Grants the executor legal authority** to act on behalf of the estate
3. **Provides a court-stamped certificate** that banks, land registries, and other institutions
   require before releasing assets

### When Is Probate Required?
- **Assets in the deceased's sole name** (bank accounts over ~$10,000-$50,000 depending on
  institution, real estate, investments)
- **Financial institutions request it** — most banks won't release more than $10,000-$75,000
  without a probate certificate (threshold varies by institution)
- **Real estate transfer** — the land registry office requires probate to transfer title

### When Is Probate NOT Required?
- **Joint ownership with right of survivorship** — asset passes automatically to survivor
- **Beneficiary designations** — RRSP, RRIF, TFSA, FHSA, life insurance go directly to
  named beneficiary (bypass estate entirely)
- **Small estates** — some provinces have simplified processes for estates under $25,000-$50,000
- **Quebec notarial wills** — no probate required ($0 probate fee — this is a major advantage)

### Timeline
| Step | Duration | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Gather documents & file application | 2-4 weeks | Will, death certificate, asset inventory |
| Court processing | 4-12 weeks | Varies enormously by province and court backlog |
| Creditor notification period | 4-6 weeks | Must advertise and wait before distributing |
| CRA tax clearance | 3-6 months | Final return must be filed and assessed |
| **Total typical timeline** | **6-18 months** | Complex estates can take 2+ years |

(Source: provincial probate courts, taxtips.ca)

## 9. Probate Fees by Province

Probate fees (called "estate administration tax" in Ontario) are calculated on the **total
value of assets that pass through the estate** (not joint, not beneficiary-designated).

| Province | Fee Structure | On $500K Estate | On $1M Estate |
|----------|-------------|----------------|---------------|
| Ontario | 0.5% on first $50K + 1.5% on remainder | $7,000 | $14,500 |
| BC | 0.6% on first $25K + 1.4% on remainder | $6,800 | $13,750 |
| Alberta | Flat fee schedule, max $525 | $525 | $525 |
| Saskatchewan | 0.7% flat rate | $3,500 | $7,000 |
| Manitoba | $70 flat fee | $70 | $70 |
| Quebec (notarial will) | $0 | $0 | $0 |
| Quebec (non-notarial) | ~$1,000-$1,500 | ~$1,000 | ~$1,000 |
| Nova Scotia | $1,003 + 1.695% over $100K | $7,793 | $16,253 |
| New Brunswick | $5 per $1,000 (0.5%) | $2,500 | $5,000 |
| PEI | $400 flat | $400 | $400 |
| Newfoundland | $385 + 0.6% over $1,000 | $3,379 | $6,379 |
| Yukon | $140 flat | $140 | $140 |
| NWT | $200-$400 | ~$300 | ~$400 |
| Nunavut | Minimal | ~$200 | ~$200 |

**(2025 — verify all. Provinces update fees periodically.)**

**Ontario and BC are the most expensive** — a $1M estate pays $14,500+ in Ontario.
**Alberta, Manitoba, PEI, and territories have minimal fees.**
**Quebec notarial wills avoid probate entirely.**

(Source: taxtips.ca/probate, provincial court websites)

## 10. Probate Avoidance Strategies

### Why Avoid Probate?
- **Save probate fees** (up to 1.5% in Ontario, 1.4% in BC)
- **Faster distribution** (no court processing delay)
- **Privacy** (probate is a public process — anyone can see the will and asset values)

### Strategy 1: Beneficiary Designations
Name beneficiaries on all accounts that allow it:

| Account | Bypass Probate? | Designation |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| RRSP/RRIF | YES | Name beneficiary or successor annuitant (spouse) |
| TFSA | YES | Name successor holder (spouse) or beneficiary |
| FHSA | YES | Name beneficiary |
| Life insurance | YES | Name beneficiary (not "estate") |
| RESP | NO | Goes through estate (no beneficiary designation) |
| RDSP | NO | Goes through estate |
| Non-registered investments | NO | Goes through estate (unless joint) |
| Real estate | NO | Goes through estate (unless joint) |
| Bank accounts | MAYBE | Some allow beneficiary designation or "in trust for" |

**Critical mistake:** Naming "my estate" as beneficiary on insurance or RRSP. This pulls the
asset INTO the estate, subjects it to probate fees, and makes it accessible to creditors.

### Strategy 2: Joint Ownership
- **Joint tenancy with right of survivorship:** Asset passes automatically to the surviving
  joint owner on death. No probate.
- **Works for:** Bank accounts, real estate, investment accounts
- **Risks:**
  - Joint owner has immediate access (can withdraw/spend)
  - Joint owner's creditors can potentially claim the asset
  - Adding a child as joint owner may trigger capital gains tax (deemed disposition of 50%)
  - Unequal treatment of heirs (joint owner gets the asset, other children get nothing)
  - CRA may view the addition as a gift or sale
- **Best practice:** Use joint ownership only between spouses. For children, use beneficiary
  designations or trusts instead.

### Strategy 3: Inter Vivos (Living) Trust
- Transfer assets into a trust during your lifetime
- Trust assets bypass probate entirely (they're owned by the trust, not you)
- Privacy maintained (trust isn't a public document)
- Cost: $1,500-$5,000 to set up + ongoing administration
- **Best for:** Large estates in high-probate-fee provinces (Ontario, BC)
- See Section 11 for trust types

### Strategy 4: Gifts During Lifetime
- Give assets away while alive → they're no longer in your estate
- **Warning:** Gifts of capital property trigger deemed disposition (capital gains tax NOW)
- **Exception:** Cash gifts have no tax implications for the giver or receiver in Canada
- **Warning:** Don't gift away assets you might need for care costs in old age

### Strategy 5: Multiple Wills (Ontario)
- Ontario allows a "primary will" (for assets requiring probate) and a "secondary will"
  (for assets that don't require probate, like private company shares)
- Probate fees are only paid on the primary will's assets
- Complex — requires a lawyer experienced in estate planning
- **Only useful in Ontario** (and possibly BC — verify)

(Source: taxtips.ca/probate, estate planning lawyers)

## 11. Trusts: Types, Costs & When to Use

### Testamentary Trust (Created in Your Will)
- Takes effect at your death
- Used to manage assets for minor children, spendthrift beneficiaries, or disabled persons
- Trustee manages the assets and distributes according to the will's instructions
- **Common use:** "My children receive their share at age 25" — trustee holds and invests
  until then
- Cost: Included in will preparation ($0 extra if simple)
- **Tax advantage:** Testamentary trusts are taxed at graduated rates (like an individual),
  not flat top rate. This changed in 2016 — only "Graduated Rate Estates" (GREs) and
  "Qualified Disability Trusts" get this benefit.

### Inter Vivos Trust (Living Trust)
- Created during your lifetime, takes effect immediately
- You transfer assets into the trust (they're no longer "yours" for probate purposes)
- **Probate avoidance:** Trust assets don't go through your estate
- **Privacy:** Trust documents are private (unlike a probated will)
- Cost: $1,500-$5,000 to establish + $500-$2,000/year administration
- Tax: Taxed at the top marginal rate on all income (no graduated rates)
- **Best for:** High-value estates in Ontario/BC where probate fees are significant

### Henson Trust (Disabled Beneficiaries)
- **Purpose:** Provide for a disabled person WITHOUT disqualifying them from government
  benefits (ODSP, AISH, BC PWD)
- **How it works:** Trustee has "absolute discretion" over distributions — the beneficiary
  has no legal right to demand payment. Because the beneficiary doesn't "own" the trust
  assets, they remain eligible for provincial disability programs.
- **ODSP (Ontario) rules:**
  - Henson trust assets are exempt from ODSP asset limits ($40,000)
  - Distributions up to $10,000/year are exempt from ODSP income calculations
  - Purchases of primary residence or vehicle from trust are exempt
- **AISH (Alberta):** Similar principles apply — discretionary trusts generally don't affect
  AISH eligibility ($100,000 asset limit)
- Cost: $2,000-$5,000 to establish (within will or standalone)
- **Critical:** The trust must be drafted by a lawyer experienced in disability trusts.
  Incorrect wording can disqualify the beneficiary from benefits.

(Source: ontario.ca/odsp, specialneedsplanning.ca)

### Family Trust
- Holds assets for the benefit of family members
- Used for income splitting (within TOSI rules — limited since 2018)
- Asset protection from creditors
- Gradual wealth transfer to next generation
- 21-year deemed disposition rule: every 21 years, trust assets are deemed sold at FMV
  (significant tax event — plan for it)
- Cost: $3,000-$10,000 to establish + $1,500-$3,000/year administration
- **Best for:** Wealthy families with complex asset structures

### Alter Ego Trust (65+)
- Creator must be 65+ at time of creation
- Creator retains full control and use of assets during lifetime
- At death, assets pass to named beneficiaries WITHOUT probate
- Taxed at graduated rates during creator's lifetime
- Cost: $2,000-$5,000 to establish
- **Best for:** Seniors in high-probate-fee provinces who want probate avoidance + control

### Joint Partner Trust (65+)
- Similar to alter ego but for couples (both 65+)
- Both partners retain control and use during both lifetimes
- At death of last survivor, assets distributed to beneficiaries without probate
- Cost: $2,000-$5,000 to establish
- **Best for:** Couples 65+ who want probate avoidance for both spouses

(Source: canada.ca/trusts, taxtips.ca)

## 12. Deemed Disposition Tax at Death

### The Rule
CRA treats all your assets as if you **sold them at fair market value** on the date of death.
This triggers capital gains tax on any appreciation.

### What's Taxed (and What Isn't)

| Asset | Tax at Death | Notes |
|-------|-------------|-------|
| RRSP/RRIF | 100% taxable as income | UNLESS rolled to surviving spouse (tax-free) |
| Non-registered investments | 50% of capital gain taxable | Gain = FMV at death - ACB |
| Primary residence | Exempt (PRE) | No tax on principal residence |
| Cottage/vacation property | 50% of capital gain taxable | Unless designated as PRE for some years |
| Business shares (QSBC) | 50% taxable, but LCGE may apply | Up to $1.25M exemption per person |
| TFSA | Not taxable | Successor holder (spouse) preserves tax-free status |
| Life insurance proceeds | Not taxable | Tax-free to named beneficiary |
| Rental property | Capital gain + CCA recapture | CCA recapture is 100% taxable |

### Example: Estate Tax Bill
**Deceased owns:**
- RRIF: $400,000 (no surviving spouse) → 100% taxable = $400K income
- Non-registered portfolio: $200,000 (ACB was $120,000) → gain $80K, 50% = $40K taxable
- Cottage: $600,000 (ACB was $200,000) → gain $400K, 50% = $200K taxable
- Primary residence: $700,000 → $0 tax (PRE)

**Total taxable income on final return:** ~$640,000
**Estimated tax:** ~$250,000-$300,000 (at top marginal rates)

**This tax bill is due within 6 months of death** (or April 30 following year, whichever
is later). If the estate doesn't have liquid assets, the executor may need to sell assets
to pay the tax.

### Spousal Rollover
If assets pass to a **surviving spouse** (married or common-law):
- RRSP/RRIF: Rolls to spouse's RRSP/RRIF at ACB (no immediate tax)
- Investments: Transfer at ACB (no capital gain triggered)
- Real estate: Transfer at ACB (no capital gain)
- **Tax is DEFERRED until the surviving spouse's death** (or disposition)

This is the most powerful estate tax tool — name your spouse as beneficiary on everything.

### Rights or Things Return (Optional)
A second tax return can be filed for certain income items (dividends declared but not received,
vacation pay owing, etc.). This uses a second set of personal credits, potentially reducing
overall tax. Consult a CPA.

(Source: canada.ca/deemed-disposition, taxtips.ca)

## 13. Estate Tax Planning Strategies

### Strategy 1: Life Insurance to Pay the Tax Bill
- Estimate the deemed disposition tax at death (see Section 12)
- Purchase a term-to-100 or whole life policy for that amount
- Death benefit provides liquidity to pay CRA without selling assets
- **Example:** $250K estimated tax → $250K permanent life insurance. Premium depends on age
  and health. At 65: ~$300-$500/month. Expensive but ensures heirs keep the assets.

### Strategy 2: Estate Freeze
- Lock in the current value of a growing asset (e.g., business shares)
- Exchange common shares for preferred shares (fixed value = current FMV)
- New common shares issued to children/trust (future growth = theirs)
- **Result:** Your estate tax is capped at today's value. Children benefit from future growth.
- Cost: $5,000-$15,000 (lawyer + accountant)
- **Best for:** Business owners expecting significant growth

### Strategy 3: Gradual Wealth Transfer
- Gift assets during your lifetime to reduce estate size
- Cash gifts: No tax implications in Canada (no gift tax)
- Property/investment gifts: Trigger deemed disposition NOW (capital gains tax)
- **Strategy:** Gift in low-income years when your marginal rate is lower
- **Warning:** Don't give away assets you'll need for your own retirement/care

### Strategy 4: Spousal Rollover + Second-Death Planning
- All assets roll to spouse tax-free at first death
- Plan for the tax hit at SECOND death (when there's no more rollover)
- Purchase "second-to-die" life insurance (pays out at second death — cheaper than two
  individual policies because it only pays once)

### Strategy 5: Charitable Donations at Death
- Donate appreciated securities to a registered charity in your will
- **Double benefit:** No capital gains tax on the donated securities + estate gets a donation
  tax credit (up to 100% of net income on final return, vs 75% normally)
- Can eliminate most or all of the estate's tax bill for generous donors

(Source: taxtips.ca/estate, FP Canada)

## 14. Blended Family Estate Planning

### The Challenge
Children from a previous relationship + current spouse creates competing claims:
- Spouse needs ongoing income/housing
- Children from first marriage want their "share"
- Without clear planning, one side is often left out

### Common Approaches

**Approach 1: Life interest for spouse, remainder to children**
- Spouse gets the right to live in the home and receive income during their lifetime
- At spouse's death, remaining assets pass to children from first marriage
- **Requires a testamentary trust** with clear instructions

**Approach 2: Specific bequests**
- Spouse receives specific assets (home, portion of investments)
- Children receive specific assets (insurance proceeds, RRSP, other investments)
- Cleaner but requires careful balancing

**Approach 3: Insurance to equalize**
- Leave the home and investments to the spouse
- Purchase life insurance with children as beneficiaries to "equalize"
- Insurance proceeds are tax-free and bypass the estate

### Critical Actions
1. **Update your will** to explicitly address the blended family situation
2. **Update beneficiary designations** — ex-spouse may still be named on old RRSP/insurance
3. **Communicate your wishes** to all parties (reduces conflict after death)
4. **Consider a marriage/cohabitation contract** addressing estate expectations
5. **Name separate executors** for each "family" if there's potential for conflict

### Child Support and Spousal Support
- Child support obligations generally end at death (but unpaid arrears are a debt of the estate)
- Spousal support may continue after death (dependent on the agreement/court order)
- The estate may be obligated to provide for dependent children regardless of the will

(Source: estate planning lawyers, provincial family law)

## 15. Cottage & Vacation Property Succession

### The Tax Problem
A cottage purchased for $50,000 in 1985 that's now worth $800,000 has a $750,000 capital
gain. At 50% inclusion and ~45% marginal rate: **~$169,000 in tax at death.**

### Principal Residence Exemption Strategy
You can designate your cottage as your principal residence for SOME years (and your home for
the remaining years) to reduce the total capital gains across both properties.

**Formula:** The exempt portion of the gain = (1 + years designated) / years owned × total gain

**Example (simplified):**
- Home: owned 30 years, gained $500K. Cottage: owned 30 years, gained $750K.
- Designate home for 20 years + cottage for 10 years (only 1 property per year per family)
- Home: (1+20)/30 × $500K = $350K exempt, $150K taxable
- Cottage: (1+10)/30 × $750K = $275K exempt, $475K taxable
- **Total taxable gain: $625K** (vs $1.25M if neither designated at all)
- **Optimal allocation requires calculating which split minimizes total tax** — consult a CPA

### Succession Options
1. **Leave it in the will** — simplest, but triggers capital gains tax and probate fees
2. **Transfer during lifetime** — triggers deemed disposition NOW, but you control the timing
   (can do it in a low-income year). Estate freeze may help.
3. **Joint ownership with children** — transfer a portion now, children acquire the rest at
   death. Complex — can create unintended tax consequences.
4. **Family trust** — transfer cottage to trust, children are beneficiaries. Avoids probate.
   But 21-year deemed disposition rule applies.
5. **Life insurance** — purchase a policy to cover the capital gains tax, so heirs can keep
   the cottage without needing to sell it to pay tax.

### Co-Ownership Agreement
If multiple siblings will share the cottage, document IN WRITING:
- Who pays property taxes, insurance, maintenance, utilities
- Usage schedule (who gets which weeks/months)
- Decision-making (renovations, improvements — unanimous or majority)
- What happens if one sibling wants to sell their share
- Right of first refusal (other siblings can match any outside offer)
- Dispute resolution process

**Without an agreement, sibling disputes about cottages are among the most common estate
conflicts in Canada.**

(Source: taxtips.ca/pre, FP Canada, MoneySense)

## 16. Digital Estate Planning

### The Problem
Your digital life is extensive — and your executor needs access.

### What to Prepare

**Financial accounts:**
- Online banking login credentials
- Investment accounts (brokerage, robo-advisor)
- Cryptocurrency wallets (seed phrases, exchange logins) — **if these are lost, the crypto
  is GONE FOREVER.** There's no "forgot password" for a crypto wallet.
- PayPal, payment apps

**Communication:**
- Email accounts (primary and secondary)
- Social media (Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn — each has a legacy contact or memorialization process)

**Subscriptions & services:**
- Streaming services, software subscriptions, domain names, hosting
- Auto-renewing payments that need to be cancelled

**Documents:**
- Cloud storage (Google Drive, iCloud, Dropbox)
- Password manager (1Password, Dashlane, Bitwarden)
- Photo libraries

### How to Prepare
1. **Use a password manager** — store all credentials in one place. Share the master password
   with your executor or store it in a sealed envelope with your will.
2. **Create a digital assets inventory** — list all accounts, platforms, and how to access them
3. **Designate legacy contacts** where available (Facebook, Apple, Google)
4. **Include a digital assets clause in your will** — grant your executor authority to access
   and manage your digital accounts
5. **Cryptocurrency:** Write down seed phrases and store in a secure location (safe deposit
   box, fireproof safe). Tell your executor where to find them.

(Source: canada.ca, digital estate planning resources)

## 17. Charitable Giving in Estate

### Donation of Securities (Most Tax-Efficient)
- Donate publicly-traded securities (stocks, ETFs, mutual funds) directly to a registered charity
- **NO capital gains tax** on the donated securities (the gain is completely exempt)
- Estate receives a **donation tax receipt** for the FMV of the securities
- On the final tax return, donation credits can offset up to **100% of net income** (vs 75%
  in a normal year)

**Example:** $100K in securities with $60K in unrealized gains.
- If sold then donated cash: $60K gain × 50% × 45% = $13,500 capital gains tax. Donation
  credit on $100K ~= $45,000 credit. Net benefit: $31,500.
- If donated directly: $0 capital gains tax. Donation credit ~= $45,000. Net benefit: $45,000.
  **$13,500 more efficient.**

### Testamentary Bequests
- Leave a specific amount or percentage of estate to charity in your will
- Donation receipt issued to the estate
- Credit applied on the final tax return

### Life Insurance to Charity
- Name a registered charity as beneficiary of a life insurance policy
- Premiums may qualify for annual donation tax credits (if charity owns the policy)
- Death benefit goes directly to charity, bypasses estate (no probate fees)

### Charitable Remainder Trust (Advanced)
- Transfer assets to a trust that pays you income during your lifetime
- At death, remaining trust assets go to charity
- You receive a partial donation receipt when the trust is established
- Complex — requires experienced legal counsel

(Source: canada.ca/charities, taxtips.ca/donations)

## 18. Guardianship for Minor Children

### Why This Is Critical
If both parents die without naming a guardian in their will, the court decides who raises
the children. The court's choice may not be who you would have chosen.

### What to Include in Your Will
- **Named guardian:** Who you want to raise your children
- **Alternate guardian:** Second choice if first can't serve
- **Consider:** Values alignment, parenting style, location, age/health of guardian,
  existing relationship with your children, financial stability, willingness
- **Guardian vs executor:** Can be different people. The guardian raises the children.
  The executor manages the money. Separating these roles provides checks and balances.

### Financial Provisions for Children
- **Don't leave money directly to minor children** — they can't legally manage it
- **Name a trustee** (in the will) to manage the children's inheritance until a specified age
  (commonly 18, 21, or 25 — your choice)
- **Instructions to trustee:** What the money can be used for (education, housing, health,
  reasonable living expenses)
- **Staggered distribution:** Consider releasing funds in stages (e.g., 1/3 at 21, 1/3 at 25,
  remainder at 30) rather than a lump sum at 18

### Talking to the Guardian
- **Have the conversation before naming them** — don't surprise someone with this responsibility
- Discuss: financial expectations, your parenting values, religious/cultural preferences,
  education priorities, what happens if the guardian's circumstances change

(Source: provincial family law, estate planning lawyers)

## 19. Common Estate Planning Mistakes

1. **No will (52% of Canadians).** The province decides everything — and common-law partners
   may inherit nothing.
2. **Outdated will.** Marriage may revoke a prior will (in some provinces). Divorce doesn't
   automatically remove an ex-spouse as executor or beneficiary in all situations.
3. **Ex-spouse still named as RRSP/insurance beneficiary.** Beneficiary designations override
   the will. Check every registered account and policy.
4. **No Powers of Attorney.** If you become incapacitated, your family must go to court
   ($5,000-$15,000+) to get authority to manage your affairs.
5. **Naming "estate" as beneficiary on RRSP/TFSA/insurance.** This subjects the proceeds to
   probate fees and creditor claims. Name a person, not your estate.
6. **Not telling anyone where the will is stored.** A will in a safe deposit box that no one
   knows about is useless. Tell your executor where to find it.
7. **Assuming joint ownership solves everything.** Adding a child as joint owner on your home
   can trigger capital gains tax, creditor exposure, and unequal treatment of heirs.
8. **No guardianship designation for minor children.** The court decides — and it may not
   be who you'd choose.
9. **Underestimating the tax bill at death.** A $400K RRIF + a $600K cottage can create a
   $200K+ tax bill. Without liquidity (insurance, cash), the executor must sell assets.
10. **Not planning for incapacity.** Estate planning is about death. Incapacity planning
    (POAs, advance directives) is about what happens while you're alive but can't decide.
11. **DIY trusts or complex provisions.** Poorly drafted trusts can be invalid, trigger
    unintended tax consequences, or disqualify beneficiaries from government benefits.
    Complex estate planning requires a lawyer.
12. **Forgetting digital assets.** Cryptocurrency with no recorded seed phrase is lost forever.
    Online accounts with no shared access create headaches for months.

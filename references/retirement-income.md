## 2. CPP Deep-Dive & Timing Optimization

### CPP Timing: The Core Decision

| Start Age | Monthly Reduction/Increase | Break-Even vs 65 | Best If... |
|-----------|---------------------------|-------------------|-----------|
| 60 | -36% (0.6%/month × 60 months) | Age ~74 | Poor health, need income now, no other bridge |
| 65 | 0% (standard) | Standard | Average health, moderate savings |
| 70 | +42% (0.7%/month × 60 months) | Age ~82 | Good health, can bridge with other income, HOOPP/pension |

**Example (2025 — verify):**
If your CPP at 65 would be $1,000/month:
- At 60: $640/month for life (36% less)
- At 65: $1,000/month for life
- At 70: $1,420/month for life (42% more)

**Cumulative received:**
- Start at 60: By age 74 you've received $640 × 168 months = $107,520
- Start at 65: By age 74 you've received $1,000 × 108 months = $108,000 → **break-even at ~74**
- Start at 70: By age 82 you've received $1,420 × 144 months = $204,480
- Start at 65: By age 82 you've received $1,000 × 204 months = $204,000 → **break-even at ~82**

**After the break-even age, the later start wins permanently.** If you live to 90:
- Started at 60: $640 × 360 = $230,400 total
- Started at 65: $1,000 × 300 = $300,000 total (**$70K more**)
- Started at 70: $1,420 × 240 = $340,800 total (**$110K more than 60**)

### When to Take CPP Early (Age 60)
- **Poor health** or family history of short lifespan (under ~74)
- **No other income** and need the money to survive
- **Significant debt** that CPP can help eliminate
- **Spouse passed away** and you need income immediately
- **Warning:** Early CPP is permanent — the reduction never goes away

### When to Defer CPP (Age 70)
- **Good health** and family longevity (parents lived to 85+)
- **Have bridge income** (TFSA, pension, savings, part-time work) to cover ages 60-70
- **Have a DB pension** (like HOOPP) that provides base income — don't need CPP early
- **Want maximum survivor benefit** — contributor's deferral may increase survivor's pension
- **The math:** Deferring CPP from 65 to 70 is an 8.4% annual guaranteed, inflation-indexed
  return. No investment matches this risk-free.

### CPP Features Most People Don't Know

**Post-Retirement Benefit (PRB):**
If you continue working after starting CPP (ages 60-70), your ongoing CPP contributions
earn additional PRB credits. Each year of work adds a small permanent increase:
- Max PRB at age 65: ~$55/month (2026 — verify) per year of maximum contributions
- Contributions are mandatory ages 60-65 (if employer), optional 65-70
- Small amounts but they're permanent, inflation-indexed additions to your CPP

**Child-Rearing Provision:**
Years spent out of the workforce caring for children under 7 can be excluded from your CPP
calculation. This prevents those low/zero-earning years from dragging down your average.
- Applies automatically if you received Family Allowance or CCB
- Can boost CPP by hundreds of dollars/month for parents who took years off

**Credit Splitting on Divorce:**
CPP credits earned during a marriage/common-law relationship can be split equally between
former spouses. Apply to Service Canada — this is separate from pension division.
- Applies to years of cohabitation only
- Can significantly affect both parties' CPP (increases one, decreases other)

**Disability-to-Retirement Transition:**
If receiving CPP-Disability, it automatically converts to CPP-Retirement at age 65.
No action needed — the transition is seamless.

(Source: canada.ca/cpp, service-canada.gc.ca)

## 3. OAS Deferral Decision Tree

### The Math
OAS increases by **0.6% per month** deferred past age 65, up to age 70.
- Maximum deferral bonus: 0.6% × 60 months = **36% increase**
- Max OAS at 65: ~$727/month (2025 — verify)
- Max OAS at 70: ~$989/month (36% more)

**Break-even age for OAS deferral (65 vs 70): approximately age 82-83.**
If you expect to live past 83, deferral wins.

### When to Defer OAS
- Good health, expect to live past 83
- Have other income to bridge ages 65-70
- Income is near or above the OAS clawback threshold ($93,454 net income, 2025 — verify)
  → deferring avoids the clawback during high-income years

### When NOT to Defer OAS
- **GIS-eligible:** If your income is low enough to qualify for GIS, take OAS at 65.
  GIS is income-tested and can provide up to ~$1,072/month (2025 — verify) on top of OAS.
  Deferring OAS means losing GIS for those years — the GIS loss usually exceeds the OAS gain.
- **Poor health** or short life expectancy
- **Need the income** to cover basic expenses
- **Already receiving GIS** — deferral makes no sense

### OAS Clawback Math
- Clawback starts at ~$93,454 net income (2025 — verify, indexed annually)
- For every $1 above the threshold, you lose $0.15 in OAS (15% recovery tax)
- Full clawback at ~$151,668 (OAS reduced to $0)

**Strategies to minimize clawback:**
1. **TFSA withdrawals** don't count as income → use TFSA instead of RRIF when near threshold
2. **Pension income splitting** (Form T1032) → shift up to 50% of eligible pension to spouse
3. **Capital gains timing** → only 50% of capital gains count as income
4. **RRSP meltdown** in early 60s → reduce RRIF balance before OAS starts (see Section 6)
5. **Non-registered investments** → return of capital from REITs/ETFs doesn't count as income

(Source: canada.ca/oas, taxtips.ca)

## 4. GIS Optimization Strategies

GIS is the most underused retirement benefit in Canada. Billions go unclaimed every year.

### Who Qualifies
- Must be receiving OAS
- Individual annual income (excluding OAS) below ~$22,488 (2025 — verify)
- Couple combined income below ~$29,712 (2025 — verify)
- Maximum benefit: ~$1,072/month for singles (2025 — verify)

### What Counts as Income for GIS (and What Doesn't)

| Income Source | Counts for GIS? | Notes |
|--------------|----------------|-------|
| CPP/QPP | YES | Full amount |
| RRIF/RRSP withdrawals | YES | Full amount |
| Employment income | PARTIALLY | First $5,000 exempt, next $10,000 at 50% |
| Pension (DB/DC) | YES | Full amount |
| Interest, dividends | YES | Full amount |
| Capital gains | YES | But only 50% inclusion |
| OAS | NO | Excluded from GIS calculation |
| TFSA withdrawals | **NO** | Completely invisible to GIS |
| GIS itself | NO | Not counted |
| Inheritance (lump sum) | NO | Not income |
| Principal residence sale | NO | PRE-exempt capital gain |

### The TFSA Strategy for GIS
**This is the single most powerful GIS optimization:**
- Every dollar in RRIF that could be in TFSA costs ~$0.50 in GIS clawback
- Before turning 65, shift as much as possible from RRSP/non-reg to TFSA
- TFSA withdrawals generate ZERO income for GIS purposes
- Example: $20,000 RRIF withdrawal = $10,000 GIS clawback. $20,000 TFSA withdrawal = $0 impact

### GIS Clawback Rate
- For every $1 of "other income," GIS is reduced by **$0.50** (50% clawback)
- This means the effective marginal tax rate for GIS recipients can exceed 70%:
  - $1 RRIF withdrawal → ~$0.15 income tax + $0.50 GIS clawback = $0.65 lost
- **This is why RRSP meltdown before 65 is so important for low-income seniors**

### Must File Tax Return
GIS eligibility is recalculated annually based on your tax return. If you don't file, you
lose GIS. File every year, even if income is $0.

(Source: canada.ca/gis, service-canada.gc.ca)

## 5. RRSP-to-RRIF Conversion Strategy

### The Rules
- RRSP must convert to RRIF by **December 31 of the year you turn 71**
- You can convert earlier (any age) — sometimes beneficial
- Once converted, minimum annual withdrawals are mandatory (see table below)

### RRIF Minimum Withdrawal Table

| Age | Minimum % | On $500K = | On $1M = |
|-----|----------|-----------|---------|
| 72 | 5.28% | $26,400 | $52,800 |
| 75 | 5.82% | $29,100 | $58,200 |
| 78 | 6.36% | $31,800 | $63,600 |
| 80 | 6.82% | $34,100 | $68,200 |
| 85 | 8.51% | $42,550 | $85,100 |
| 90 | 11.92% | $59,600 | $119,200 |
| 94+ | 20.00% | $100,000 | $200,000 |

**The problem:** At 90+ with a large RRIF, forced withdrawals push you into high tax brackets
and trigger full OAS clawback. A $1M RRIF at age 90 forces $119,200 withdrawal — that's
~$40,000+ in tax and full OAS clawback.

### Spouse's Age Election
You can base RRIF minimums on your **younger spouse's age** instead of your own.
- If you're 75 and spouse is 70: minimum is 3.85% (age 70) instead of 5.82% (age 75)
- On $500K: $19,250 vs $29,100 = **$9,850 less forced withdrawal**
- Must elect at time of RRIF setup — can't change later
- **Best for:** Large RRIF balances where you don't need the income

### Over-Withdrawal Strategy
In low-income years (first years of retirement, before CPP/OAS at full strength):
- Withdraw MORE than the RRIF minimum (you're in a low tax bracket)
- This reduces the RRIF balance, which means lower forced minimums in later high-income years
- **Example:** Age 63, income $30K. Withdraw $40K from RRIF (tax ~$8K). At age 80, RRIF is
  $40K smaller = $2,700 less forced withdrawal each year. Over 15 years = $40K less taxable income.

### Withholding Tax on RRIF
Withdrawals above the minimum trigger withholding:
- 10% on amounts up to $5,000
- 20% on $5,001-$15,000
- 30% on amounts over $15,000
- Quebec: additional provincial withholding

This is NOT a final tax — it's a prepayment. Your actual tax is determined when you file.

(Source: canada.ca/rrif, taxtips.ca)

## 6. RRSP Meltdown Strategy

### What It Is
Deliberately withdrawing RRSP/RRIF in your early 60s (when income is low) to reduce the
balance before mandatory minimums and OAS/GIS kick in at 65+.

### Why It Works
- In your early 60s, if you've retired and have no employment income, you're in a **low
  tax bracket** (potentially 0% on first ~$16K via basic personal amount)
- Withdrawing RRSP at this low rate is much cheaper than being forced to withdraw at 80+
  when CPP + OAS + RRIF minimums push you into 40-50% brackets
- Also prevents OAS clawback and preserves GIS eligibility

### Example
**Without meltdown:**
- Age 63-65: Income $0. RRSP sits at $400K growing.
- Age 72: RRSP converts to RRIF at $550K. Minimum = $29K. Plus CPP $18K + OAS $9K = $56K total. Tax ~$12K.
- Age 85: RRIF at $500K. Minimum = $42.5K. Plus CPP $18K + OAS $9K = $69.5K. OAS clawback: $0. Tax ~$16K.

**With meltdown:**
- Age 63: Withdraw $50K from RRSP. Tax ~$7K (low bracket, basic personal amount offsets first $16K).
- Age 64: Withdraw $50K. Tax ~$7K.
- Age 72: RRSP converts to RRIF at $400K (instead of $550K). Minimum = $21K. Plus CPP $18K + OAS $9K = $48K. Tax ~$8K.
- **Saved ~$4K/year in tax from age 72 onward** = $60K+ over 15 years.

### When It Makes Sense
- You're retiring before 65 with low income
- Your RRSP/RRIF balance is $300K+ and growing
- You expect CPP + OAS + minimum withdrawals to push you near the OAS clawback threshold
- You're GIS-eligible and RRIF withdrawals would reduce your GIS

### When It Doesn't
- You need the RRSP money to stay invested (can't afford to withdraw)
- Your income in early 60s is already high (from pension, employment, etc.)
- Your RRSP is small enough that minimums won't cause problems

### The TFSA Twist
Withdraw from RRSP, pay the tax, and contribute the after-tax amount to your TFSA (if you
have room). The money moves from a taxable account to a tax-free account. Every dollar in
TFSA instead of RRIF saves future tax AND preserves GIS.

(Source: taxtips.ca, FP Canada retirement planning guides)

## 7. Retirement Income Layering (Which Source, Which Year)

### The Sequencing Framework

| Age | Primary Income Sources | Strategy |
|-----|----------------------|----------|
| **55-60** | TFSA + non-registered + part-time work | Meltdown RRSP at low rates. No government benefits yet. |
| **60-65** | CPP (if taken early) + TFSA + non-reg + RRSP meltdown | Bridge to OAS. Consider partial CPP at 60 if needed. |
| **65-70** | OAS + CPP (if taken at 65) + GIS (if eligible) + TFSA | Minimize RRIF to preserve GIS. TFSA is invisible to clawback. |
| **70-72** | Full CPP (if deferred) + OAS + TFSA + non-reg | CPP at maximum. OAS at maximum if deferred. |
| **72+** | CPP + OAS + RRIF (forced minimum) + TFSA buffer | Manage RRIF minimums. Use TFSA to smooth income spikes. |
| **85+** | CPP + OAS + RRIF (increasing minimums) + estate drawdown | RRIF minimums are now very high. LTC costs may appear. |

### General Rules
1. **Non-registered first** — stop the annual tax drag (interest, dividends, capital gains)
2. **RRSP/RRIF second** — meltdown in low-income years, then forced minimums at 72+
3. **TFSA last** — tax-free growth as long as possible, invisible to clawbacks
4. **Exception for GIS:** If GIS-eligible, flip the RRSP/TFSA order — use TFSA first to
   preserve GIS, keep RRIF withdrawals minimal

### OAS Clawback Zone Management
If total income is approaching $93,454:
- Stop RRIF withdrawals (take only the minimum)
- Use TFSA for additional income needs
- Split pension income with spouse (Form T1032)
- Time capital gains realization to low-income years

(Source: FP Canada, taxtips.ca/retirement)

## 8. Pension Decisions (DB vs DC, Commuted Value)

### DB Pension: Monthly vs Commuted Value

**Monthly pension:** Receive $X/month for life, starting at retirement.
- **Pros:** Guaranteed, inflation-indexed (some plans), no investment risk, survivor options
- **Cons:** Locked in, no flexibility, dies with you (except survivor %, typically 60%)

**Commuted value:** Take a lump sum (PV of future pension payments) transferred to a LIRA/RRSP.
- **Pros:** Full control, can pass to heirs, investment flexibility
- **Cons:** Investment risk is yours, can outlive the money, complex to manage

### When to Take the Monthly Pension
- You expect to live a long time (family longevity)
- You're risk-averse and want guaranteed income
- The pension is indexed to inflation
- You have a spouse who benefits from the survivor option
- You don't want to manage investments in your 80s+

### When to Take Commuted Value
- Poor health (short life expectancy — get the lump sum now)
- Interest rates are low (commuted value is HIGH when rates are low)
- You're confident in your investment ability (or have a fee-only advisor)
- You want to leave the full amount to heirs (monthly pension dies with you/survivor)
- You're leaving Canada (pension may not be portable)

### Commuted Value Sizing
Rough rule: commuted value ≈ 10-15× annual pension (varies by age, discount rate, interest rates).
- $40,000/year pension → commuted value ~$400,000-$600,000
- At higher interest rates: commuted value is LOWER (discount rate is higher)
- At lower interest rates: commuted value is HIGHER

### DC Pension: LIRA Rules
DC pension balances are transferred to a **Locked-In Retirement Account (LIRA)** on departure.
- Can't withdraw freely (locked-in rules, provincial)
- Converts to **LIF (Life Income Fund)** or annuity at retirement
- Maximum annual withdrawal from LIF varies by province and age
- Some provinces allow partial unlocking: small balance unlocking ($23,890 in Ontario, 2025 — verify), shortened life expectancy, financial hardship, non-residency

(Source: provincial pension legislation, canada.ca)

## 9. Pension Income Splitting (Form T1032)

### What Can Be Split
**Age 65+:** Up to 50% of eligible pension income:
- RRIF withdrawals
- Life annuity from RPP (defined benefit pension payments)
- DPSP payments
- Some PRPP payments

**Under 65:** Only life annuity payments from RPP qualify. RRIF and RRSP withdrawals do NOT
qualify until age 65.

### What Cannot Be Split
- CPP/QPP (use CPP pension sharing instead — different program)
- OAS
- Employment income
- Investment income
- RRSP withdrawals (any age)
- RRIF withdrawals (under 65)

### How It Saves Tax
**Example:**
- Spouse A: $80K income (CPP $18K + RRIF $52K + OAS $10K). Marginal rate ~36%.
- Spouse B: $25K income (CPP $7K + OAS $10K + GIS $8K). Marginal rate ~20%.
- **Without splitting:** Household tax ~$18,000
- **Split $26K of RRIF (50%) to Spouse B:** Spouse A income = $54K, Spouse B = $51K
- **With splitting:** Household tax ~$14,000. **Savings: ~$4,000/year**

### Interaction with GIS
**Warning:** If one spouse is receiving GIS, splitting pension income TO them increases their
income and reduces their GIS. Run the math both ways before splitting — sometimes the GIS
loss exceeds the tax saving.

### CPP Pension Sharing (Different from Splitting)
- Both spouses must be 60+ and receiving CPP retirement pension
- Share CPP credits based on years of cohabitation
- Apply to Service Canada (not CRA — this is a pension adjustment, not a tax election)
- Reduces tax if one spouse has much higher CPP than the other
- **Can be combined with pension income splitting** for maximum benefit

### How to File
- Both spouses must agree and sign Form T1032
- File with BOTH tax returns
- Must be elected annually (not permanent)
- Can choose different split percentages each year (0% to 50%)

(Source: canada.ca/pension-splitting, Form T1032)

## 10. Bridge Income for Early Retirement

### The Challenge
Retiring before 60 means 5-10+ years with no CPP, no OAS, no GIS. You need to fund the
gap entirely from savings, investments, and/or part-time work.

### Bridge Income Sources (Ranked by Tax Efficiency)

| Source | Tax Impact | GIS Impact | Best For |
|--------|-----------|-----------|----------|
| TFSA | Tax-free | No impact | Primary bridge source |
| Non-registered (capital gains) | 50% taxable | 50% counts | When gains are modest |
| Part-time work | Fully taxable | First $5K exempt | Supplemental income |
| RRSP meltdown | Fully taxable | Counts | Low-bracket years only |
| HELOC | Not income (loan) | No impact | Short-term bridge |
| CPP at 60 (reduced) | Fully taxable | Counts | Last resort |

### Example: Retire at 58, Need $70K/Year

| Source | Annual Amount | Tax |
|--------|-------------|-----|
| TFSA withdrawal | $25,000 | $0 |
| Non-reg capital gains (sell $40K, $15K gain) | $25,000 | ~$2,500 |
| RRSP meltdown | $20,000 | ~$3,000 |
| **Total** | **$70,000** | **~$5,500** |

At age 60, add CPP ($15K/year reduced). At 65, add OAS ($9K/year). Portfolio withdrawal
decreases as government income layers in.

### Part-Time Work in Retirement
- **CPP Post-Retirement Benefit:** If you work and contribute to CPP after starting your
  pension (ages 60-70), you earn additional PRB credits: up to ~$55/month per year of
  maximum contributions (2026 — verify). Small but permanent.
- **Tax implication:** Employment income is fully taxable. At $20K/year part-time, tax is
  ~$3,000-$4,000 (depending on other income).
- **Psychological benefit:** Studies show part-time work in early retirement significantly
  improves well-being and cognitive health.

### FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early) — Canadian Context
Retiring at 40-50 requires a much larger portfolio because you can't access CPP/OAS for
15-25 years:
- **With government income at 65:** Need ~4% of annual expenses × 25 = portfolio. Example:
  $80K expenses, $35K from CPP/OAS at 65, need $45K from portfolio = ~$1.125M at 4%.
- **Without government income (ages 40-60):** Need full $80K from portfolio = ~$2M at 4%.
- **Strategy:** Heavy TFSA and non-registered (RRSP creates RRIF problems at 72). Maintain
  low expenses. Plan for 50+ year retirement horizon.

(Source: FP Canada, taxtips.ca, Vanguard retirement research)

## 11. Annuities as Retirement Income

### Life Annuity
Exchange a lump sum for guaranteed monthly income for life.

**Approximate payout (2025 — verify, rates change with interest rates):**
- $100,000 at age 65: ~$500-$550/month for life (male), ~$475-$525/month (female)
- $100,000 at age 70: ~$575-$625/month (higher because shorter expected payout period)
- $100,000 at age 75: ~$675-$750/month

**Pros:** Guaranteed income, no investment risk, can't outlive it, simplifies finances
**Cons:** No flexibility (can't get money back), no inheritance, inflation erodes value
(unless indexed — indexed annuities pay ~20-30% less initially)

### When Annuities Make Sense
- Age 75+ and tired of managing investments
- Want to de-risk completely
- No heirs or don't need to leave an inheritance
- Already have TFSA and non-reg for flexible needs
- Supplement to CPP/OAS for guaranteed base income

### When They Don't
- Under 70 (too early — lock in at low rates, miss growth)
- Need flexibility (health costs, travel, emergencies)
- Want to leave money to heirs
- Interest rates are very low (annuity payouts are poor)

### Prescribed Annuity (Non-Registered Only)
For money in non-registered accounts, a prescribed annuity offers better tax treatment:
- Each payment is split: part is return of capital (tax-free), part is interest (taxable)
- The split is leveled over the annuity term (constant taxable portion each year)
- Much more tax-efficient than RRIF withdrawals from non-registered investments

### Partial Annuitization
Don't annuitize everything. Consider annuitizing enough to cover essential fixed expenses
(housing, food, utilities, insurance) and keep the rest invested for flexibility.

**Example:** Essential expenses = $3,000/month. CPP + OAS = $2,200/month. Gap = $800/month.
Annuitize ~$175,000 to cover the $800 gap. Keep remaining portfolio invested for variable
expenses, travel, emergencies, and inheritance.

(Source: canada.ca/annuities, lifeannuities.com, Sun Life)

## 12. Retirement Budget Planning

### How Expenses Change in Retirement

**Expenses that typically DECREASE:**
- Commuting and work-related transportation
- Work clothing, professional wardrobe
- Workplace meals and coffee
- Professional dues, continuing education
- Mortgage (if paid off by retirement — plan for this)
- RRSP/pension contributions (you're now withdrawing, not contributing)
- CPP/EI premiums (no longer working)
- Childcare (kids are adults)

**Expenses that typically INCREASE:**
- Healthcare: dental ($1,000-$3,000/year without employer plan), prescriptions ($1,500-$5,000+/year), vision ($500/year), therapy/paramedical
- Travel: many retirees spend heavily on travel in their 60s-70s ("go-go years")
- Hobbies and recreation
- Home maintenance (aging home needs more work)
- Long-term care (ages 80+): $36,000-$180,000+/year depending on level and province

### The "Go-Go, Slow-Go, No-Go" Framework
- **Go-Go (65-75):** Active, travel, hobbies. Spending is highest. Budget ~90-100% of pre-retirement.
- **Slow-Go (75-85):** Activity decreases. Spending drops. Budget ~70-80% of pre-retirement.
- **No-Go (85+):** Home-bound or care facility. Healthcare costs spike. Budget varies wildly — could be very low (staying home) or very high (private LTC at $8,000-$15,000/month).

### Healthcare Cost Projection
Without employer benefits (which end at retirement for most private-sector workers):

| Item | Annual Cost (Estimate) | Notes |
|------|----------------------|-------|
| Dental | $1,000-$3,000 | Major work (crowns, implants) can be $3,000-$10,000 |
| Prescriptions | $1,500-$5,000 | Provincial pharmacare helps (income-based deductible) |
| Vision | $400-$600 | Glasses every 2-3 years, exams |
| Paramedical | $1,000-$3,000 | Physio, massage, chiropractic if needed |
| Hearing aids | $0-$5,000 | One-time cost, may need replacement every 5 years |
| **Total (healthy)** | **$4,000-$8,000/year** | |
| **LTC (if needed)** | **$36,000-$180,000/year** | Subsidized vs private, province matters |

### The "70-80% Rule" — Is It Accurate?
The rule of thumb says you need 70-80% of pre-retirement income. Reality varies:
- Mortgage paid off + no commute + kids gone: could be 60%
- Travel, hobbies, healthcare: could be 90%+
- **Better approach:** Build a line-by-line retirement budget with actual expected expenses,
  not a percentage rule.

(Source: FP Canada, Statistics Canada retirement spending data)

## 13. Retirement for Couples

### Coordinated CPP Timing
If one spouse earned significantly more, their CPP is more valuable to defer:
- **High earner:** Defer to 70 (bigger pension, bigger survivor benefit)
- **Lower earner:** Take at 65 (or 60 if needed for bridge income)
- **Why:** If the high earner dies first, the survivor gets ~60% of the larger pension.
  Deferring maximizes this survivor benefit.

### Income Equalization Strategy
Equalize incomes between spouses to minimize household tax:
- **Spousal RRSP:** Higher earner contributes, lower earner withdraws in retirement
- **TFSA gifting:** Give money to lower-income spouse to fill their TFSA (no attribution)
- **Pension splitting:** Form T1032, up to 50% of eligible pension income
- **CPP sharing:** Both 60+, share retirement pension based on cohabitation years

### Survivor Planning
When one spouse dies, the household income drops but expenses don't drop proportionally:
- Housing stays the same (or similar)
- Utilities, insurance, property tax stay the same
- Only food and some personal expenses decrease
- **CPP survivor pension** partially replaces deceased's CPP
- **OAS** is individual — surviving spouse keeps theirs
- **RRSP/RRIF** rolls to surviving spouse tax-free
- **Life insurance** provides a lump sum buffer

**Plan for the "survivor gap"** — the difference between two-income household and one-income
household. This is why adequate life insurance matters even in retirement.

### Two-Pension Household
If both spouses have DB pensions:
- Each pension has survivor options (typically 60% to surviving spouse)
- Consider: if both pensions provide $30K/year each, survivor gets $30K (own) + $18K (60% of
  deceased's) = $48K. Plus CPP survivor + OAS. Usually adequate.
- If only one has a pension: survivor planning is more critical.

(Source: FP Canada, service-canada.gc.ca)

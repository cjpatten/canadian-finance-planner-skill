# Debt Strategy — Deep-Dive for Canadians in Serious Debt

Read this reference when debt is a major concern: payday loans, collections, CRA tax debt,
consumer proposals, bankruptcy, credit rebuilding, or when the user can't make minimum payments.
Also read when the crisis protocol in SKILL.md activates.

All dollar amounts should be verified via web search before presenting — they change annually.
Key sources: canada.ca (OSB, CRA, NSLSC), FCAC, Credit Counselling Canada, provincial

## 1. When to Use This Reference

Load this file when the user:
- Has payday loans or other predatory debt
- Is in collections or being contacted by collection agencies
- Owes money to CRA and can't pay
- Is considering (or has filed) a consumer proposal or bankruptcy
- Can't make minimum payments on their debts
- Asks about credit scores, credit reports, or rebuilding credit
- Has student loan repayment difficulties
- Expresses significant debt stress (stress level 8-10)
- Is in financial crisis (can't make rent, utilities disconnected, etc.)

The existing calculations in `calculations-and-dashboard.md` section 1.3 handle normal debt
payoff (avalanche/snowball). This file handles the harder situations.

## 2. Debt Triage: Assessing the Situation

Use this decision tree to determine the right path:

### Can they make all minimum payments?
**YES** → Standard debt payoff (avalanche or snowball from calculations-and-dashboard.md).
Focus on optimization: which debt to attack first, where to find extra money.

### Can they make SOME but not all minimums?
**Negotiate** → Contact creditors to reduce payments or interest. Consider a Debt Management
Plan (DMP) through non-profit credit counselling. Consider consolidation if they qualify for
a lower-rate loan or LOC.

### Can they make NO minimums / are they in default?
**Formal options** → Free consultation with a Licensed Insolvency Trustee (LIT). The LIT will
assess whether a consumer proposal or bankruptcy is the better path. This consultation is
free and confidential — there's no obligation.

### Are they in immediate crisis? (Can't make rent, no food, utilities disconnected)
**Stabilize first** → 211 Canada for local crisis resources. Food banks. Emergency shelter.
Provincial emergency assistance (Ontario Works, BC Income Assistance, etc.). Do NOT focus
on debt payoff — focus on survival. Debt strategy comes after basic needs are met.

### Key numbers to calculate:
- **Debt-to-income ratio:** Total monthly debt payments / gross monthly income. Over 40% = serious.
- **Total interest paid per month:** Sum of (balance × annual rate / 12) for each debt. This
  is money that's doing nothing for them.
- **Months to payoff at current payments:** If any debt would take 20+ years at minimums, flag it.
- **Insolvency test:** Total debts > total assets (excluding exempt assets)? If yes, they're
  technically insolvent and formal options should be discussed.

## 3. Consolidation: When It Helps vs When It's a Trap

### Good Consolidation
- Moving high-interest debt to a lower-rate product: CC at 20% → LOC at 8% saves significant
  interest. Calculate exact savings and show the math.
- One payment instead of many reduces cognitive load and missed payments.
- Works best when: the root cause of the debt is solved (one-time event like job loss, medical
  emergency) and spending is now under control.

### Bad Consolidation (The #1 Trap)
- Consolidating credit card debt into a LOC, then running the credit cards back up. Now they
  have BOTH the LOC balance AND new CC debt. This is extremely common.
- Consolidation treats the symptom, not the cause. If spending habits haven't changed,
  consolidation makes things worse.
- **Rule of thumb:** Only consolidate if the user commits to not using the paid-off credit
  cards (freeze them, reduce limits, or close them).

### Balance Transfer Strategies
- 0% promotional rate cards: transfer existing CC balance, pay 0% for 6-12 months.
- Fees: typically 1-3% of transferred balance (one-time).
- Risk: if not paid off before promo expires, rate jumps to 20%+.
- Strategy: divide the balance by the promo months to calculate the required monthly payment.
  Example: $5,000 balance, 12-month promo = $417/month to clear it at 0%.
- **Only works if they can actually make those payments.** If not, this just delays the problem.

### Home Equity Consolidation (HELOC)
- HELOC rates are much lower (prime + 0.5-2% vs CC at 20%).
- **Danger:** Converts unsecured debt to secured debt. If they default on CCs, credit score
  suffers. If they default on HELOC, they can lose their home.
- Only consider if: the total debt is manageable relative to equity, they have stable income,
  and spending habits have changed.
- Never consolidate ALL debt into HELOC — keep some unsecured debt as a buffer.

### When NOT to Consolidate
- If they can't afford the consolidated payment either → consumer proposal is likely better.
- If total unsecured debt exceeds 50% of annual gross income → consider formal options.
- If they've consolidated before and re-accumulated debt → the pattern suggests consolidation
  won't work. Consider DMP or proposal instead.

## 4. Consumer Proposals (Canada-Specific)

A consumer proposal is a formal, legally binding agreement between you and your unsecured
creditors to repay a portion of what you owe. It's the most common insolvency option in
Canada — 127,000 Canadians filed in 2025. (Source: canada.ca/OSB)

### Process
1. **Free consultation with a Licensed Insolvency Trustee (LIT)** — confidential, no obligation.
   The LIT reviews income, debts, assets, and expenses.
2. **LIT files the proposal** with the Office of the Superintendent of Bankruptcy (OSB).
   Immediately: stay of proceedings takes effect — all collections calls, lawsuits, wage
   garnishments, and interest charges STOP.
3. **Creditors have 45 days** to review and vote. If creditors holding a majority of the total
   dollar value of claims vote to accept, the proposal is binding on ALL unsecured creditors.
4. **97% approval rate** — creditors usually accept because they get more than they would
   in a bankruptcy. (Source: mnpdebt.ca)
5. **Make payments** for the agreed term (typically 3-5 years, max 5 years).
6. **Complete two mandatory counselling sessions** during the proposal period.
7. **Receive certificate of completion** and the remaining debt is legally discharged.

### Cost
Fees are set by federal law and come OUT of your payments, not on top:
- $750 LIT fee on filing + $750 on approval = $1,500 base
- 20% of funds distributed to creditors
- $170 for two mandatory counselling sessions
- $100 government filing fee
- **You pay nothing upfront.** Everything comes from your monthly proposal payments.

### Typical Terms
- Repay 20-40% of total unsecured debt over 3-5 years.
- Example: $50,000 in unsecured debt, proposal at 30% = $15,000 over 5 years = $250/month.
  Compare to minimum payments on $50K debt = years of $800+/month with most going to interest.

### What's Included
- Credit cards, lines of credit, personal loans
- CRA tax debt
- Student loans (if 7+ years since leaving school — see Section 8)
- Payday loans
- Medical debts, utility arrears

### What's NOT Included
- Secured debts (mortgage, car loan) — these continue as normal
- Child support and spousal support arrears
- Court-ordered fines
- Fraud-related debts

### Eligibility
- Owe at least $1,000 in unsecured debt
- Unsecured debts under $250,000 (excluding mortgage). Over $250K → Division I proposal
  (different process, still possible).
- Must be insolvent (unable to pay debts as they come due)

### Credit Impact
- **R7 rating** on your credit report during the proposal.
- Stays on credit report for **3 years after completion** or **6 years from filing date**,
  whichever comes first.
- Compare to bankruptcy: R9 rating, stays 6-7 years after discharge.
- You can start rebuilding credit immediately after completion (see Section 11).

### Consumer Proposal vs Bankruptcy
| Factor | Consumer Proposal | Bankruptcy |
|--------|------------------|------------|
| Keep assets | Yes — all assets | Lose non-exempt assets |
| Duration | Up to 5 years | 9-21 months |
| Credit impact | R7, 3 years after | R9, 6-7 years after |
| Cost | 20-40% of debt | Surplus income + non-exempt assets |
| Monthly payments | Fixed, agreed upfront | Variable (surplus income) |
| Creditor approval | Required (45-day vote) | Not required |
| Best for | Higher income, assets to protect | Low income, few assets |

## 5. Bankruptcy (BIA Process)

Bankruptcy is the option of last resort but sometimes it's the right choice. Only a Licensed
Insolvency Trustee (LIT) can administer it. (Source: canada.ca/OSB)

### When Bankruptcy Is the Right Choice
- Income is too low to afford even a consumer proposal
- Total debts vastly exceed ability to repay any meaningful portion
- No significant assets to protect (or assets are already exempt)
- Need a faster fresh start than a 5-year consumer proposal

### Process
1. **Free LIT consultation** — same as consumer proposal, LIT assesses all options.
2. **LIT files assignment in bankruptcy** with the OSB.
3. **Stay of proceedings** takes effect immediately — all collections, garnishments, and
   lawsuits stop.
4. **Surrender non-exempt assets** to the LIT (see provincial exemptions below).
5. **Report monthly income and expenses** to LIT for the entire bankruptcy period.
6. **Complete two mandatory counselling sessions** (first within 60 days, second within 210 days).
7. **Discharge** — debts are legally eliminated.

### Duration
- **First bankruptcy, no surplus income:** 9 months to automatic discharge.
- **First bankruptcy, with surplus income (>$200/month over threshold):** 21 months.
- **Second bankruptcy:** 24 months (no surplus) or 36 months (with surplus).
- Surplus income thresholds (2025 — verify):
  - Single person: ~$2,543/month
  - Two-person household: ~$3,167/month
  - Three-person: ~$3,892/month
  - Four-person: ~$4,725/month

### Provincial Asset Exemptions (Key Examples — 2025, verify)

**Ontario:**
- Home equity: $12,997
- Vehicle: $7,117
- Household goods: $14,405
- Tools of trade: $14,405
- RRSPs: fully exempt (except contributions made in last 12 months)
- Clothing: $7,117

**Alberta:**
- Home equity: $40,000
- Vehicle: $5,000
- Household goods: $4,000
- RRSPs: fully exempt (except last 12 months)

**British Columbia:**
- Home equity: $12,000 (Greater Vancouver/Victoria), $9,000 (elsewhere)
- Vehicle: $5,000 ($2,000 if in arrears with child support)
- Household goods: no specific dollar limit (necessary furnishings)
- RRSPs: fully exempt (except last 12 months)

**Quebec:**
- Home equity: depends on context (often $0 for owned property in some circumstances)
- Vehicle: $7,000 (necessary for work)
- Household goods: necessary furnishings
- RRSPs: fully exempt (except last 12 months)

*Always verify current exemptions — they change. See sources in Section 13.*

### Credit Impact
- **R9 rating** (worst possible) during and after bankruptcy.
- First bankruptcy: stays on credit report **6-7 years after discharge** (varies by province
  and credit bureau — Equifax 6 years, TransUnion 6-7 years).
- Second bankruptcy: stays **14 years after discharge.**
- You CAN start rebuilding credit after discharge (see Section 11).

### What Bankruptcy Does NOT Discharge
- Student loans (if less than 7 years since leaving school)
- Child support and spousal support
- Court-ordered fines and restitution
- Debts arising from fraud
- Secured debts (you either keep paying or surrender the asset)

## 6. Collections & Creditor Remedies (Provincial)

### Limitation Periods by Province
The statute of limitations is how long a creditor has to SUE you for the debt. After this
period, they can still contact you, but they can't take you to court. (Source: provincial
limitation acts, hoyes.com)

| Province | Limitation Period | Notes |
|----------|------------------|-------|
| Ontario | 2 years | From last payment or acknowledgment |
| Alberta | 2 years | Reset by written acknowledgment |
| British Columbia | 2 years | Basic Limitation Act |
| Saskatchewan | 2 years | As of 2020 update |
| Manitoba | 6 years | Longest in Canada |
| Quebec | 3 years | Civil Code of Quebec |
| New Brunswick | 6 years | Limitation of Actions Act |
| Nova Scotia | 6 years | Limitation of Actions Act |
| PEI | 6 years | Statute of Limitations |
| Newfoundland | 6 years | Limitations Act |

**(2025 — verify all. Provinces update these periodically.)**

### What Resets the Limitation Clock
- Making any payment on the debt (even $1)
- Written acknowledgment of the debt (letter, email, text, even "I'm working on it")
- A new agreement or promise to pay
- **Critical:** Courts in Ontario have confirmed that even a casual email acknowledging
  the debt resets the 2-year clock. Be very careful about what you say to collectors.

### Wage Garnishment Rules
- Creditors MUST get a court judgment first before garnishing wages (except CRA — see Section 7)
- Typical garnishment: up to **20% of gross wages** for commercial creditors
- Child support garnishment: up to **50%** of wages
- Federal government debts (student loans, EI overpayments): can garnish without court order
  via "set-off" or Requirements to Pay
- Some income is exempt from garnishment: Employment Insurance, CPP, OAS, social assistance

### Your Rights with Collectors
- Collectors **cannot** call you at unreasonable hours (before 7 AM or after 9 PM, typically)
- Collectors **cannot** threaten, harass, or use abusive language
- Collectors **cannot** contact your employer (except to verify employment, once)
- Collectors **cannot** arrest you or have you arrested for debt
- You CAN request that all communication be in writing
- You CAN dispute the debt and request validation
- You CAN file complaints with your provincial consumer protection office

### If Debt Is Past the Limitation Period
- The debt still exists — it doesn't disappear
- But the creditor **cannot sue you** or get a court judgment
- It will eventually fall off your credit report (6 years from last activity in most provinces)
- **Warning:** Making any payment or acknowledgment restarts the clock
- Collections agencies may still call — you can tell them the debt is statute-barred and to
  stop contacting you (put it in writing)

## 7. CRA Tax Debt

CRA is the most powerful creditor in Canada. They have collection tools that no other
creditor has. (Source: canada.ca/CRA)

### CRA's Collection Powers (No Court Order Required)
- **Requirements to Pay (RTP):** CRA can send a notice directly to your employer or bank to
  redirect your pay or freeze your accounts. No lawsuit needed.
- **Wage garnishment:** CRA can garnish up to 50% of employment income (more aggressive than
  the 20% limit for other creditors).
- **Bank account freeze:** CRA can freeze your bank account with no warning.
- **Property liens:** CRA can register a lien against your property.
- **Set-off:** CRA can take your tax refund, GST/HST credit, CCB, or other federal payments.

### Payment Arrangements
- Call CRA collections (1-888-863-8657) and propose a monthly payment plan.
- CRA will ask for your income, expenses, and assets to determine affordable payments.
- **Interest continues to accrue** on the balance during the arrangement (~7% on overdue tax,
  2025 — verify, changes quarterly). (Source: canada.ca/understanding-interest)
- CRA generally won't take enforcement action if you have an active payment arrangement and
  are making agreed payments.
- Set up automatic payments to avoid accidentally missing one.

### Taxpayer Relief Provisions (Section 220 of the Income Tax Act)
CRA has discretion to cancel or waive penalties and interest. Apply using Form RC4288.

**Grounds for relief:**
1. **Extraordinary circumstances:** Natural disaster, serious illness, emotional/mental distress,
   civil unrest. The 2025 wildfires and COVID-19 have been recognized.
2. **CRA actions:** CRA error, processing delay, incorrect information given by CRA.
3. **Financial hardship:** Unable to pay due to circumstances beyond your control.

**Important:** CRA can only grant relief for penalties and interest that arose within the last
10 calendar years. They cannot reduce the actual tax owing — only penalties and interest.

### Including CRA Debt in a Consumer Proposal
- Yes, CRA debt CAN be included in a consumer proposal.
- CRA is treated as an unsecured creditor and gets to vote on the proposal.
- CRA often accepts proposals because they get more than they would in a bankruptcy.
- This is one of the strongest reasons to consider a proposal when CRA debt is significant.

### CRA Limitation Period
- CRA has 6 years from the date of the last assessment to collect on tax debt (10 years for
  some categories). But they can restart the clock by taking collection action.
- Unlike provincial limitation periods for consumer debt, CRA's collection powers are broader
  and harder to outlast.

## 8. Student Loan Strategies

### Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP)
If you can't afford your federal student loan payments, RAP can help. (Source: canada.ca/student-aid)

- **Stage 1 (Affordable Payments):** If your income is below the threshold (~$40,000 for a
  single borrower, 2025 — verify), your required payment may be **$0**. Above the threshold,
  payments are capped at **10% of your income**.
- **Stage 2 (Principal Reduction):** After 60 months of RAP payments OR 10 years since you
  finished school (whichever comes first), the government begins paying down your principal
  and remaining interest.
- **Apply through NSLSC.** Must reapply every 6 months.
- **Provincial loans:** Some provinces have their own RAP-equivalent programs (e.g., OSAP
  Repayment Assistance in Ontario).

### Federal Student Loan Interest
- **No interest on federal student loans** since November 2022 (verify if still current).
  This was a major policy change — previously the rate was prime or prime + 2.5%.
- Provincial student loan interest varies by province and may still be charged.
- Student loan interest tax credit: 15% federal non-refundable credit on interest paid
  (federal loans only). Can carry forward unused interest for 5 years.

### The 7-Year Rule for Insolvency
- Student loans **cannot** be included in a consumer proposal or discharged in bankruptcy
  **unless** at least 7 years have passed since you were last a full-time or part-time student.
- After 5 years, you can apply to the court for early discharge on hardship grounds, but this
  is rarely granted and requires proof of good faith effort to repay and genuine hardship.
- If you're close to the 7-year mark, it may be worth waiting before filing.

### Student Loan Rehabilitation
- If your student loan is in default, you can rehabilitate it by making 3 consecutive
  voluntary payments. This restores your eligibility for RAP and other assistance.
- Contact NSLSC to set up a rehabilitation agreement.

## 9. Payday Loans & Predatory Lending Exit

### The Math
- Typical payday loan cost: **$15 per $100 borrowed for 2 weeks.**
- Annual effective interest rate: **~391%** (Source: FCAC)
- On a $500 loan: you repay $575 two weeks later. If you can't, you re-borrow + new fee.
- The cycle: borrow $500 → repay $575 → short $500 again → re-borrow → repeat forever.
  After 6 cycles, you've paid $450 in fees on a $500 loan and still owe $500.

### Provincial Regulations (2025 — verify)
Provinces regulate the maximum cost of payday loans:
- Ontario: $15 per $100
- Alberta: $15 per $100
- BC: $15 per $100
- Saskatchewan: $17 per $100
- Manitoba: $17 per $100
- Nova Scotia: $14 per $100
- Quebec: effectively banned (max 35% annual rate makes payday loans unviable)

### Exit Strategy
1. **Stop the cycle.** This is the hardest part. Do NOT take another payday loan to cover
   the current one. Accept that you'll be short this pay period.
2. **Call 211 Canada.** They connect you to local emergency resources (food banks, rent
   assistance, utility payment programs) to survive the gap.
3. **Contact a non-profit credit counselling agency.** They can negotiate with the payday
   lender and set up a Debt Management Plan (DMP) at 0% interest.
4. **If you have other debts too:** A consumer proposal can include payday loan debt and
   stop the payday lender from collecting.
5. **Build a micro emergency fund.** Even $500 in savings breaks the payday loan cycle.
   Set up automatic $25/pay deposits to a separate account the day you stop re-borrowing.

### Other Predatory Products to Watch For

**Buy-Now-Pay-Later (BNPL):**
- Afterpay, Klarna, PayBright, Zip — split purchases into 4 payments over 6-8 weeks.
- Appears free but: late fees ($10-$25 per missed payment), encourages spending you can't
  afford, multiple BNPL plans create payment juggling.
- Can affect credit score if payments are missed.
- **Red flag:** If someone uses BNPL for groceries or gas, they're in financial distress.

**Rent-to-Own:**
- EasyHome, Aaron's — rent furniture/appliances with option to buy.
- Total cost is typically **2-3x retail price** over the contract.
- Example: a $500 washer costs $1,200-$1,500 through rent-to-own.
- Better alternatives: buy used, ask for payment plan from retailer, credit union loan.

**Car Title Loans:**
- Borrow against your vehicle title at 30-60%+ interest.
- **You lose your car if you default.** This can cascade into job loss (can't get to work).
- Almost never worth the risk. Better to sell the car and buy cheaper.

### Red Flags for Any Lender
- "Guaranteed approval" or "no credit check"
- Requires automatic renewal or re-borrowing
- Won't provide clear written terms before you sign
- Penalties for early repayment
- Pressure to decide immediately ("this offer expires today")

## 10. Debt Negotiation & Settlement

### Negotiating Directly with Creditors
- **When to negotiate:** Before the debt goes to collections. Creditors prefer to work with
  you directly because collection agencies take 25-50% of what they collect.
- **What to ask for:**
  - Reduced interest rate (call and ask — sometimes they'll lower it)
  - Temporary payment reduction (hardship program, usually 3-6 months)
  - Waived fees (late fees, over-limit fees — often waived if you ask)
  - Settlement: lump sum payment at 30-50% of the balance in exchange for closing the account

### Settlement Process
1. **Assess what you can afford** as a lump sum. Creditors rarely accept monthly settlement plans.
2. **Start low.** Offer 20-25% and negotiate up. Many creditors accept 30-50%.
3. **Get the agreement in writing** BEFORE making any payment. The letter should state: the
   amount you'll pay, that the debt will be considered "settled in full," and the account
   will be reported as "settled" (not "unpaid") to credit bureaus.
4. **Pay by certified cheque or money order** — not from your regular bank account (to avoid
   giving the creditor access to garnish).
5. **Keep all documentation forever.**

### Tax Implications of Settled Debt
- In Canada, forgiven debt over $200 may result in a T4A slip from the creditor.
- The forgiven amount reduces your capital loss carry-forwards or other tax attributes first.
- In practice, many creditors don't issue T4As for consumer debt settlements, but technically
  they should. Keep records in case CRA asks.

### Debt Management Plans (DMP)
- Administered by **non-profit credit counselling agencies** (Credit Counselling Canada members).
- How it works: the agency negotiates with your creditors to reduce or eliminate interest.
  You make one monthly payment to the agency, who distributes to creditors.
- Typical DMP: 3-5 years, reduced interest (often 0-5%), full principal repaid.
- Credit impact: noted on credit report as R7 (same as consumer proposal), stays 2-3 years
  after completion.
- **Legitimate agencies charge modest fees** ($50-$75 setup + $25-$50/month). If someone
  charges thousands upfront, they're NOT legitimate.

### Debt Relief Company Red Flags
- Charges large upfront fees before doing any work
- "Guarantees" to settle your debt for pennies on the dollar
- Tells you to stop paying creditors and save money in a separate account (while they collect fees)
- Not a member of Credit Counselling Canada or provincial association
- Aggressively advertises on late-night TV or social media
- **Always verify:** Check the agency with Credit Counselling Canada (creditcounsellingcanada.ca)
  or your provincial consumer protection office.

## 11. Credit Score & Rebuilding

### How Credit Reporting Works in Canada
Two credit bureaus: **Equifax** and **TransUnion.** Both maintain separate credit files on you.
Lenders may report to one or both. (Source: equifax.ca, transunion.ca)

### Credit Score Components
| Factor | Weight | What It Means |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| Payment history | 35% | On-time payments vs late/missed |
| Credit utilization | 30% | How much of available credit you're using |
| Credit history length | 15% | Age of your oldest and average accounts |
| Credit mix | 10% | Variety (credit cards, loans, mortgage) |
| New credit inquiries | 10% | Recent applications for credit |

### Score Ranges (Canada)
- 300-559: Poor
- 560-659: Fair
- 660-724: Good
- 725-759: Very Good
- 760-900: Excellent

### How Long Negatives Stay on Your Report
| Item | Duration | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Late payments | 6 years | From the date of the missed payment |
| Collections | 6 years | From the date of last activity |
| Consumer proposal | 3 years after completion | Or 6 years from filing, whichever is first |
| Bankruptcy (first) | 6-7 years after discharge | Equifax 6 years, TransUnion 6-7 |
| Bankruptcy (second) | 14 years after discharge | Severe and long-lasting |
| Hard inquiries | 3 years | But impact on score fades after ~12 months |

### Checking Your Credit Report (Free)
- **Equifax:** Request free report by mail or online at equifax.ca
- **TransUnion:** Request free report by mail or online at transunion.ca
- Checking your OWN report does NOT affect your score (soft inquiry)
- Review for errors: wrong accounts, incorrect balances, accounts you don't recognize (possible
  fraud). Dispute errors directly with the bureau.

### Rebuilding Strategies (Post-Proposal, Post-Bankruptcy, or Poor Credit)

**Step 1: Secured Credit Card (Month 1)**
- Apply for a secured credit card ($300-$500 deposit = your credit limit).
- Use it for one small recurring purchase (e.g., phone bill) each month.
- Pay the balance in full by the due date. Every month. Without exception.
- This builds payment history (35% of your score).
- Available from: Capital One, Home Trust, Refresh Financial, many credit unions.

**Step 2: Keep Utilization Low (Ongoing)**
- Use less than 30% of your available credit at any time.
- Ideally under 10% for maximum score benefit.
- If your limit is $500, keep the balance under $150 at all times.

**Step 3: Don't Apply for Multiple Products (First 12 months)**
- Each application creates a hard inquiry (-5 to -10 points each).
- Space out applications by at least 6 months.

**Step 4: Credit-Builder Loan (Month 6-12)**
- Some credit unions offer small loans specifically for building credit.
- You borrow $500-$1,000, it goes into a locked savings account, and you make monthly
  payments. When fully paid, you get the money.
- This adds a different type of credit to your mix (10% of score).

**Step 5: Become an Authorized User (If Possible)**
- Ask a trusted family member to add you as an authorized user on their credit card.
- Their positive payment history can boost your score.
- You don't need to use or even hold the card.

### Realistic Timeline
- **3-6 months:** Score starts moving up from consistent on-time payments
- **12 months:** Meaningful improvement (50-100+ points possible)
- **24 months:** Eligible for most standard credit cards and reasonable loan rates
- **36 months:** After consumer proposal falls off, significant jump possible
- **Full recovery (proposal):** 4-5 years to a "good" score range
- **Full recovery (bankruptcy):** 7-8 years to a "good" score range

## 12. Debt & Mental Health / Behavioral Coaching

### Normalize the Situation
- **1.6 million Canadians** are in problem debt. The person in front of you is not alone.
- Debt is not a moral failing. It's usually the result of circumstances: job loss, illness,
  divorce, predatory lending, lack of financial education, or simple bad luck.
- The fact that they're talking about it is a sign of strength, not weakness.

### Common Emotional Patterns

**Debt shame and avoidance:**
- Not opening mail, ignoring phone calls, avoiding bank accounts.
- The ostrich effect — if I don't look, it's not real.
- Address this directly but gently: "I know looking at these numbers is hard. But the anxiety
  of not knowing is usually worse than the reality. Let's look together."

**The stress-debt cycle:**
- Financial stress → poor sleep, anxiety, depression → impaired decision-making → impulse
  spending for temporary relief → more debt → more stress.
- Breaking the cycle requires addressing BOTH the financial and emotional sides.
- Encourage: therapy (EAP is free through most employers), physical activity, social connection.

**"I'll never get out":**
- This is rarely true. Show the math.
- Even $4,200 in CC debt at 21% is payoff-able in 14 months at $321/month.
- Even $50,000 in total debt has a path — consumer proposal at $250/month for 5 years.
- Calculate the exact debt-free date and show them. A date makes it feel finite.

### Couples and Debt
- One partner high-debt-tolerant ("it'll work out"), the other anxious ("we're drowning").
- Financial secrecy (hidden credit cards, undisclosed debts) is one of the top causes of
  relationship breakdown. Create a safe space for disclosure.
- Consider separate "personal spending" accounts so each person has autonomy, while joint
  account handles shared obligations.
- Monthly "money dates" — 30 minutes to review finances together. Not during fights.

### When Debt Signals a Deeper Issue
- **Compulsive spending:** Shopping as emotional regulation. Recommend therapy.
- **Gambling:** Losses masked as "I don't know where the money went." Ask directly.
- **Addiction:** Substance use funded by debt. Financial plan alone won't help.
- **Domestic abuse:** One partner controlling finances, running up debt in other's name.
  Sensitive — connect to provincial resources.
- In ALL these cases: recommend professional support first, financial planning second.
  EAP is free through most employers. 211 Canada connects to local mental health resources.

### Behavioral Strategies That Work
1. **Automate everything.** Debt payments, savings, bill payments — all automatic on payday.
   Willpower is unreliable. Systems aren't.
2. **The envelope method.** For variable spending (groceries, fun money), withdraw cash and
   use envelopes. When the envelope is empty, you're done for the month.
3. **The 48-hour rule.** No non-essential purchase over $50 without waiting 48 hours.
   Eliminates ~60% of impulse buys.
4. **Progress visibility.** Track the debt balance weekly. Watch it go down. This is
   motivating and breaks the avoidance pattern.
5. **Celebrate milestones.** Paid off a credit card? That's huge. Acknowledge it.
   $20 dinner out is not "wasting money" — it's reinforcing positive behavior.
6. **One thing at a time.** Don't try to overhaul everything at once. Pick the single most
   impactful action and do that first. Then the next one.

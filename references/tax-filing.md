taxes, needs help with deductions/credits, wants to optimize their return as a couple, or
has questions about CRA My Account, penalties, or disputes.

All amounts, rates, and deadlines should be verified via web search — they change annually.
Key sources: canada.ca (CRA), taxtips.ca, NETFILE provider list. Full URLs in Section 15.

## 2. Key Deadlines

### 2026 Tax Filing Season (for 2025 Tax Year — verify annually)

| Deadline | Who | What | Penalty if Missed |
|----------|-----|------|------------------|
| **March 2, 2026** | Everyone with RRSP room | RRSP contribution deadline (for 2025 deduction) | No penalty, just miss the deduction for 2025 |
| **April 30, 2026** | Most individuals | File 2025 T1 return + pay any balance owing | 5% of balance + 1%/month (up to 12 months) |
| **April 30, 2026** | Self-employed | **Payment deadline** (even though filing deadline is June 15) | Interest from May 1 at ~7% compounded daily |
| **June 15, 2026** | Self-employed & their spouses | File 2025 T1 return | Late filing penalty if balance owing |
| **Quarterly** | Self-employed (if >$3K owing) | Installment payments (Mar 15, Jun 15, Sep 15, Dec 15) | Interest on deficient installments |

**Critical self-employed trap:** You can FILE by June 15, but you must PAY by April 30.
If you owe $10,000 and file on June 15, you've avoided the late filing penalty but CRA
charges interest from May 1 (46 days of interest at ~7% = ~$88).

### RRSP Deadline Strategy
- RRSP contributions made in the first 60 days of the year (Jan 1 - March 1/2) can be
  deducted on EITHER the current year's return OR the prior year's return.
- **Strategy:** If your income was higher last year, deduct against last year. If higher this
  year, deduct against this year. You choose on your return.

(Source: canada.ca/important-dates)

## 3. Documents & T-Slips Checklist

Gather these BEFORE you start filing. Most T-slips are available on CRA My Account by
mid-March (Auto-fill feature imports them directly into your tax software).

### Everyone Needs
- [ ] T4 — Employment income (from each employer)
- [ ] T4A — Pension, retirement, annuity income, scholarships, self-employment commissions
- [ ] T5 — Investment income (interest, dividends from bank/brokerage)
- [ ] RRSP contribution receipts (from financial institution)
- [ ] Notice of Assessment from prior year (confirms RRSP room, balances)
- [ ] Social Insurance Number (SIN) for yourself, spouse, dependents

### If Applicable
- [ ] T4E — Employment Insurance benefits
- [ ] T4RSP — RRSP income (withdrawals)
- [ ] T4RIF — RRIF income
- [ ] T3 — Trust income (from mutual funds, REITs in non-registered)
- [ ] T5008 — Securities transactions (from brokerage — capital gains/losses)
- [ ] T4A(OAS) — Old Age Security
- [ ] T4A(P) — Canada Pension Plan
- [ ] T2202 — Tuition (from educational institution)
- [ ] RC62 — Universal Child Care Benefit (if applicable)
- [ ] T5007 — Workers' compensation or social assistance

### Receipts to Gather
- [ ] Medical expense receipts (prescriptions, dental, vision, paramedical, travel for medical)
- [ ] Charitable donation receipts (official receipts with registration number)
- [ ] Childcare receipts (daycare, camp, nanny — need provider's SIN or business number)
- [ ] Moving expense receipts (if moved 40+ km closer to work/school)
- [ ] Professional/union dues receipts
- [ ] RESP contribution receipts
- [ ] Political contribution receipts
- [ ] Home office expenses (if T2200 from employer, or self-employed)
- [ ] Vehicle expense log (if claiming business/employment vehicle use)
- [ ] Rental income and expense records (if landlord)
- [ ] Self-employment income and expense records

### Pro Tip
**Use CRA's Auto-fill My Return** feature in your tax software. It imports most T-slips
directly from CRA's records, reducing errors and saving time. Available through all major
NETFILE-certified software.

## 5. CRA My Account & Auto-Fill

### What CRA My Account Provides
- **RRSP contribution room** — the definitive number (don't guess)
- **TFSA contribution room** — verify before contributing
- **Notice of Assessment** — view online, download PDF
- **T-slips** — most slips available by mid-March (Auto-fill)
- **Benefit payment information** — CCB, GST/HST credit, OTB amounts
- **Installment payment reminders** — suggested amounts and due dates
- **Tax return status** — track processing after filing
- **Reassessment history** — view any changes CRA made
- **Direct deposit setup** — faster refunds

### Auto-Fill My Return
Most NETFILE-certified software connects to CRA My Account and imports:
- All T-slips (T4, T5, T3, T4E, T4A, etc.)
- RRSP contribution information
- Home Buyers' Plan and Lifelong Learning Plan repayment status
- Tuition carry-forward amounts

**Always verify** the auto-filled amounts against your own records. CRA's data is usually
correct but occasionally slips are missing or amounts differ from what you received.

### Notice of Assessment (NOA)
After CRA processes your return, you receive a NOA. **Review it carefully:**
- [ ] Does the assessed income match what you reported?
- [ ] Were all credits and deductions accepted?
- [ ] Is the refund or balance owing what you expected?
- [ ] Is the RRSP room for next year correct?
- [ ] Are there any notes or flags from CRA?

**If something is wrong:** File a T1 Adjustment Request (T1-ADJ) through CRA My Account or
by mail. You have **10 years** to request an adjustment (extended from the previous 4-year
window for most situations — verify).

(Source: canada.ca/my-account)

| Eligible dependent amount | Up to ~$16,129 | Single parent with dependent child | 30400 |

### Refundable Credits (Paid to You Even with No Tax Owing)

| Credit | Max Amount | Who Qualifies |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Canada Workers Benefit (CWB) | ~$1,518 (single), ~$2,616 (family) | Low-income workers |
| GST/HST Credit | ~$519 (single), ~$680 (couple) quarterly | Income-tested |
| Climate Action Incentive | Varies by province and family size | All tax filers |

### Commonly Missed Deductions

**Medical Expenses (Line 33099):**
People often don't realize these qualify:
- Prescription medications (not over-the-counter)
- Dental work (cleanings, fillings, orthodontics)
- Vision (glasses, contact lenses, eye exams)
- Paramedical (physiotherapy, chiropractic, massage therapy if prescribed)
- Mental health (psychologist, psychiatrist, therapy if prescribed)
- Medical travel (40+ km for treatment — mileage, meals, accommodation)
- Mobility aids, hearing aids, wheelchairs
- Service animals
- In-vitro fertilization and surrogacy expenses
- Premiums for private health insurance plans
- **Claim for any 12-month period ending in the tax year** (not just calendar year)

**Charitable Donations (Line 34900):**
- Carry forward unused donations for up to 5 years
- Donate publicly-traded securities directly: **no capital gains tax** on the donation,
  full tax receipt for FMV
- First-time donor super credit may still apply (verify)
- Political contributions have a separate credit (75% on first $400)

**Moving Expenses (Line 21900):**
- Must move 40+ km closer to new work location or school
- Qualifies: travel costs, temporary living (up to 15 days), transportation of belongings,
  lease cancellation, legal fees on old/new home sale/purchase, utility hookups/disconnects
- Claimed against income earned at the new location

(Source: canada.ca/deductions-credits, taxtips.ca)

## 7. Couples Optimization (Who Claims What)

Filing separately is mandatory in Canada (no joint returns), but strategic allocation of
claims between spouses can save thousands.

### Medical Expenses
- **Claim on the LOWER-income spouse's return** (usually better)
- Why: the 3% of net income threshold is lower, so more expenses exceed it
- Exception: if the lower-income spouse has very low income and no tax owing, claim on the
  higher-income spouse instead

### Charitable Donations
- **Combine and claim on the HIGHER-income spouse's return**
- Why: the 29% rate (or 33% above $253K) applies to amounts over $200. Combining both
  spouses' donations gets past the $200 threshold faster and more is taxed at the higher rate.
- **Carry forward strategy:** If both incomes are moderate, carry donations forward and
  claim in a high-income year for maximum benefit.

### Childcare Expenses
- **Must be claimed by the LOWER-income spouse** (CRA requirement, not a choice)
- Exceptions: lower-income spouse is a student, in prison, or unable to care for the child
- Max: $8,000/child under 7, $5,000/child 7-16 (2025 — verify)

### Pension Income Splitting (Form T1032)
- Can split up to **50% of eligible pension income** with spouse
- Eligible: RRIF withdrawals, life annuity from pension plan, some RPP income
- NOT eligible until age 65: RRSP withdrawals, non-life-annuity pension
- **Both spouses must agree** and file Form T1032
- Best when one spouse is in a significantly higher bracket than the other

### Spousal RRSP Coordination
- Higher-income spouse contributes to spousal RRSP → gets the deduction at higher marginal rate
- Spouse eventually withdraws at lower marginal rate (after 3-year attribution period)
- Coordinate with pension splitting for maximum benefit in retirement

### TFSA Contributions
- Give money to your spouse to contribute to their TFSA — **no attribution rules**
- The growth and withdrawals are in their name, tax-free
- This is one of the simplest and most effective income-splitting strategies

### CPP Pension Sharing
- Both spouses must be 60+ and receiving CPP
- Can share CPP retirement pension based on years of cohabitation
- Apply to Service Canada — reduces tax if one spouse has higher CPP

### Basic Strategy Summary
| Claim | Optimal Spouse | Why |
|-------|---------------|-----|
| Medical expenses | Lower income (usually) | Lower 3% threshold |
| Charitable donations | Higher income | Higher credit rate |
| Childcare | Lower income | CRA requirement |
| Pension splitting | Split to equalize | Reduce higher bracket |
| Spousal amount | Higher income | Credit if spouse earns < BPA |
| Eligible dependent | Either (if separated/single parent) | One parent claims |

(Source: canada.ca, taxtips.ca/couples)

## 8. Investment Income Reporting

### Types of Investment Income & How They're Taxed

| Type | Inclusion Rate | Tax Treatment | Reported On |
|------|---------------|--------------|-------------|
| Interest | 100% | Fully taxable at marginal rate | T5, T3 slip |
| Eligible dividends | 138% (gross-up) then credit | Effective ~20-30% | T5, T3 slip |
| Non-eligible dividends | 115% (gross-up) then credit | Effective ~30-45% | T5, T3 slip |
| Capital gains | 50% | Half of gain is taxable | Schedule 3 |
| Return of capital | 0% (but reduces ACB) | Tax-free until ACB reaches $0 | T3 slip |

### Reporting Capital Gains/Losses (Schedule 3)
1. Calculate: **Sale proceeds - ACB - selling costs = capital gain (or loss)**
2. Report on Schedule 3 of your T1 return
3. 50% of net capital gains are included in income (Line 12700)
4. Net capital losses can be:
   - Applied against gains in the **current year**
   - Carried back **3 years** (file Form T1A)
   - Carried forward **indefinitely**

### Principal Residence Sale
- Must report the sale on your tax return (Schedule 3) even though the gain is exempt
- Designate using Form T2091 (if not your only property)
- Failure to report can result in the exemption being denied

### Foreign Income
- Report ALL foreign income in Canadian dollars (use Bank of Canada exchange rate for the
  transaction date, or average annual rate)
- Claim Foreign Tax Credit (Form T2209) for foreign taxes paid
- **T1135:** If you own foreign property costing more than $100,000 CAD at any time during
  the year, you must file Form T1135 (Foreign Income Verification Statement). Penalty for
  not filing: $25/day, up to $2,500.

(Source: canada.ca/investment-income, Schedule 3 guide)

## 9. Employment Expenses (T2200)

### Who Can Claim
Employees who are **required by their employer** to pay for expenses as a condition of
employment. Your employer must sign **Form T2200** (Declaration of Conditions of Employment)
confirming this.

### Claimable Employment Expenses
- **Home office:** Same two methods as self-employed (detailed % or flat $2/day).
  Must be required to work from home (T2200 confirms this).
- **Vehicle:** If required to use personal vehicle for work (not commuting).
  Mileage log required. CRA per-km rates: $0.72 first 5,000 km, $0.66 after (2025 — verify).
- **Supplies:** Office supplies consumed in employment duties.
- **Professional dues:** Required union dues, professional association fees.
- **Meals:** If required to be away from employer's place of business for 12+ hours. 50% deductible.

### What You CANNOT Claim
- Commuting costs (home to office)
- Clothing (unless uniform required by employer)
- Tools over $500 (special rules for tradespeople — separate calculation)
- Parking at employer's office
- Employment expenses CANNOT create a loss (reduce employment income to zero only)

### Home Office for Employees
- **Detailed method:** Percentage of home × eligible expenses (rent, utilities, internet,
  maintenance). Requires T2200.
- **Flat rate:** $2/day worked from home, max $500/year. No T2200 needed. No receipts needed.
- **Choice:** Most people should calculate both and choose the higher amount.

(Source: canada.ca/employment-expenses, T2200 guide)

## 10. Students & Education

### Tuition Tax Credit
- 15% federal non-refundable credit on eligible tuition fees
- Reported on Form T2202 from your educational institution
- **Carry forward indefinitely** — use it when you have income to offset
- Can transfer up to $5,000 to a parent, grandparent, or spouse (minus the amount you need
  to reduce your own tax to zero) — **verify if transfer is still available**
- Provincial tuition credits vary (some provinces eliminated theirs)

### Student Loan Interest
- 15% federal non-refundable credit on interest paid on **government student loans** only
  (not private loans, not LOC)
- Can carry forward unused interest for **5 years**
- **No interest on federal student loans** since November 2022 (verify if still current) —
  this means no credit to claim on federal loans currently

### Scholarships, Bursaries & Grants
- Generally **tax-free** if you're enrolled in a qualifying program
- Post-doctoral fellowship income is taxable
- Research grants may be partially taxable (amounts for personal expenses)

### Moving Expenses for School
- Same 40+ km rule as employment moves
- Can only be claimed against scholarship/bursary/grant income earned at the new location
- If moving expenses exceed scholarship income, carry forward to the next year

### Education-Related Tax Benefits
- **Canada Training Credit:** Accumulated at $250/year (if income $10K-$150K, age 26-65).
  Can offset up to 50% of eligible training fees. Check balance on CRA My Account.
- **RESP withdrawals:** Educational Assistance Payments (EAPs) are taxable income to the
  STUDENT (usually at a very low rate since students have low income).
- **Textbook and education amounts:** Eliminated federally. Some provinces still offer.

### New Immigrants Filing First Return
- Report **worldwide income** from the date you became a Canadian resident
- Foreign income earned before arriving is NOT taxable in Canada
- Claim Foreign Tax Credit for any foreign taxes paid on income also taxable in Canada
- RRSP room starts accumulating from year of arrival (based on Canadian earned income)
- TFSA room: $7,000/year from the year you became a resident AND turned 18
- File Form T1135 if foreign property > $100K at any point during the year
- **Apply for all benefits:** CCB, GST/HST credit, provincial benefits — must file a return
  to receive them, even if income is zero

(Source: canada.ca/students, T2202 guide)

## 11. Seniors & Retirees

### Age Amount (Line 30100)
- Non-refundable credit for Canadians **65 and older**
- Maximum: ~$8,790 (2025 — verify). Reduced when net income exceeds ~$44,325.
  Fully clawed back at ~$102,835.
- 15% federal credit = up to ~$1,319 in federal tax savings

### Pension Income Amount (Line 31400)
- Non-refundable credit on up to $2,000 of eligible pension income
- Federal credit: 15% × $2,000 = $300
- **Eligible pension income (age 65+):** RRIF withdrawals, life annuity from RPP, DPSP
- **Under 65:** Only life annuity from RPP qualifies (not RRSP/RRIF)
- **Strategy:** If you're 65+ and have RRSP, convert a small portion to RRIF and withdraw
  $2,000 to claim this credit. The $300 credit often exceeds the tax on the $2,000 withdrawal.

### Pension Income Splitting
- See Section 7 (Couples Optimization) — split up to 50% via Form T1032
- **Most powerful for seniors** with unequal pension/RRIF income
- Can save $3,000-$10,000+/year in tax for couples with income imbalance

### OAS Clawback Planning
- OAS recovery tax starts at ~$93,454 net income (2025 — verify)
- Fully clawed back at ~$151,668
- **Strategies to minimize:**
  - Withdraw from TFSA (not income) when near the threshold
  - Split pension income with spouse
  - Defer non-essential RRIF withdrawals to low-income years
  - Use non-registered capital gains strategically (only 50% included)
  - Consider deferring OAS to age 70 (36% increase) if other income covers the gap

### GIS (Guaranteed Income Supplement)
- For low-income seniors (individual income below ~$21,768, 2025 — verify)
- Up to ~$1,072/month (2025 — verify)
- **TFSA withdrawals do NOT count as income** for GIS purposes
- RRIF withdrawals DO count — minimize them if GIS-eligible
- **Must file a tax return every year** to maintain GIS eligibility

### RRIF Conversion & Minimum Withdrawals
- RRSP must convert to RRIF by December 31 of the year you turn 71
- Minimum withdrawal starts the following year (increases with age)
- You can base minimum on your spouse's age if younger (lower minimums)
- **Withholding tax:** Applied to RRIF withdrawals exceeding the minimum:
  - 10% on amounts up to $5,000
  - 20% on $5,001-$15,000
  - 30% on amounts over $15,000
  - (Quebec: additional provincial withholding)

(Source: canada.ca/seniors, taxtips.ca/seniors)

## 12. Common Filing Errors

### Errors That Cost You Money
1. **Not claiming medical expenses.** People forget that dental, prescriptions, therapy,
   medical travel, and health insurance premiums qualify.
2. **Not combining spousal charitable donations.** Claim on the higher-income spouse's return
   to get the 29% rate on more of the donation.
3. **Forgetting carry-forward amounts.** Capital losses, tuition credits, charitable donations,
   RRSP deductions — all can be carried forward. Check CRA My Account for balances.
4. **Claiming childcare on the wrong spouse.** Must be the lower-income spouse (CRA will
   reassess if you file it on the higher-income spouse).
5. **Not claiming the pension income amount.** If 65+ with RRSP, convert a small amount to
   RRIF and withdraw $2,000 just to claim the $300 credit.
6. **Missing the RRSP deadline.** Contributions after March 1/2 can only be claimed on the
   current year, not carried back.
7. **Not reporting principal residence sale.** CRA requires reporting even though the gain is
   exempt. Failing to report can result in losing the exemption.
8. **Forgetting T1135 for foreign property.** $25/day penalty for not filing. Easy to miss
   if you have US investments exceeding $100K CAD.

### Errors That Trigger CRA Attention
1. **Inconsistent income.** Reported income doesn't match T-slips on file.
2. **Large business losses year after year.** CRA may question if it's a real business.
3. **Home office > 30-40% of home.** Higher percentages trigger audits.
4. **Vehicle expenses without a mileage log.** CRA will deny the entire claim.
5. **Charitable donations disproportionate to income.** Legitimate large donations are fine
   — just keep the receipts.
6. **Not reporting crypto transactions.** Platforms now report to CRA. Every trade is taxable.

## 13. Penalties, Disputes & Voluntary Disclosure

### Late Filing Penalty
- **5% of balance owing** + **1% per month** late, up to **12 months** = maximum 17%
- If you've been penalized before (within 3 years), doubles to: 10% + 2%/month up to 20
  months = maximum 50%
- **No penalty if you don't owe money.** If you're getting a refund, there's no late penalty
  (but you delay your refund and may delay benefits like CCB/GST credit).

### Late Payment Interest
- Compound daily interest starting May 1 (or day after due date)
- Rate: prescribed rate + 4% = currently ~7% (2025 — verify, changes quarterly)
- Applies to ANY unpaid balance, including installment deficiencies

### How to Dispute an Assessment

**Step 1: Informal review**
- Call CRA or use My Account to ask about the discrepancy
- Sometimes it's a simple error that CRA can correct immediately

**Step 2: T1 Adjustment Request**
- File through CRA My Account (ReFILE) or by mail (Form T1-ADJ)
- Include supporting documentation
- CRA has up to 12 months to process

**Step 3: Notice of Objection**
- If you disagree with CRA's (re)assessment, file within **90 days** of the assessment date
- Use Form T400A or file through My Account
- CRA Appeals Division reviews independently of the auditor who made the original assessment
- Processing: 6-18 months (backlog varies)

**Step 4: Tax Court of Canada**
- If objection is denied, you can appeal to Tax Court within 90 days
- For amounts under $25,000: informal procedure (no lawyer needed)
- For larger amounts: general procedure (lawyer recommended)

### Voluntary Disclosure Program (VDP)
If you forgot to report income, made errors, or didn't file returns from prior years, the
VDP allows you to come forward **before CRA contacts you** in exchange for reduced penalties.

- **Benefits:** Penalties waived or reduced, potential prosecution avoided
- **Requirements:** Disclosure must be voluntary (CRA hasn't already contacted you about it),
  complete (disclose everything), and include payment of taxes owing + interest
- **Application:** Form RC199 through CRA
- **Lookback:** Typically covers the last 3 years, but can extend to 10 years for repeated
  non-compliance
- **When to use:** Unreported foreign income, missed cryptocurrency gains, undisclosed bank
  accounts, unfiled returns from past years

(Source: canada.ca/vdp, canada.ca/objections)

## 14. Tax-Free Income (What NOT to Report)

These amounts are NOT taxable and do NOT need to be reported on your return:

| Income Type | Taxable? | Notes |
|-------------|---------|-------|
| TFSA withdrawals | No | Not income, not reported |
| Canada Child Benefit (CCB) | No | Tax-free |
| GST/HST Credit | No | Tax-free |
| Lottery/gambling winnings | No | Unless professional gambler |
| Life insurance death benefit | No | Tax-free to beneficiary |
| Personal injury settlement | No | If for bodily injury/illness |
| Gifts from family/friends | No | No gift tax in Canada |
| Inheritance (cash) | No | Estate pays tax, not heir |
| Workers' compensation | No | But may reduce other deductions |
| Social assistance | No | But may be included in net income for clawback calculations |
| RDSP withdrawals (grant/bond portion) | No | Tax-free |
| War veteran pensions | No | Tax-free |
| RCMP pension (certain portions) | Varies | Check specific provisions |

**Tricky items:**
- **EI benefits:** TAXABLE (T4E issued)
- **CPP/OAS:** TAXABLE (T4A(P) / T4A(OAS) issued)
- **RRSP withdrawals:** TAXABLE (T4RSP issued)
- **Scholarship income:** Usually tax-free if enrolled in qualifying program, but check
- **Crypto gains:** TAXABLE (every trade, swap, or conversion is a taxable event)

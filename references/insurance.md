# Insurance — Deep-Dive for Canadians

Read this reference when a user has insurance questions, gaps have been flagged in the plan,
they're shopping for insurance, or when a life event triggers an insurance review (baby,
marriage, divorce, job loss, death of spouse, home purchase).

All cost estimates should be verified via web search before presenting — premiums change
based on age, health, and market conditions. Key sources: CLHIA, Insurance Bureau of Canada,
FCAC, PolicyAdvisor, PolicyMe, provincial regulators. Full URLs in Section 13.

## 1. When to Use This Reference

Load this file when the user:
- Has insurance gaps flagged in their plan (life, disability, critical illness)
- Is shopping for or comparing insurance products
- Had a life event that triggers insurance review (baby, marriage, divorce, job change, home purchase)
- Asks about a specific insurance type or whether they need it
- Is self-employed and needs to replace employer benefits
- Is approaching retirement and employer coverage is ending
- Asks "how much life insurance do I need?" or "do I need disability insurance?"
- Has been told by a bank advisor or agent to buy a specific product (especially whole life or
  universal life — see Section 12 for red flags)

## 2. Insurance Needs Analysis (DIME Method)

Use DIME to calculate life insurance needs. Walk through each component with the user's
actual numbers — don't just give them a "10-12x income" rule of thumb.

### D — Debt
Add up ALL debts that would need to be paid off:
- Mortgage balance
- Car loans
- Student loans
- Credit card balances
- Lines of credit
- Any other debts

### I — Income Replacement
How many years of income does the surviving family need?
- **Young children:** Replace income until youngest child is 18-22 (through post-secondary)
- **No children:** Replace income for 5-10 years (adjustment period for surviving spouse)
- **Factor in:** CPP survivor pension, OAS (if 65+), surviving spouse's own income, workplace
  pension. Subtract these from the replacement amount needed.
- **Replacement rate:** 60-80% of the deceased's after-tax income (family expenses decrease
  somewhat with one fewer person, but childcare costs may increase)

**Formula:** Annual after-tax income × replacement rate × years needed - (CPP survivor pension
× years + other income sources × years) = income replacement need

### M — Mortgage
Include the full mortgage balance (if not already counted in Debt above). Many people want
the mortgage fully paid off to ensure the family keeps the home.

### E — Education
If children will attend post-secondary:
- Estimate $20,000-$30,000/year for 4 years per child (2026, Canadian university — verify)
- Subtract existing RESP balance and projected CESG
- Adjust for inflation if children are young

### Additional Costs
- **Final expenses:** Funeral, burial/cremation, legal fees: $10,000-$15,000
- **Childcare:** If the deceased was the primary caregiver, add childcare costs × years until
  youngest is in school: $12,000-$30,000/year
- **Emergency buffer:** 6 months of household expenses as a cushion

### Total Need
```
Debts + Income Replacement + Mortgage + Education + Final Expenses + Childcare + Buffer
- Existing life insurance (employer + personal)
- Existing savings/investments that could be used
= Insurance gap to fill
```

### Example (Marcus & Sarah Chen from demo)
Marcus dies:
- Debts: $31,500 (car + CC + student loan)
- Income replacement: $5,420/month × 80% × 15 years = ~$779,000 (minus CPP survivor ~$500/month × 15yr = $90,000) = ~$689,000
- Mortgage: $412,000
- Education: 2 kids × $100,000 = $200,000 - $8,700 RESP = $191,300
- Final expenses: $12,000
- Childcare: not needed (Sarah already works, kids in care)
- **Total need: ~$1,335,800**
- Existing: $184,000 (employer life)
- **Gap: ~$1,152,000** → needs ~$1.15M in additional coverage

## 3. Term Life Insurance

The most common, most affordable, and usually the right choice for families.

### How It Works
- Pay a fixed premium for a set term (10, 20, or 30 years).
- If you die during the term, beneficiaries receive the death benefit tax-free.
- If you outlive the term, coverage ends (no payout). You can renew at a much higher rate or
  convert to permanent insurance (if the policy has a conversion option).

### Term Length Selection
| Term | Best For | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| 10-year | Short-term need (debt, bridge gap) | Cheapest, but need re-evaluation soon |
| 20-year | Young families, mortgage period | Most popular. Covers kids through university. |
| 30-year | Long-term need, young parents | More expensive but locks in rate for longer |

**Rule of thumb:** Match the term to your longest financial obligation (usually mortgage term
or years until kids are independent).

### Cost Benchmarks (2026 — verify via web search)
Approximate monthly premiums for $500,000 coverage, 20-year term, non-smoker, standard health:

| Age | Male | Female |
|-----|------|--------|
| 25 | $20-25 | $17-22 |
| 30 | $22-28 | $19-24 |
| 35 | $28-36 | $24-30 |
| 40 | $42-55 | $36-45 |
| 45 | $65-80 | $52-65 |
| 50 | $95-130 | $75-100 |
| 55 | $155-200 | $115-155 |

(Source: PolicyMe, PolicyAdvisor, Sun Life — verify current rates)

**Key cost factors:**
- Age (biggest factor — buy young)
- Smoker status (2-3x more expensive)
- Health history (medical conditions increase premiums)
- Occupation (high-risk jobs cost more)
- Gender (females typically 15-25% less)
- Coverage amount (not linear — $1M is less than 2x the cost of $500K)

### Renewable vs Convertible
- **Renewable:** Can renew at end of term without medical exam, but at significantly higher rates
  (rates based on your age at renewal, not original age). Useful as a backup.
- **Convertible:** Can convert some or all of the term policy to permanent insurance without
  medical exam. This is valuable if your health deteriorates. Check conversion deadline (often
  age 65 or 70, or a specific date — varies by insurer).
- **Get both** if possible. Most quality term policies include both features.

### Underwriting Process
1. **Application:** Health questions, lifestyle, occupation, income, existing coverage.
2. **Medical exam:** For larger policies ($500K+), insurer may require paramedical exam (blood,
   urine, blood pressure, measurements). Takes 20-30 minutes, done at your home or office.
3. **Non-medical policies:** Available for smaller amounts (up to $100K-$500K depending on age
   and insurer). No exam, but higher premiums. Good for people with health concerns who want
   quick coverage.
4. **Timeline:** Traditional underwriting: 4-8 weeks. Simplified/accelerated: 1-2 weeks.
   Some digital-first insurers (PolicyMe, Sun Life Go): same-day to a few days.
5. **Temporary coverage:** Many insurers provide temporary insurance from the date of
   application (conditional receipt). Ask about this.

### Shopping Tips
- Get quotes from at least 3 sources (online comparison + broker + direct insurer)
- Compare apples to apples: same coverage amount, same term, same features
- Check conversion options — a cheap policy without conversion rights may cost more long-term
- Check the insurer's financial strength rating (AM Best, DBRS) — you need them to be around
  in 20+ years to pay the claim
- Independent brokers can shop multiple insurers. Bank insurance advisors can only sell their
  bank's products.

(Source: PolicyMe, PolicyAdvisor, Sun Life, Blue Cross Life, CLHIA)

## 4. Permanent Life Insurance

### When Permanent Life Insurance Makes Sense
- **Estate planning:** Large estate with significant deemed disposition tax at death (need
  insurance to pay the tax bill so heirs don't have to sell assets)
- **Business succession:** Buy-sell agreements funded by insurance, key person coverage
- **High net worth:** All registered accounts maxed, looking for tax-advantaged growth
- **Lifelong dependents:** Adult child with disability who needs lifetime support
- **Charitable giving:** Insurance as a charitable bequest

### When It Does NOT Make Sense (Most Families)
- Young family with debt, mortgage, and limited savings → **term life is almost always better**
- The cost difference between term and permanent is enormous. A 35-year-old might pay $35/month
  for $500K term vs $300+/month for $500K whole life. The difference ($265/month) invested in
  a TFSA at 7% for 30 years = ~$318,000. This is the "buy term and invest the difference"
  strategy — almost always wins for families still building wealth.

### Types of Permanent Insurance
| Type | How It Works | Best For |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Whole Life | Fixed premium, guaranteed death benefit, cash value grows at guaranteed rate | Conservative, estate planning |
| Universal Life (UL) | Flexible premiums, death benefit can adjust, cash value invested (you choose investments) | More control, higher risk tolerance |
| Term-to-100 | Level premium for life, no cash value, guaranteed coverage | Pure lifelong coverage at lower cost than whole/UL |

### Cash Value
- Whole life and UL policies accumulate cash value over time.
- You can borrow against it (policy loan) or surrender the policy for its cash value.
- Cash value grows tax-deferred inside the policy.
- On surrender, the gain (cash value - ACB) is taxable.
- **Don't buy permanent insurance FOR the cash value.** The returns are typically lower than
  what you'd earn investing independently. Buy it for the lifelong death benefit.

### Red Flags (Products to Avoid)
- Bank advisor pushing whole life or universal life to a young family with debt → **they earn
  higher commissions on permanent products.** Ask: "Would term insurance meet my needs?"
- "Investment-linked" insurance products with high MERs (2-4%) → you're paying for insurance
  AND bad investing at the same time
- Policies where you can't clearly understand what you're paying for
- Any advisor who won't show you a term vs permanent comparison

(Source: CLHIA, FCAC, PolicyAdvisor)

## 5. Disability Insurance

Disability is the most underinsured risk in Canada. You're far more likely to be disabled
than to die during your working years.

### The Statistics
- A 30-year-old has a **~25% chance** of being disabled for 3+ months before age 65
- 1 in 3 Canadians will experience a disability lasting 90+ days during their career
- The average long-term disability claim lasts **2.5 years**
- Most people insure their car, home, and life — but not their ability to earn income

### Key Terms & Decisions

**Definition of Disability:**
| Definition | What It Means | Cost | Recommendation |
|-----------|--------------|------|----------------|
| Own-occupation | Can't do YOUR specific job | Most expensive | Best if specialized career |
| Modified any-occupation | Can't do a job suited to your education/training | Mid-range | Good balance for most |
| Any-occupation | Can't do ANY job | Cheapest | Avoid if possible — too restrictive |

**Benefit Period:**
| Period | What It Means | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| 2 years | Benefits stop after 2 years | Cheapest |
| 5 years | Benefits stop after 5 years | Moderate |
| To age 65 | Benefits until retirement | Most expensive — **recommended** |

**Elimination Period (waiting period before benefits start):**
| Period | What It Means | Cost |
|--------|--------------|------|
| 30 days | Benefits start after 1 month | Most expensive |
| 60 days | Benefits start after 2 months | Moderate |
| 90 days | Benefits start after 3 months | **Best value — recommended** |
| 120 days | Benefits start after 4 months | Cheapest |

**Most common recommendation:** Own-occupation (or modified any-occ), to age 65 benefit period,
90-day elimination period. This balances cost and coverage.

### Cost Estimates (2026 — verify)
Individual disability insurance typically costs **1-3% of annual income.**
- $65,000 income → $650-$1,950/year ($54-$163/month)
- $100,000 income → $1,000-$3,000/year ($83-$250/month)

Factors: age, occupation risk class, benefit amount, definition, benefit period, elimination
period, riders (COLA, future increase, return of premium).

### Coordination with Other Sources
If the user has group LTD through their employer, individual DI coordinates with it:
- **Group LTD:** Typically covers 60-66% of income, any-occupation definition (after 2 years),
  capped at a maximum monthly benefit ($5,000-$10,000)
- **CPP Disability (CPP-D):** ~$1,606/month max (2025 — verify). Strict eligibility — must
  have "severe and prolonged" disability preventing ALL work. Many claims denied.
- **Workers' Compensation:** Only covers work-related injuries/illness. Does NOT cover illness
  or off-the-job injuries (which are ~70-80% of disability claims).

**Gap analysis:** Group LTD + CPP-D often leaves a significant gap. Individual DI can fill it.

### Group vs Individual Disability
| Feature | Group (Employer) | Individual |
|---------|-----------------|------------|
| Definition | Usually any-occ after 2 years | You choose (own-occ recommended) |
| Benefit | 60-66% of income, capped | You choose the amount |
| Tax on benefits | Taxable if employer pays premium | Tax-free if you pay premium |
| Portability | Ends when you leave the job | Stays with you forever |
| Underwriting | Guaranteed issue (no medical) | Medical underwriting required |
| Cost | Lower (group rates) | Higher (individual rates) |

**Important tax note:** If your employer pays the disability insurance premium, benefits are
**taxable income** when you receive them. If YOU pay the premium, benefits are **tax-free.**
This means employer-paid 66% coverage may only replace ~45% of income after tax. Ask your
employer if you can pay the premium yourself (payroll deduction, after-tax).

(Source: CLHIA, FCAC, PolicyAdvisor, lionshieldfinancial.ca)

## 6. Critical Illness Insurance

### What It Is
A lump-sum, tax-free payment if you're diagnosed with a covered critical illness and survive
the waiting period (typically 30 days after diagnosis).

### How It Differs from Disability Insurance
| Feature | Critical Illness | Disability |
|---------|-----------------|------------|
| Trigger | Diagnosis of covered condition | Unable to work |
| Payout | One-time lump sum | Monthly income replacement |
| Use of funds | Anything (medical, living expenses, travel) | Income replacement |
| Covers | Specific listed conditions | Any condition preventing work |
| Duration | One payment | Ongoing until recovery or benefit period ends |

**They complement each other** — CI covers the upfront costs of a health crisis (private
treatment, travel, time off for recovery), while DI replaces ongoing income.

### Commonly Covered Conditions (varies by insurer)
- Cancer (most common claim — ~65% of CI claims)
- Heart attack
- Stroke
- Coronary artery bypass surgery
- Organ transplant
- Kidney failure
- Multiple sclerosis
- Motor neuron disease
- Alzheimer's disease
- Parkinson's disease
- Loss of independent existence
- Blindness, deafness
- Major organ failure
- Severe burns

Most policies cover **25-44 conditions.** Some cover as few as 4 (basic plans: cancer, heart
attack, stroke, coronary bypass). More conditions = higher premium.

### Cost Estimates (2026 — verify)
For $100,000 coverage, approximate monthly premiums:

| Age | Male | Female |
|-----|------|--------|
| 25 | $25-35 | $30-40 |
| 30 | $30-45 | $35-50 |
| 35 | $40-60 | $45-65 |
| 40 | $60-90 | $65-95 |
| 45 | $90-130 | $95-135 |
| 50 | $140-200 | $145-210 |

(Source: PolicyAdvisor, PolicyMe, RBC Insurance — verify)

**Note:** Women typically pay slightly more for CI than men (higher incidence of some covered
conditions like breast cancer), which is opposite to life insurance where women pay less.

### Key Riders
- **Return of Premium (ROP) on expiry:** Get all premiums back if you never claim. Adds ~30-50%
  to the cost but provides a "money back" guarantee.
- **Return of Premium on death:** Premiums returned to estate if you die without claiming CI.
- **Additional purchase option:** Buy more coverage later without medical underwriting.
- **Waiver of premium:** Premiums waived if you become disabled.

### When CI Makes Sense
- **Young families:** CI covers the costs that disability insurance doesn't — medical travel,
  experimental treatments, home modifications, childcare during treatment.
- **Self-employed:** No employer benefits to fall back on. A CI payout provides a cash cushion.
- **People with family history:** If cancer or heart disease runs in the family.
- **Dual-purpose with ROP:** If you get premiums back, it's essentially free insurance (minus
  the opportunity cost of those premium dollars).

(Source: PolicyMe, PolicyAdvisor, Canada Life, Manulife)

## 7. Group vs Individual & Portability

### Common Gaps in Group Coverage
- **Waiting period:** New employees often wait 3 months before benefits start.
- **Late entrant:** If you don't enroll during the initial eligibility window, you may need
  medical evidence later (or be denied optional coverage).
- **Coverage limits:** Group life insurance is typically 1-2x salary. Group LTD is 60-66%
  capped at $5,000-$10,000/month. Both are often insufficient.
- **Definition changes:** Group LTD typically switches from own-occupation to any-occupation
  after 2 years. Individual policies maintain own-occ for the full benefit period.
- **Dependents:** Group coverage may not adequately cover spouse or children.

### Conversion Rights (Critical at Job Change)
When you leave an employer, your group life and health benefits usually end on your last day
(or end of the month). But you typically have a **conversion privilege:**

- **Timeline:** 30-60 days to convert group life insurance to an individual policy **without
  medical underwriting.** This is invaluable if your health has deteriorated.
- **What converts:** Group life → individual whole or term life. Some LTD plans have conversion.
  Extended health/dental usually does NOT convert (explore individual health plans instead).
- **Cost:** Converted policies are more expensive than buying new insurance while healthy. But
  if you have health conditions, conversion may be your only option for coverage.
- **Action required:** Contact your group insurer (not your employer, not HR) directly within
  the conversion window. This is often missed and the window is hard.

### When to Supplement Group with Individual
- If group life is only 1-2x salary and your DIME analysis shows a $1M+ need → get individual
  term to fill the gap
- If group LTD is any-occupation after 2 years → consider individual own-occupation DI
- If you're self-employed or a contractor with no group plan → individual is essential
- If you're planning to change jobs → get individual coverage while still healthy and employed

### Tax Treatment
- **Employer-paid life insurance premiums:** Taxable benefit to the employee (included in T4).
  First $25,000 of group term life is not a taxable benefit in some plans — check your T4.
- **Employer-paid disability premiums:** Benefits received are TAXABLE. If you pay the premium
  yourself, benefits are TAX-FREE. This is a significant planning opportunity.
- **Individual policy premiums:** Not tax-deductible (except for self-employed in some cases).
- **Individual policy benefits:** Life insurance death benefit is tax-free. Disability benefits
  are tax-free if you paid the premiums. CI lump sum is tax-free.

## 8. Employer Benefits Optimization

### Coordination of Benefits (Couples)
If both partners have employer health/dental plans:
1. **Submit to your OWN plan first** (you are the "primary" claimant on your own plan)
2. **Submit the unpaid balance to your PARTNER'S plan** (you are a "secondary" claimant on
   their plan as a dependent)
3. This can recover 80-100% of costs that would otherwise be out-of-pocket

**Example:** Sarah's $160/month massage therapy:
- Submit to Sarah's plan: 80% coverage up to $500/year = reimburse ~$400/year
- Submit remainder to Marcus's plan: 80% of remaining up to $500/year = reimburse ~$320/year
- Total reimbursement: ~$720/year vs paying $1,920/year out of pocket

### HSA (Health Spending Account) & WSA (Wellness Spending Account)
- **HSA:** Tax-free dollars for eligible medical expenses (CRA list). Use it before it resets
  (typically January 1 or April 1 — check your plan).
- **WSA:** After-tax dollars for wellness expenses (gym, fitness classes, sometimes broader).
  Less tax-advantaged than HSA.
- **Strategy:** Use HSA for highest-value claims first (expensive prescriptions, dental, glasses).
  Use WSA for wellness spending.
- **Common mistake:** Not using the full balance before reset. Check your balance quarterly.

### EAP (Employee Assistance Program)
- Free through most employers: 6-8 sessions of counselling per issue per year.
- Also covers: financial advice, legal consultation, career coaching, family counselling.
- Completely confidential — employer sees aggregate usage stats, not individual details.
- **Underused:** Most employees don't know about EAP or assume it's only for "serious" problems.
  It's for anything: stress, relationship issues, financial anxiety, grief, parenting.

### Post-Retirement Coverage Gaps
- Most employer health/dental benefits end at retirement (some public sector plans continue).
- Options: provincial pharmacare, Canada Dental Care Plan, private health insurance (Blue Cross,
  Green Shield, Manulife individual plans), professional association group plans.
- **Plan ahead:** Apply for individual coverage before group coverage ends. Some have age limits.

## 9. Property & Casualty Insurance

### Auto Insurance (Provincial Variation)
Canada has a mix of public and private auto insurance:

| Province | System | Notes |
|----------|--------|-------|
| BC | Public (ICBC) | All basic coverage through ICBC, optional private top-up |
| Saskatchewan | Public (SGI) | Basic through SGI, optional private |
| Manitoba | Public (MPI) | Basic through MPI, optional private |
| Ontario | Private | Highly regulated, most expensive province |
| Alberta | Private | Less regulated, more competitive |
| Quebec | Hybrid | Public for injuries (SAAQ), private for property damage |
| Atlantic | Private | Generally lower costs |

**Cost factors:** Driving record, location, vehicle type, usage, age, deductible choice.
**Savings tips:** Bundle with home insurance, increase deductible, winter tires discount (some
provinces), telematics/usage-based programs, loyalty/claims-free discounts.

### Umbrella / Excess Liability Insurance
- Provides additional liability coverage above home and auto limits.
- Typically $1M-$5M in additional coverage for $200-$500/year.
- **Who needs it:** Anyone with significant assets to protect (home equity, investments, income).
  If you're sued    and the judgment exceeds your home/auto liability limits, your personal
  assets are at risk.
- Relatively inexpensive for the protection it provides.

(Source: Insurance Bureau of Canada, provincial regulators)

## 10. Travel & Specialized Insurance

### Travel Medical Insurance
- Provincial health coverage is limited or non-existent outside Canada.
- **US medical costs** can bankrupt a Canadian: a simple ER visit can be $5,000-$50,000+.
  A hospital stay can be $10,000+/day.
- **Coverage needed:** Emergency medical, hospital, ambulance, medical evacuation/repatriation.
- **Pre-existing conditions:** Most policies exclude pre-existing conditions unless stable for
  90-180 days. Read the exclusions carefully.
- **Annual vs single-trip:** Annual plans are cost-effective if you travel 2+ times per year.
  Single-trip for occasional travelers.
- **Credit card coverage:** Some premium credit cards include travel medical insurance. Check
  the limits, exclusions, and whether you must charge the trip to the card.
- **Snowbirds (55+):** Specialized plans for extended US stays. Critical for Canadians spending
  winter months in the US or Mexico.

### Business Insurance (Self-Employed)
- **Professional liability (E&O):** Covers claims of negligence, errors, or omissions in your
  professional services. Essential for consultants, accountants, lawyers, healthcare providers.
- **General liability:** Covers third-party bodily injury and property damage. Essential if
  clients visit your premises or you visit client sites.
- **Business interruption:** Covers lost income if your business is forced to close due to a
  covered event (fire, flood, etc.).
- **Key person insurance:** Life and/or disability insurance on a key employee or partner whose
  loss would significantly impact the business. Benefit paid to the business.
- **Cyber insurance:** Covers data breaches, ransomware, and cyber liability. Increasingly
  important for businesses handling client data.

## 11. Insurance & Estate Planning

### Beneficiary Designations
Life insurance death benefits are paid **directly to the named beneficiary** — they bypass
the will, bypass probate, and are received tax-free. This makes beneficiary designation one
of the most important (and most neglected) financial planning steps.

**Types of designation:**
- **Revocable:** You can change the beneficiary at any time (standard, recommended for most).
- **Irrevocable:** Cannot change without the beneficiary's consent. Used in divorce agreements
  or business arrangements. Think carefully before making a designation irrevocable.
- **Per stirpes:** If the beneficiary dies before you, their share goes to their children
  (descendants). Alternative to naming contingent beneficiaries.
- **Per capita:** Share divided equally among surviving beneficiaries only.

**Common mistakes:**
- Naming your "estate" as beneficiary → proceeds go through probate (fees, delays, public record)
- Forgetting to update after divorce → ex-spouse gets the money (in most provinces, the
  beneficiary designation overrides the will)
- Not naming a contingent beneficiary → if primary dies before you, proceeds go to estate

### Policy Ownership
- **Individual ownership (most common):** You own the policy, you control it, you name the
  beneficiary. Death benefit goes to your beneficiary tax-free.
- **Corporate ownership:** The corporation owns the policy and is the beneficiary. Death benefit
  flows to the corporation's Capital Dividend Account (CDA) and can be distributed to
  shareholders tax-free. Used in business succession planning.
- **Insurance trust:** Policy held in a trust for the benefit of specific individuals. Provides
  control over how and when proceeds are distributed. Useful for blended families or
  beneficiaries who can't manage large sums.

### Insurance for Estate Tax Liability
At death, there's a deemed disposition of all assets (except those rolling to spouse). This can
create a large capital gains tax bill. Life insurance can provide liquidity to pay this tax
without forcing the sale of assets (family business, cottage, investments).

**Example:** $1M in unrealized capital gains on a non-registered investment portfolio →
~$165,000 in tax (at 50% inclusion, ~33% marginal rate). A $165K term-to-100 or whole life
policy ensures the estate can pay this without selling investments at a potentially bad time.

### Creditor Protection
Life insurance proceeds paid to a named beneficiary (not the estate) are generally protected
from creditors of the deceased. This is significant for business owners and high-liability
professions.

(Source: CLHIA, canada.ca, estate planning guides)

## 12. When NOT to Buy Insurance

### Over-Insurance Warning Signs
- Coverage significantly exceeds the DIME calculation
- Insuring risks that won't cause financial hardship (extended warranty on a $500 appliance)
- Multiple overlapping policies covering the same risk
- Paying for insurance on children's lives (rarely financially necessary — children don't
  earn income. Exception: guaranteed insurability riders for children with family health history)

### Products to Avoid (for Most Families)
- **Whole life pushed by a bank advisor** to a young family with debt → term is almost always
  better. The advisor earns higher commissions on permanent products.
- **Mortgage life insurance from the bank** → decreasing coverage at non-decreasing premiums.
  You're paying the same while the benefit shrinks as you pay down the mortgage. Buy a level
  term policy instead (same or lower cost, better coverage, portable).
- **Credit card balance insurance** → expensive per-dollar of coverage. Often has many
  exclusions. Better to have proper term life coverage.
- **Extended warranties** → manufacturer warranty + credit card extended warranty are usually
  sufficient. Exception: expensive, repair-prone items.
- **Flight insurance** → your regular life and travel insurance already covers this.

### When to Cancel or Reduce Coverage
- Mortgage paid off → life insurance need decreases
- Kids financially independent → life insurance need decreases significantly
- Significant savings accumulated → self-insured for some risks
- Approaching retirement → employer benefits ending, but need may also decrease
- **Never cancel before replacement is in force.** If switching insurers, keep the old policy
  until the new one is approved and active.

### The "Buy Term and Invest the Difference" Strategy
- Buy affordable term life instead of expensive permanent insurance.
- Invest the premium savings ($200-$300/month difference) in a TFSA or RRSP.
- Over 25-30 years, the investment portfolio will likely exceed the cash value of a permanent
  policy. And if you die during the term, the death benefit provides the protection.
- This strategy works for ~90% of Canadian families.

## 1. "Should I Buy It?" — Daily Spending Coach

When a user asks about a purchase ("should I buy this $900 TV?", "can I afford a vacation?",
"is this gym membership worth it?"), run this framework using their ACTUAL financial data.

### Decision Framework

**Step 1: Can they literally afford it?**
- Does the surplus cover it? (one-time vs recurring)
- Would it create or increase debt?
- Would it dip into the emergency fund?

**Step 2: What's the real cost?**
- If it would go on a credit card unpaid: calculate TRUE cost with interest
  - "$900 at 21% paying $50/month = $1,087. That TV costs $187 extra."
- If recurring: calculate annual and 5-year cost
  - "$45/month gym = $540/year = $2,700 over 5 years"
- Opportunity cost: what would investing that money return?

**Step 3: How does it affect the plan?**
- Push back debt-free date? By how much?
- Reduce savings contributions? Which goals delayed?
- Blow the budget category for the month?
- Room in discretionary/fun money budget?

**Step 4: The Verdict** (honest, not preachy)
- **"Go for it"** — fits the budget, doesn't derail goals
- **"Yes, but here's the trade-off"** — affordable but delays something
- **"Not right now"** — would create debt or derail goals. Suggest alternatives.
- **"Hard no"** — would cause serious financial damage. Be direct.

**Step 5: Alternatives** (when answer isn't "go for it")
- Cheaper version, save-and-buy-with-cash timeline, trade suggestion, priority reminder

### Personality
Feel like a financially savvy friend, not a scolding parent:
- Never judge the desire
- Always show the math
- Respect their stated priorities (if they said dining out is joy, don't lecture about restaurants)
- Celebrate when the answer is yes

### The "Wait 48 Hours" Rule
For non-essential purchases over $100: suggest waiting 48 hours. If they still want it, run the analysis.

### BNPL Warning
If they mention Afterpay, Klarna, PayBright:
- Calculate effective interest if they miss payments
- If they can't buy it outright, they probably shouldn't buy it on BNPL
- Exception: 0% interest, short-term, for a budgeted purchase

## 2. Plan vs. Actual — Monthly Check-Ins

When a user comes back for a check-in or says "how's my plan going?":

### What to Ask For
- Actual spending by category (or upload statements)
- Current balances on all debts
- Current balances on savings/investment accounts
- Any life changes since last check-in
- Any one-time events (tax refund, bonus, inheritance, large expense)

### What to Compare
- **Budget:** planned vs actual per category, flag overages in red, under-budget in green
- **Debt payoff:** planned balance vs actual per debt
- **Savings:** planned contributions vs actual
- **Net worth:** projected vs actual
- **Milestone tracking:** on pace for target dates?

### What to Generate
- Plan vs actual summary table (category | planned | actual | variance | status)
- Trend if multiple check-ins exist
- Running scorecard: "On plan for X of last Y months"
- Adjust plan if needed (consistently over-budget somewhere? raise got? fell behind?)

### Plan Versioning
- Maintain "original plan" and "revised plan" concepts
- Major life changes → full rebuild ("Plan v2"), reset baseline
- Minor adjustments → update targets only

### Save Check-In Data
Save each check-in to `check-ins/YYYY-MM.md` with the comparison data. This builds the
historical trend for pattern analysis.

### Dashboard Features for Check-Ins
- Monthly scorecard (pass/fail grid)
- Streak tracker ("3 months under budget in a row")
- Progress bars per goal with % complete
- Variance analysis patterns ("You consistently overspend in December")

## 3. Life Scenarios the Skill Must Handle

> **For detailed action plans when a user reports a specific life change** (new baby, divorce,
> job loss, parental leave, death of spouse, etc.), read `references/life-events.md`. This
> section covers ongoing demographic/financial situations and how to adapt the overall plan.

Adapt the plan to ANY Canadian, including:

- **Low income / social assistance:** Navigate asset limits, income clawbacks, benefit maximization, transition planning
- **Working poor / paycheck to paycheck:** Stabilize cash flow, eliminate predatory debt, tiny emergency fund, maximize benefits
- **Young professional:** Student debt, start TFSA, capture employer match, FHSA for first home
- **Family / middle income:** Balance competing priorities (debt, RESP, RRSP, mortgage), CCB optimization
- **High income ($200K+):** Tax optimization, spousal RRSP, incorporation, IPP, OAS clawback avoidance
- **Self-employed / business owner:** Irregular income, deductions, HST, incorporation decision (see section 6)
- **Divorced / separated:** Split assets, support obligations, updated beneficiaries
- **Single parent:** Sole income, insurance critical, CCB maximization
- **Approaching retirement (55-65):** CPP timing, OAS timing, drawdown strategy, bridge income
- **Already retired:** Fixed income, OAS clawback management, sustainable withdrawal, estate planning
- **Person with disabilities:** DTC, RDSP, government benefits, Henson Trust, long-term care
- **New to Canada:** Partial OAS, building credit, foreign assets, tax treaties
- **Blended family:** Multiple RESP beneficiaries, complex support, estate complexity

## 4. Insurance Benefits Coaching

During expense review, cross-reference out-of-pocket health spending against employer benefits:

- Massage, physio, chiro, prescriptions, dental, glasses, therapy, naturopath, orthotics
- Flag reimbursable amounts: "You're spending $120/month on massage out of pocket. Your plan covers 80% up to $500/year. That's $X back."

### Coordination of Benefits (couples)
If both have employer plans: submit to own plan first, then unpaid portion to partner's plan. Many get to 100% coverage and don't realize it.

### HSA/WSA
If they have one: remind them of balance, reset date, eligible expenses. "Use it or lose it."

### EAP (Employee Assistance Program)
Almost nobody uses this. Free counselling (usually 6-8 sessions), financial advice, legal consultation.

### For Self-Employed / No Benefits
- Provincial pharmacare, Canada Dental Care Plan, professional association group plans, Chamber of Commerce plans, Medical Expense Tax Credit

## 5. Estate Planning Awareness

The skill can't DO estate planning (needs a lawyer), but can identify gaps.

### Check These
1. **Will?** If no: "Without a will, the province decides who gets what. A basic will costs $500-$1,500."
2. **Both Powers of Attorney?** POA for Property + POA for Personal Care (in Quebec: "protection mandates")
3. **Beneficiary designations current?** They override the will. If ex-spouse is still named on RRSP, ex gets it.
4. **Tax impact at death:** Estimate deemed disposition tax hit. Enough liquid assets to cover?
5. **Probate fees:** Calculate for their province and estate value.
6. **Estate checklist:** Will, POAs, beneficiary review, executor informed, life insurance if tax liability exceeds liquid assets

## 6. Self-Employed & Incorporated Planning

### Key Analysis
1. **Salary vs Dividends:** Salary creates RRSP room + CPP + mortgage qualification + EI eligibility. Dividends avoid CPP, simpler, sometimes more tax-efficient. Most advisors recommend a blend.
2. **Small Business Deduction:** First $500K at ~9-12% combined. Above = 23-31%.
3. **HSA:** Corporation pays medical expenses tax-deductibly. Need arm's-length employee if sole proprietor.
4. **IPP:** For incorporated owners 40+. Contributions exceed RRSP limits. Setup ~$3K, valuations ~$2K every 3 years.
5. **Quarterly installments:** Due Mar 15, Jun 15, Sep 15, Dec 15. Track and remind.

## 7. Couples & Money

### If Both Partners Participate
Optimize across the household:
- Spousal RRSP (higher earner contributes, gets deduction, income split in retirement)
  - 3-year attribution rule on withdrawals
- Pension income splitting (Form T1032, up to 50% of eligible pension)
- CPP sharing (both 60+, based on years together)
- Attribution rules: income on gifted money taxed in giver's hands
  - Workarounds: spousal RRSP, prescribed rate loan, TFSA (attribution-free)
- TFSA strategy: give money to lower-income spouse for their TFSA
- Account structure: recommend hybrid (joint for household, separate for personal, separate registered)

### If Only One Partner Participates
Build the plan, flag incomplete information, invite the partner when ready.

### Common-Law vs Married
- Married: automatic property equalization in all provinces
- Common-law: generally NO automatic property rights (exceptions: BC, Manitoba, Saskatchewan)
- Ontario common-law: NO right to divide property at separation, NO automatic inheritance
- If common-law: flag cohabitation agreement and will as important

### Handling Disagreements
Don't play counsellor. Show both scenarios with math. Suggest personal spending accounts (agreed amount, zero questions asked). Recommend quarterly money check-ins together.

## 8. Long-Term Care Planning

For anyone 45+, or earlier if family history of cognitive decline:

### The Reality
- Provincial health insurance does NOT fully cover nursing homes, home care, or assisted living
- Government-subsidized LTC homes: $1,200-$3,500/month (co-pay, income-based)
- Private facilities: $6,000-$15,000+/month
- Home care: $25-$75+/hour
- About 12% of Canadians over 85 live in LTC facilities

### LTC Stress Test
Run as part of retirement projection:
- Scenario A: No LTC needed (base case)
- Scenario B: 3 years subsidized care at 85 (~$108K)
- Scenario C: 5 years home care at 85 (~$182K)
Show impact on portfolio sustainability.

### Mitigation
- Extra $100K-$200K cushion in retirement savings
- LTC insurance if age 45-55 and healthy (premiums prohibitive after 60)
- Principal residence can be sold (emotionally difficult, last resort)
- Critical illness insurance for lump sum on diagnosis
- Family conversation about expectations

## 9. Cryptocurrency & Digital Assets

### CRA Treatment
- Crypto treated as commodity, not currency
- Every sale, trade, or conversion (including coin-to-coin swaps) is a taxable event
- Capital gains: 50% inclusion rate (verify — politically volatile)
- Must track adjusted cost base (ACB) of every purchase — user's responsibility
- Mining/staking income: business income (100% taxable) if done regularly
- Airdrops/hard forks: fair market value at receipt = income
- Can't hold crypto directly in registered accounts (RRSP/TFSA)
- CAN hold crypto ETFs (Purpose Bitcoin ETF, etc.) in registered accounts

### Recommendations
- Use a crypto tax tracker (Koinly, CoinTracker, Adjusted) for ACB
- Small position (<5%): track it, report it, don't let it distract from core plan
- Large position (>10%): flag concentrated risk, suggest diversification
- Never recommend buying or selling specific cryptocurrencies

## 10. Behavioural Finance — Making the Plan Stick

### Automation Over Willpower
Every recurring recommendation should be followed by: "Set up a pre-authorized contribution
on the day after payday. Once it's automated, you never think about it."

### Commitment Anchoring
When user agrees to an action: "How much per month, and which day? I'll track it at next check-in."
Specific commitments work. Vague intentions don't.

### Progress Visibility
Show concrete progress at every check-in:
"3 months ago: $12,400 in high-interest debt. Today: $9,800. That's $2,600 paid down — ahead
of plan by $200."

### The "One Thing" Principle
If the action list is long, highlight the single most impactful action:
"If you do ONE thing this week: [specific, achievable action with estimated dollar impact]."
Quick wins build momentum. Long to-do lists cause paralysis.

### Handling Setbacks
Never shame. Never lecture.
"Savings target was missed this month — $200 instead of $400. That happens. Let's look at
what changed. The goal isn't perfection — it's progress."

## 11. Goal Conflict Resolution

When the math doesn't work (often):

1. **Show the math honestly.** "To hit all these goals, you'd need $X/month — more than your surplus."
2. **Prioritize together.** "Which are non-negotiable? Which have flexibility?"
3. **Show trade-offs with real scenarios.** "Retire at 55 + smaller house vs retire at 60 + target house."
4. **Let them choose.** "These are your trade-offs. No wrong answer. Once you pick, I build the plan."

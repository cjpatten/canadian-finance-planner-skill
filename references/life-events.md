# Life Events — Financial Action Plans for Canadians

Read this reference when a user reports a major life change. Each event includes a structured
action plan with Canadian programs, tax implications, timelines, and common mistakes to avoid.

All dollar amounts should be verified via web search before presenting — they change annually.
Key sources: canada.ca (EI, CPP, CCB, FHSA, HBP, tax credits), cmhc-schl.gc.ca (mortgage
insurance), taxtips.ca (provincial tax rates, probate fees), rqap.gouv.qc.ca (Quebec QPIP).
Full source URLs are in Section 11 at the bottom of this file.

## 1. Having a Baby

### Trigger Phrases
> "We're expecting" / "we're having a baby" / "just had a baby" / "we're pregnant" /
> "adopting a child" / "baby on the way"

### Immediate Actions (This Week)
- [ ] Register the birth with your province (this automatically triggers CCB application in
  most provinces — ON, BC, AB, QC do this electronically at the hospital)
- [ ] If not auto-registered, file RC66 (Canada Child Benefits Application) within 11 months
  of birth — do it ASAP, benefits are retroactive only 11 months
- [ ] Notify employer about upcoming parental leave (most provinces require 4 weeks written notice)
- [ ] Review employer benefits — does your plan cover the baby automatically or do you need
  to add them within 30-60 days?
- [ ] If you have a will, update it with guardianship designation. If no will → this is urgent.

### Short-Term Actions (This Month / This Quarter)
- [ ] Open a RESP for the new child. Contribute at least $2,500/year to capture full CESG
  ($500/year government match). Even $208/month gets the full grant.
- [ ] Apply for EI maternity benefits (can apply up to 12 weeks before expected due date)
- [ ] Apply for provincial benefits: Ontario Baby Bonus, BC Early Childhood Tax Benefit,
  Alberta Child and Family Benefit, etc. (varies by province — verify via web search)
- [ ] Review life insurance needs — a new child increases the life insurance need by ~$200K-$300K
  (education costs + income replacement during dependent years)
- [ ] Review disability insurance — if the primary earner can't work, can the family survive?
- [ ] Research childcare costs in your area — typical ranges: $1,000-$2,500/month depending
  on province and type (home daycare vs centre vs nanny)
- [ ] Check if your province has subsidized childcare (Quebec $9.35/day, Ontario CWELCC program)

### Canadian Programs & Benefits
- **Canada Child Benefit (CCB):** Tax-free monthly payment. ~$7,787/year per child under 6,
  ~$6,570/year per child 6-17 (2025 — verify). Phases out starting at $37,487 family net income.
  (Source: canada.ca/child-family-benefits)
- **CESG:** 20% match on first $2,500 of annual RESP contributions per child ($500/year max,
  $7,200 lifetime). Additional CESG for lower-income families (extra 10-20% on first $500).
- **Canada Learning Bond (CLB):** $500 initial + $100/year for low-income families (no
  contribution required). Must open a RESP to receive it.
- **EI Maternity:** 15 weeks at 55% of insurable earnings (max ~$729/week in 2026 — verify).
  Birth mothers only. 1-week waiting period. (Source: canada.ca/ei-maternity-parental)
- **EI Parental:** Standard: 35 weeks at 55% (max ~$729/week). Extended: 61 weeks at 33%
  (max ~$437/week). Can be shared between parents. In Quebec, use QPIP instead (higher rates).
- **Child Care Expense Deduction:** Must be claimed by the lower-income spouse. Max $8,000/year
  per child under 7, $5,000/year per child 7-16 (2025 — verify).

### Tax Implications & Deadlines
- CCB is calculated based on prior year's family net income — file tax returns on time every year
- RRSP contributions can reduce family net income, potentially increasing CCB
- Maternity/parental EI benefits are taxable income
- Child care expenses are deductible (by lower-income spouse)
- Medical expenses related to pregnancy/birth are eligible for Medical Expense Tax Credit

### Impact on Existing Plan
- **Budget:** Add childcare ($1,000-$2,500/month), baby expenses (~$200-$400/month for
  diapers, formula, clothing), RESP contribution ($208/month for full CESG). Offset partly by CCB.
- **Registered accounts:** Open RESP, start contributing. RRSP strategy may change to boost CCB.
- **Insurance:** Life insurance need increases significantly. Review disability coverage.
- **Projections:** Childcare costs are temporary (5-6 years). Education costs are distant but real.
  Retirement timeline may shift if one parent reduces work hours.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. **Waiting to apply for CCB.** Benefits are only retroactive 11 months. Apply at birth.
2. **Not opening a RESP immediately.** CESG is use-it-or-lose-it each year. Starting at birth
   gives you 18 years of government matching.
3. **Forgetting to update beneficiaries** on life insurance, RRSP, TFSA after baby arrives.
4. **Underestimating childcare costs.** Budget for this before the baby arrives.
5. **Not checking employer top-up.** Many employers top up EI to 75-95% of salary — ask HR.

### When to Get Professional Help
- **Lawyer:** Will and guardianship designation ($500-$1,500 for a basic will + POAs)
- **Insurance advisor:** Life and disability insurance review (especially if no employer coverage)

## 2. Getting Married / Becoming Common-Law

### Trigger Phrases
> "We're getting married" / "we just got married" / "we moved in together" / "we're common-law
> now" / "my partner and I are combining finances"

### Immediate Actions (This Week)
- [ ] Update beneficiary designations on all registered accounts (RRSP, TFSA, RESP, life
  insurance). Your spouse/common-law partner should generally be the beneficiary.
- [ ] Review employer benefits — add spouse to your health/dental plan if applicable (usually
  must be done within 30-60 days of marriage/common-law status change)
- [ ] Discuss financial goals and money management approach (joint, separate, or hybrid accounts)

### Short-Term Actions (This Month / This Quarter)
- [ ] Review tax situation — spousal amount tax credit, potential income splitting strategies
- [ ] Consider spousal RRSP if one spouse earns significantly more
- [ ] Update will and Powers of Attorney (or create them if you don't have them)
- [ ] Review insurance — does combined coverage have gaps or overlaps?
- [ ] Coordinate employee benefits (submit claims to own plan first, then spouse's plan for
  remainder — "coordination of benefits")
- [ ] Discuss and set up account structure (joint chequing for household, separate personal,
  separate registered accounts)

### Canadian Programs & Benefits
- **Spousal Amount Tax Credit:** Claim up to $15,705 (2026 — verify) if spouse's net income
  is below this threshold. Non-refundable credit at 15% = up to ~$2,356 federal tax savings.
- **Pension Income Splitting:** Can split up to 50% of eligible pension income with spouse
  (Form T1032). Primarily useful in retirement.
- **Spousal RRSP:** Higher-income spouse contributes to a spousal RRSP, gets the tax deduction.
  After 3 years, withdrawals are taxed in the lower-income spouse's hands.
- **TFSA:** No attribution rules on TFSA. You can gift money to your spouse to contribute to
  their TFSA — the growth and withdrawals are tax-free in their name.
- **CPP Pension Sharing:** Both spouses must be 60+. Can share CPP retirement pension based
  on years of cohabitation. Reduces tax if one spouse has higher CPP.

### Tax Implications & Deadlines
- Common-law status: CRA considers you common-law after 12 months of continuous cohabitation
  (or immediately if you have a child together). Update marital status with CRA.
- Attribution rules: If you gift money to your spouse for investing (not TFSA), income earned
  on that money is attributed back to you for tax purposes. Capital gains are attributed to
  the spouse. Prescribed rate loan can avoid attribution.
- Marriage/common-law affects CCB calculation — family net income now includes both spouses.
  CCB may decrease if combined income is higher.
- GST/HST credit recalculated based on family income.

### Impact on Existing Plan
- **Budget:** Combine incomes and expenses. Identify overlapping subscriptions/insurance.
  Two-income household changes emergency fund target.
- **Registered accounts:** Spousal RRSP strategy, TFSA gifting, beneficiary updates.
- **Insurance:** Review life and disability needs as a couple. May need less or more depending
  on income dependency.
- **Projections:** Retirement planning now includes two CPP/OAS streams, potential pension splitting.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. **Not updating CRA marital status.** Can result in CCB overpayments you'll have to repay.
2. **Forgetting beneficiary designations.** RRSP beneficiary designation overrides your will
   in most provinces. If your ex is still listed, they get the money.
3. **Ignoring attribution rules.** Gifting investment money to a spouse without a prescribed
   rate loan triggers attribution.
4. **Assuming common-law = married for property rights.** Property division on separation varies
   significantly by province. In Ontario, common-law partners don't have automatic equalization.
5. **Not discussing money before merging finances.** Different attitudes toward debt, spending,
   and risk cause the most financial conflict in relationships.

### When to Get Professional Help
- **Lawyer:** Prenup/marriage contract if significant asset disparity, blended family, or
  business ownership ($1,500-$5,000). Will and POA update.
- **CPA:** If one or both are self-employed, tax optimization as a couple is complex.

## 3. Divorce / Separation

### Trigger Phrases
> "We're getting divorced" / "we're separating" / "my marriage is ending" / "my partner and I
> are splitting up" / "going through a separation"

### Immediate Actions (This Week)
- [ ] Open a separate bank account in your name only if you don't have one
- [ ] Secure copies of all financial documents: tax returns, bank statements, investment
  statements, mortgage documents, pension statements, insurance policies
- [ ] Do NOT move money out of joint accounts without legal advice — this can harm your position
- [ ] Consult a family lawyer for initial advice (many offer free 30-minute consultations)
- [ ] Change passwords on personal accounts and email

### Short-Term Actions (This Month / This Quarter)
- [ ] File Form RC65 or update CRA My Account to change marital status — this recalculates CCB
- [ ] Update beneficiary designations on RRSP, TFSA, life insurance (remove ex-spouse if
  appropriate — but check separation agreement first)
- [ ] Understand your province's property division rules (see below)
- [ ] Get a valuation of the matrimonial home, pensions, and business interests if applicable
- [ ] Review and adjust your budget for single-income or reduced-income living
- [ ] Consider mediation before litigation — significantly cheaper ($3,000-$7,000 vs $15,000-$50,000+)

### Canadian Programs & Benefits
- **CCB Recalculation:** If you become the primary caregiver, CCB will be recalculated based
  on YOUR individual net income (likely higher CCB). Update CRA immediately.
- **GST/HST Credit:** Recalculated as individual. May now qualify if you didn't before.
- **Legal Aid:** Available in all provinces for low-income individuals in family law matters.
  Income thresholds vary by province.
- **EI:** If you need to leave your job due to domestic violence, you may qualify for EI
  special benefits without the usual quit disqualification.

### Tax Implications & Deadlines
- **Spousal support:** Deductible for the payer, taxable income for the receiver (if paid
  under a written agreement or court order). Must be periodic payments, not lump sum.
- **Child support:** NOT deductible for the payer, NOT taxable for the receiver. No tax impact.
- **RRSP division:** RRSPs can be transferred between spouses on separation under a court
  order or written separation agreement WITHOUT triggering tax. Use Form T2220.
- **Pension division:** Workplace pensions can be divided via a Domestic Relations Order (DRO)
  or equivalent. DB pensions are valued actuarially. DC pensions divided by balance.
- **TFSA division:** Unlike RRSPs, TFSA transfers between spouses on separation are NOT
  automatic. The holder must withdraw (regains room next year), and the other spouse contributes
  to their own TFSA (must have room). Plan this carefully.
- **RESP:** Can change the subscriber. CESG stays with the child regardless of which parent
  is subscriber. Both parents can contribute.
- **Principal residence exemption:** If one spouse keeps the home, the exemption continues.
  If sold, the gain is exempt. If one spouse buys out the other, the buyout is not taxable.
- **Date of separation matters:** Property is valued as of date of separation in most provinces.

### Impact on Existing Plan
- **Budget:** Fundamental restructure. One household becomes two. Housing costs often increase
  (two rents/mortgages). Childcare arrangements may change.
- **Registered accounts:** RRSP and pension division. TFSA room management. RESP subscriber change.
- **Insurance:** Life insurance beneficiary updates critical. May need to maintain coverage
  if required by separation agreement. Review disability insurance.
- **Projections:** Retirement projection completely changes. CPP credits can be split for the
  years of cohabitation (Canada Pension Plan credit splitting).

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. **Not updating CRA.** If you don't report the separation, you may receive CCB based on
   combined income and owe it back later.
2. **Leaving ex-spouse as RRSP beneficiary.** In most provinces, the beneficiary designation
   on an RRSP overrides the will. If your ex is still listed, they get the money at your death.
3. **Forgetting CPP credit splitting.** You're entitled to split CPP credits earned during
   the relationship. Apply to Service Canada — this is separate from pension division.
4. **Moving out without legal advice.** In some provinces, leaving the matrimonial home can
   affect your property claims.
5. **Not valuing pensions properly.** A DB pension can be worth hundreds of thousands of dollars.
   Get an actuarial valuation.
6. **Agreeing to waive spousal support without understanding the implications.** Consult a lawyer.

### When to Get Professional Help
- **Family lawyer:** Essential. Even if you use mediation, get independent legal advice ($200-$500/hour).
- **Actuary:** If dividing a DB pension ($1,500-$3,000 for a valuation).
- **CPA:** If business assets are involved or tax implications are complex.
- **Financial planner:** To model post-separation financial scenarios and rebuilding timeline.

## 4. Job Loss / Layoff

### Trigger Phrases
> "I lost my job" / "I got laid off" / "I was let go" / "I'm being terminated" / "my contract
> wasn't renewed" / "the company is downsizing"

### Immediate Actions (This Week)
- [ ] DO NOT sign a severance package immediately — you typically have time to review.
  Get legal advice if the package seems low (rule of thumb: ~1 month per year of service,
  but varies widely based on age, position, and employment law)
- [ ] Apply for EI within 4 weeks of your last day — there's a waiting period, so apply early
- [ ] Review your employer benefits — most extended health/dental ends on your last day or
  end of the month. You usually have 30-60 days to convert to a personal policy (conversion
  privilege). This is important if you have health conditions.
- [ ] Create an emergency budget: cut to essentials only. Calculate how many months your
  savings will last at the reduced spending level.
- [ ] File for any outstanding expense reimbursements, vacation pay, banked overtime

### Short-Term Actions (This Month / This Quarter)
- [ ] If offered a lump sum severance, consider directing some or all to your RRSP (see
  tax implications below)
- [ ] Review your investment strategy — this is a low-income year, which creates opportunities
  (RRSP withdrawal at low tax rate, or strategic TFSA contributions)
- [ ] Update your resume and LinkedIn. Consider career counselling (employer EAP may still
  cover a few sessions post-termination)
- [ ] If you have a mortgage, contact your lender proactively — they may offer payment
  deferral or reduced payments
- [ ] Pause non-essential automatic savings (RESP top-ups, extra mortgage payments) until
  income is restored

### Canadian Programs & Benefits
- **Employment Insurance (EI):** 55% of average insurable earnings, up to ~$729/week
  (2026 — verify). Duration: 14-45 weeks depending on hours worked and regional unemployment
  rate. Need 420-700 insurable hours in the past 52 weeks (varies by region).
  Apply at canada.ca/ei within 4 weeks. (Source: canada.ca/ei)
- **Canada Workers Benefit:** If you take a lower-paying job, you may qualify (~$1,518 max
  for single, ~$2,616 for family, 2025 — verify).
- **Provincial programs:** Ontario Works, BC Employment Assistance, Alberta Income Support, etc.
  Apply if EI is insufficient or runs out.
- **Canada Training Credit:** If you plan to retrain, check your CTC balance (accumulates
  $250/year if income $10K-$150K). Can offset up to 50% of eligible training fees.

### Tax Implications & Deadlines
- **Severance tax treatment:**
  - Lump sum severance: employer withholds tax at a flat rate (often 30%+), but your actual
    tax rate for the year may be lower if you don't work the rest of the year.
  - Salary continuance: taxed as normal employment income through regular payroll.
  - **Retiring allowance RRSP transfer:** If you have pre-1996 service, you can transfer
    $2,000/year of service directly to your RRSP (tax-free transfer, no RRSP room needed).
    This is separate from your regular RRSP contribution room.
- **Low-income year strategy:** If your total income this year will be low:
  - Consider withdrawing from RRSP at the lower marginal rate (especially if you expect higher
    income in future years)
  - Maximize TFSA contributions (income from TFSA doesn't affect EI or other benefits)
  - Consider converting RRSP to TFSA via strategic withdrawal
- **EI benefits are taxable.** They'll issue a T4E. Plan for the tax bill.
- **Group RRSP/DPSP:** Your employer plan contributions vest immediately in most cases.
  Transfer to a personal RRSP or LIRA (locked-in if pension-related).

### Impact on Existing Plan
- **Budget:** Switch to emergency budget immediately. Calculate monthly burn rate vs savings.
- **Registered accounts:** Pause voluntary contributions. Consider low-income-year RRSP strategies.
- **Insurance:** Extended health/dental likely ending — use conversion privilege within deadline.
  Life insurance through employer also ends — get personal coverage while healthy.
- **Projections:** Retirement timeline may shift. Update employment income assumptions when
  new job is secured.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. **Signing severance without legal review.** An employment lawyer's fee ($500-$2,000) often
   pays for itself many times over in improved severance.
2. **Not applying for EI immediately.** The clock starts when you apply, not when you lose
   your job. Every week you delay is a week of lost benefits.
3. **Letting employer benefits lapse without converting.** If you have health conditions,
   converting to a personal policy (within 30-60 days) avoids medical underwriting.
4. **Panic-selling investments.** Job loss is stressful but selling investments in a down market
   locks in losses. Use cash savings and EI first.
5. **Not adjusting tax withholdings.** If you start a new job mid-year, you may be over-withheld.
   File Form T1213 to reduce withholding.

### When to Get Professional Help
- **Employment lawyer:** If severance seems low, you were terminated without cause, or there's
  a potential human rights issue ($500-$2,000 for a consultation and demand letter).
- **Career coach:** If considering a career change or retraining.

## 5. Maternity / Parental Leave

### Trigger Phrases
> "I'm going on mat leave" / "starting parental leave" / "taking time off for the baby" /
> "my leave starts next month" / "planning our parental leave"

### Immediate Actions (This Week)
- [ ] Confirm your employer's top-up policy — many top up EI to 75-95% of salary for a
  portion of the leave. This is taxable income.
- [ ] Apply for EI maternity/parental benefits (can apply up to 12 weeks before due date).
  Choose standard (35 weeks at 55%) or extended (61 weeks at 33%) parental benefits.
- [ ] Plan which parent takes which portion of leave — parental benefits can be shared.
  There's a "use it or lose it" provision: 5 extra weeks (standard) or 8 extra weeks
  (extended) if the second parent takes at least some leave.
- [ ] Budget for reduced income during leave (EI replaces only 55% of income, capped at
  ~$729/week in 2026 — verify)

### Short-Term Actions (This Month / This Quarter)
- [ ] Build a leave budget: income drops to EI + employer top-up (if any) + CCB + partner's income
- [ ] Front-load RRSP contributions before leave starts (you won't earn RRSP room during leave)
- [ ] Check if your employer continues pension contributions during leave — some do, some don't.
  If not, you may be able to buy back the service later.
- [ ] Continue TFSA contributions during leave if cash flow allows — TFSA room accumulates
  regardless of income
- [ ] Review whether to pause RESP or other savings during the income reduction period

### Canadian Programs & Benefits
- **EI Maternity Benefits:** 15 weeks, 55% of insurable earnings (max ~$729/week, 2026 — verify).
  Birth mothers only. Can start up to 12 weeks before due date.
- **EI Standard Parental:** 35 weeks at 55% (max ~$729/week). Shareable between parents.
  + 5 bonus weeks if second parent takes at least 5 weeks.
- **EI Extended Parental:** 61 weeks at 33% (max ~$437/week). Shareable between parents.
  + 8 bonus weeks if second parent takes at least 8 weeks.
- **Quebec (QPIP):** Higher replacement rates: maternity 70% for 18 weeks + 55% for 7 weeks.
  Parental 70% for 25 weeks (or 55% for 32 weeks). Paternity 70% for 5 weeks. More generous
  than federal EI. Quebec residents use QPIP, not federal EI, for parental benefits.
- **CCB:** Starts after birth registration. Based on prior year family net income.

### Tax Implications & Deadlines
- EI and employer top-up are both taxable income.
- **RRSP room warning:** RRSP contribution room is based on prior year's earned income.
  During leave, your earned income may be $0 or very low. This means you'll have little or
  no new RRSP room for the following tax year. Front-load contributions before leave.
- TFSA room is unaffected by income — it accumulates every January 1 regardless.
- If one parent stays on leave for the full year, family net income may be lower, potentially
  increasing CCB and GST/HST credit.
- Medical expenses for pregnancy/delivery are eligible for the Medical Expense Tax Credit.

### Impact on Existing Plan
- **Budget:** Temporary income reduction (6-18 months depending on leave length). Plan for
  the gap between normal income and EI + top-up.
- **Registered accounts:** Front-load RRSP before leave. TFSA unaffected. RESP contributions
  may need to pause if cash is tight.
- **Insurance:** Employer group benefits usually continue during leave — confirm with HR.
- **Projections:** Short-term cash flow impact. Long-term minimal impact if return-to-work
  is planned. If one parent plans to stay home longer, recalculate retirement projections.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. **Not choosing between standard and extended before applying.** Once you choose, you generally
   can't switch. Extended sounds better but the lower weekly rate can be very tight.
2. **Forgetting about the "use it or lose it" bonus weeks.** 5-8 extra weeks are available
   if the second parent takes some leave. Worth thousands in benefits.
3. **Not front-loading RRSP.** You'll lose a year of contribution room if your earned income
   during leave is zero.
4. **Assuming employer top-up covers the whole leave.** Most top-ups are for 6-17 weeks, not
   the full 35-61 weeks. Budget for the cliff when top-up ends.
5. **Quebec residents applying for federal EI instead of QPIP.** Different programs, different
   rates. QPIP is more generous.

### When to Get Professional Help
- **HR/Benefits specialist:** Confirm employer top-up, benefits continuation, and pension buyback.
- **CPA:** If self-employed, parental benefits work differently (QPIP covers self-employed in
  Quebec; federal EI requires opting into special benefits for self-employed).

## 6. Death of Spouse

### Trigger Phrases
> "My husband/wife/partner passed away" / "my spouse died" / "I lost my partner" /
> "dealing with my spouse's estate"

### Immediate Actions (This Week)
- [ ] Obtain multiple copies of the death certificate (10+ copies — you'll need them for
  banks, insurance, CRA, pension, property transfers)
- [ ] Contact the life insurance company to file a claim (need death certificate + policy number)
- [ ] Apply for the CPP Death Benefit ($2,500 lump sum) — apply to Service Canada
- [ ] Apply for CPP Survivor's Pension (monthly — see amounts below)
- [ ] Notify the bank(s) — joint accounts typically remain accessible. Individual accounts
  may be frozen until estate matters are settled.
- [ ] If your spouse had a will, contact the named executor. If no will (intestate), consult
  a lawyer about estate administration.

### Short-Term Actions (This Month / This Quarter)
- [ ] File for probate if required (depends on province and asset types — see below)
- [ ] Notify CRA of the death — a final tax return must be filed
- [ ] Update your own will and Powers of Attorney (your spouse was likely your executor/POA)
- [ ] Review all beneficiary designations — RRSP, TFSA, RESP, insurance
- [ ] Apply for OAS/GIS changes (if your spouse was receiving OAS, notify Service Canada)
- [ ] Adjust your budget to single-income reality
- [ ] Claim the RRSP/RRIF rollover to your name (tax-free if you're the named beneficiary
  or beneficiary under the will — see tax implications)
- [ ] Review insurance: you may need to increase your own life insurance now

### Canadian Programs & Benefits
- **CPP Death Benefit:** One-time $2,500 payment to the estate (or eligible applicant).
  Apply within 60 days for priority processing. (Source: canada.ca/cpp-death-benefit)
- **CPP Survivor's Pension:** Monthly payment based on deceased's CPP contributions.
  - Under 65: up to ~$804/month (2026 — verify). Flat-rate portion + 37.5% of contributor's pension.
  - Over 65: up to ~$905/month (2026 — verify). 60% of contributor's pension.
  - Combined with your own CPP, capped at the maximum retirement pension.
- **CPP Children's Benefits:** ~$294/month (2026 — verify) per dependent child under 18
  (or 18-25 if in full-time school).
- **OAS/GIS:** No OAS survivor benefit per se, but as a single person your GIS eligibility
  changes (income threshold and amount differ for singles vs couples).
- **Provincial programs:** Some provinces have survivor benefits, bereavement leave (3-5 days
  job-protected in most provinces), and estate administration assistance.

### Tax Implications & Deadlines
- **Final tax return:** Must be filed by April 30 of the year following death, or 6 months
  after the date of death, whichever is later. All income from January 1 to date of death.
- **Deemed disposition:** At death, all assets are deemed sold at fair market value. This can
  trigger large capital gains taxes. Exception: assets transferred to the surviving spouse
  are "rolled over" at cost base (no immediate tax).
- **RRSP/RRIF rollover:** If you're the named beneficiary (or sole beneficiary of the estate),
  the RRSP/RRIF can be transferred to your own RRSP/RRIF tax-free. If no rollover, the full
  value is taxable on the deceased's final return.
- **TFSA:** If you're named as "successor holder" (spouse only), the TFSA continues in your
  name with no tax consequences. If named as "beneficiary," the FMV at death is tax-free but
  growth after death is taxable. If no designation, it goes through the estate.
- **Probate fees:** Vary significantly by province:
  - Ontario: 1.5% of estate value over $50,000
  - BC: 1.4% of estate value over $50,000
  - Alberta: flat $525 max
  - Quebec: $0 for notarial wills, ~$1,000 for probate
  - Saskatchewan: 0.7% of estate value
  (Verify current rates — some provinces have changed recently)
- **Rights or things return:** An optional second tax return can be filed for certain income
  items (dividends declared but not received, vacation pay owing, etc.). This can reduce
  overall tax by using a second set of personal credits.

### Impact on Existing Plan
- **Budget:** Fundamental change. One income stream gone. Life insurance proceeds may provide
  a buffer. CPP survivor pension provides ongoing income.
- **Registered accounts:** RRSP/RRIF rollover preserves tax sheltering. TFSA successor holder
  preserves tax-free status. Review RESP subscriber (can continue as surviving parent).
- **Insurance:** Life insurance claim. Review your own coverage — you may now be the sole
  earner for dependents.
- **Projections:** Complete recalculation. Retirement projection now based on single income +
  survivor pension + inherited assets.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. **Not naming a TFSA "successor holder."** Only a spouse can be named successor holder. If
   you only named them as "beneficiary," growth after death is taxable.
2. **Forgetting CPP credit splitting vs survivor pension.** These are different. Survivor
   pension is automatic on application. Credit splitting is for divorced/separated couples.
3. **Not filing the optional "rights or things" return.** This can save thousands in tax.
4. **Making major financial decisions too quickly.** Grief affects judgment. If possible, avoid
   selling the house, changing investments, or making large purchases for 6-12 months.
5. **Not updating your own will.** Your deceased spouse was likely your executor, beneficiary,
   and/or POA. Update immediately.

### When to Get Professional Help
- **Estate lawyer:** Essential for probate, especially if no will or complex assets ($2,000-$10,000+)
- **CPA:** Final tax return and estate tax planning ($500-$2,000)
- **Financial planner:** Restructure the entire financial plan as a single person
- **Grief counsellor:** Many employer EAPs cover this. Mental health affects financial decisions.

## 7. Caring for Aging Parents

### Trigger Phrases
> "My parent needs care" / "mom/dad is getting older" / "looking at long-term care" /
> "my parent can't live alone anymore" / "I'm becoming a caregiver" / "dealing with
> my parent's finances"

### Immediate Actions (This Week)
- [ ] Have a family meeting about care needs, preferences, and cost-sharing among siblings
- [ ] Check if your parent has a will, Powers of Attorney (property + personal care), and
  up-to-date beneficiary designations. If not → this is urgent.
- [ ] Review your parent's current financial situation: income sources (CPP, OAS, GIS, pension,
  savings), monthly expenses, assets, and debts
- [ ] Check if your parent qualifies for GIS (Guaranteed Income Supplement) — many eligible
  seniors don't claim it

### Short-Term Actions (This Month / This Quarter)
- [ ] Research care options in their area: home care (PSW visits), retirement home, long-term
  care facility. Waitlists for subsidized LTC can be years in some provinces.
- [ ] Apply for provincial home care programs (e.g., Ontario Home and Community Care, BC Home
  Support). These are often publicly funded based on assessment.
- [ ] Consider the Disability Tax Credit (DTC) if your parent has a prolonged physical or
  mental impairment. DTC unlocks other benefits and credits.
- [ ] Explore respite care programs — you need breaks to sustain caregiving long-term
- [ ] Evaluate the impact on YOUR financial plan — reduced work hours, career impact, retirement

### Canadian Programs & Benefits
- **Canada Caregiver Credit:** Non-refundable tax credit of $8,773 (2026 — verify) for
  supporting an infirm dependent 18+. Reduced when dependent's income exceeds $20,601
  (2026 — verify). Claimed on your tax return, not your parent's.
  (Source: canada.ca/canada-caregiver-amount)
- **Medical Expense Tax Credit:** You can claim your parent's medical expenses if they qualify
  as your dependent (income below ~$8,773, 2026 — verify). Includes nursing home fees,
  attendant care, medical devices, prescriptions.
- **GIS for your parent:** If your parent's individual income (excluding OAS) is below ~$21,768
  (2025 — verify), they may qualify for GIS. This is significant — up to ~$1,072/month
  (2025 — verify) for single seniors.
- **Provincial drug programs:** Ontario Drug Benefit (free for 65+), BC PharmaCare, Alberta
  Seniors Benefit, etc. Covers prescription drugs for seniors.
- **Veterans Affairs:** If your parent is a veteran, additional care benefits may be available.
- **EI Compassionate Care Benefits:** Up to 26 weeks at 55% if you need to take time off
  work to care for a family member who is gravely ill with a significant risk of death.
- **EI Family Caregiver Benefits:** Up to 15 weeks (adult) or 35 weeks (child under 18)
  at 55% for caring for a critically ill or injured family member.

### Tax Implications & Deadlines
- Claim the Canada Caregiver Credit on your tax return (line 30425 or 30450)
- If your parent lives with you, you may be able to claim them as an eligible dependent
  (line 30400) — can't double up with caregiver credit on same person though
- Long-term care facility fees that are "attendant care" qualify for Medical Expense Tax Credit
- Keep ALL receipts for medical expenses — the 3% of net income threshold means you need
  significant expenses before the credit kicks in

### Impact on Existing Plan
- **Budget:** Potential costs: home modifications ($5,000-$50,000), care costs ($2,000-$8,000/month
  for LTC), travel to visit/assist, reduced work income if cutting hours
- **Registered accounts:** Your own RRSP/TFSA contributions may need to reduce if funding
  parent's care
- **Insurance:** Consider whether parent has adequate insurance. Your own disability insurance
  becomes more important.
- **Projections:** Your own retirement may be delayed if caregiving reduces savings. Model
  the scenarios honestly.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. **Not getting Power of Attorney while your parent can still grant it.** Once they lose
   mental capacity, you need a court-appointed guardian (expensive and slow — $5,000-$15,000+).
2. **One sibling bearing all costs without agreement.** Have the family cost-sharing conversation
   early. Document the arrangement.
3. **Not checking GIS eligibility.** Billions in GIS goes unclaimed. If your parent's income
   is low, apply.
4. **Raiding your own retirement.** You can't pour from an empty cup. Protect your RRSP/TFSA
   contributions first, then help with surplus.
5. **Not exploring government programs.** Many families pay privately for care that's available
   publicly. Research before paying.

### When to Get Professional Help
- **Elder law attorney:** POA, guardianship, estate planning ($1,000-$5,000)
- **Geriatric care manager:** Assessment and care coordination ($100-$200/hour)
- **CPA:** Tax optimization for medical expenses and caregiver credits

## 8. Inheritance / Windfall

### Trigger Phrases
> "I inherited money" / "I received an inheritance" / "my parent left me their house" /
> "I got a large settlement" / "lottery winnings" / "unexpected large sum of money"

### Immediate Actions (This Week)
- [ ] **The #1 rule: Do nothing for at least 30 days.** Put the money in a HISA and resist
  all urges to spend, invest, or make decisions. Grief (if from a death) or excitement
  (if from a windfall) impairs financial judgment.
- [ ] Tell as few people as possible. Windfalls attract requests.
- [ ] If you inherited property, understand the tax implications before selling (see below)
- [ ] If you inherited an RRSP/RRIF, understand the tax treatment (see below — this is critical)

### Short-Term Actions (This Month / This Quarter)
- [ ] After the 30-day cooling period, assess: what are your highest-impact uses?
  Priority order: (1) eliminate high-interest debt, (2) fill emergency fund, (3) maximize
  TFSA, (4) maximize RRSP if beneficial, (5) invest remainder
- [ ] Check your TFSA and RRSP contribution room before contributing — over-contributions
  are penalized 1%/month
- [ ] If the amount is large ($100K+), consider consulting a fee-only financial planner
- [ ] For very large amounts ($500K+), strongly recommend a fee-only advisor
- [ ] If inherited property: decide whether to keep, rent, or sell. Each has different tax
  implications.

### Canadian Programs & Benefits
- **No inheritance tax in Canada.** The estate pays any taxes owed (deemed disposition at death).
  You receive the inheritance tax-free. However:
  - Inherited RRSP/RRIF: If you're NOT the spouse, the full value is taxable on the deceased's
    final return (or to you if the estate doesn't have enough assets to pay)
  - Inherited TFSA: FMV at death is tax-free. Any growth AFTER death is taxable to you.
  - Inherited property: Your cost base is the FMV at the date of death. Future gains above
    that are yours to claim.
- **TFSA Successor Holder:** If your deceased spouse named you as successor holder, the TFSA
  transfers to you seamlessly with no tax consequences and no room impact.

### Tax Implications & Deadlines
- **Cash inheritance:** Not taxable. But income earned ON the inheritance (interest, dividends,
  capital gains) is taxable going forward.
- **Inherited RRSP/RRIF (not spouse):** Fully taxable to the estate on the final return.
  If you're a financially dependent child/grandchild, it can be rolled to your RRSP or RDSP.
- **Inherited property:** Cost base = FMV at date of death. If you sell later for more, you
  pay capital gains on the increase. If you move in and make it your principal residence,
  future gains may be exempt.
- **Inherited stocks/investments:** Cost base = FMV at date of death. ACB resets.
- **Life insurance proceeds:** Tax-free to the named beneficiary.
- **Lottery/gambling winnings:** Tax-free in Canada (unless you gamble professionally).
- **Legal settlement:** Depends on the type. Personal injury settlements are generally tax-free.
  Employment-related settlements may be taxable.

### Impact on Existing Plan
- **Budget:** Windfall should NOT inflate lifestyle spending. Direct it to the priority list
  (debt → emergency fund → registered accounts → investing).
- **Registered accounts:** Check TFSA room ($7,000/year accumulation since age 18 or 2009).
  Check RRSP room. Don't over-contribute.
- **Insurance:** If the windfall eliminates debt, your life insurance need may decrease.
- **Projections:** Recalculate retirement projection with the additional assets. A $200K
  inheritance at age 35 invested at 7% is worth ~$1.5M by age 65.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. **Lifestyle inflation.** The #1 windfall destroyer. A $200K inheritance spent on a car,
   vacation, and home renovation is gone in months. Invested, it's $1.5M at retirement.
2. **Not checking TFSA/RRSP room.** Over-contributions are penalized. Check CRA My Account first.
3. **Assuming inherited RRSP is tax-free.** If you're not the spouse, it's fully taxable to the estate.
4. **Telling everyone.** Family, friends, and strangers suddenly have great investment ideas
   and urgent needs. Be private.
5. **Investing all at once in something you don't understand.** If you're not experienced,
   a simple all-in-one ETF (VBAL, VGRO, VEQT) in a TFSA is almost always the right first move.
6. **Not getting professional advice for large amounts.** $500K+ warrants a fee-only advisor
   ($1,000-$3,000 for a comprehensive plan).

### When to Get Professional Help
- **Fee-only financial planner:** For amounts over $100K ($1,500-$3,000 for a plan)
- **CPA:** For inherited RRSP/RRIF tax implications, estate income tax
- **Estate lawyer:** If the inheritance is contested or complex (multiple beneficiaries, property)

## 9. Moving Provinces

> **Moving TO or FROM Canada** (immigration, emigration, snowbirding)? Read
> `references/cross-border.md` for departure tax, newcomer onboarding, or return-to-Canada guidance.
> This section covers moves **within** Canada only.

### Trigger Phrases
> "We're moving to [province]" / "relocating to [city in different province]" / "transferring
> to [province]" / "thinking about moving provinces"

### Immediate Actions (This Week)
- [ ] Understand the tax implication: your province of residence on December 31 determines
  which province's tax rates apply for the ENTIRE year. Moving in December vs January can
  make a significant tax difference.
- [ ] Research the new province's tax rates — the differences are substantial:
  - Alberta: flat 10% provincial rate (lowest for high earners)
  - Ontario: 5.05% to 13.16%
  - Quebec: 14% to 25.75% (highest, but includes QPP, QPIP, and many public services)
  - BC: 5.06% to 20.5%
- [ ] Start the health card transfer process — most provinces have a 3-month waiting period.
  Keep your old province's health card active during the transition.

### Short-Term Actions (This Month / This Quarter)
- [ ] Apply for the new province's health card immediately upon arrival
- [ ] Update your address with CRA (this triggers benefit program transitions)
- [ ] Register for new provincial benefit programs (each province has different programs)
- [ ] If buying property in the new province, research land/property transfer tax:
  - BC: 1-3% (plus foreign buyer tax if applicable)
  - Ontario: 0.5-2.5% (plus Toronto municipal LTT if applicable)
  - Alberta: relatively low fixed fees
  - Quebec: "welcome tax" (droits de mutation) 0.5-3%
- [ ] Update driver's license and vehicle registration (most provinces require within 90 days)
- [ ] Notify your employer — payroll tax withholdings will change

### Canadian Programs & Benefits
- **Health card:** Apply in new province immediately. 3-month wait in most provinces (covered
  by old province during transition). Quebec has no waiting period if coming from another
  province.
- **CCB:** Continues unchanged — federal program, not provincial.
- **Provincial benefits transition:**
  - Ontario Trillium Benefit → BC Climate Action Tax Credit (or equivalent)
  - Ontario Drug Benefit → BC PharmaCare (or equivalent)
  - Provincial child benefits vary significantly
- **Quebec specifics:** QPP instead of CPP (separate but similar), QPIP instead of federal EI
  for parental benefits, QST in addition to GST, Revenu Québec (separate tax return required).

### Tax Implications & Deadlines
- **December 31 rule:** Province of residence on Dec 31 = provincial tax rate for the year.
  If you're moving from a high-tax province to a low-tax province, timing the move before
  Dec 31 saves money. Moving FROM a low-tax to high-tax: consider January.
- **RRSP/TFSA/RESP:** Completely unaffected by province moves. Federal programs.
- **Property transfer tax:** Paid when buying in the new province. Can be significant.
- **Quebec requires a separate provincial tax return** (Revenu Québec). All other provinces
  are on the federal return.
- **Moving expenses deduction:** If you're moving for work (40+ km closer to new workplace),
  moving expenses are tax-deductible. Includes travel, temporary housing, legal fees on
  selling/buying, utility hookups. Claimed against income earned at the new location.

### Impact on Existing Plan
- **Budget:** Housing costs vary dramatically by province/city. Tax take-home changes.
  Provincial insurance rates differ. Cost of living adjustment needed.
- **Registered accounts:** No impact on RRSP, TFSA, RESP, FHSA.
- **Insurance:** Auto insurance rates vary by province (BC is public, Ontario is private, etc.).
  Health coverage gaps during 3-month transition.
- **Projections:** Tax rate changes affect retirement projections. Provincial benefits change.
  If moving to lower-tax province, retirement income stretches further.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. **Not timing the move around Dec 31.** This one date determines your entire year's provincial
   tax rate. Moving from Ontario to Alberta on Dec 30 vs Jan 2 can save thousands.
2. **Forgetting to apply for new province's health card immediately.** The 3-month clock starts
   when you apply, not when you arrive.
3. **Not researching property transfer tax before buying.** BC and Ontario LTT can be $10,000+
   on a typical home. Budget for this.
4. **Not updating CRA address.** Benefits get sent to old address and programs don't transition.
5. **Moving to Quebec without understanding the separate tax return.** Quebec has its own tax
   system. Budget for the additional complexity (and potentially higher fees for tax prep).

### When to Get Professional Help
- **CPA:** If moving between significantly different tax jurisdictions, especially to/from Quebec.
- **Real estate lawyer:** For property transactions in the new province.

## 10. Buying First Home

### Trigger Phrases
> "We want to buy a house" / "looking at buying our first home" / "saving for a down payment" /
> "ready to buy a place" / "should we rent or buy?"

### Immediate Actions (This Week)
- [ ] Check your FHSA status — if you haven't opened one, do it NOW. You can contribute $8,000/year
  (tax-deductible, like an RRSP) and withdraw tax-free for a qualifying home purchase.
  Lifetime limit: $40,000.
- [ ] Check your RRSP balance and the Home Buyers' Plan (HBP) — you can withdraw up to $60,000
  per person ($120,000 per couple) from your RRSP tax-free for a first home. Must repay over
  15 years starting 2 years after withdrawal.
- [ ] Get a mortgage pre-approval to understand what you qualify for (doesn't commit you)
- [ ] Start tracking your spending if you haven't — lenders look at your financial habits

### Short-Term Actions (This Month / This Quarter)
- [ ] Calculate your total available down payment:
  - Savings + FHSA + HBP (RRSP withdrawal) = total
  - A couple can combine: up to $80K FHSA ($40K each) + $120K HBP ($60K each) = $200K
- [ ] Understand down payment requirements:
  - 5% on first $500,000
  - 10% on $500,001 to $1,500,000
  - 20% required above $1,500,000 (or if amortization > 25 years for non-first-time buyers)
- [ ] Budget for closing costs (1.5-4% of purchase price): land transfer tax, legal fees,
  home inspection, title insurance, moving costs, utility connections
- [ ] Get mortgage rate quotes from multiple lenders (banks, credit unions, mortgage brokers)
- [ ] Consider the stress test: you must qualify at the higher of your contract rate + 2% or
  the benchmark rate (currently ~5.25% — verify). This limits how much you can borrow.

### Canadian Programs & Benefits
- **First Home Savings Account (FHSA):** $8,000/year, $40,000 lifetime. Tax-deductible
  contributions (like RRSP) + tax-free withdrawals for a qualifying home (like TFSA).
  Unused room carries forward (max 1 year, so max $16,000 in one year). Must be open for
  at least 1 year before withdrawal. Account must be closed within 15 years or age 71.
  (Source: canada.ca/first-home-savings-account)
- **Home Buyers' Plan (HBP):** Withdraw up to $60,000 from your RRSP tax-free for a first home.
  Must repay 1/15th per year starting the second year after withdrawal. If you don't repay,
  the missed amount is added to your taxable income. (Source: canada.ca/home-buyers-plan)
- **First-Time Home Buyer Tax Credit:** $10,000 non-refundable credit (15% = $1,500 federal
  tax reduction). Claim on your tax return for the year of purchase.
- **GST/HST New Housing Rebate:** If buying new construction, you may qualify for a rebate
  of up to $6,300 federal (plus provincial). Purchase price must be under $450,000 for full
  rebate (partial up to $350,000-$450,000, scales down).
- **CMHC Mortgage Insurance:** Required if down payment < 20%. Premiums:
  - 5-9.99% down: 4.00% of mortgage amount
  - 10-14.99% down: 3.10%
  - 15-19.99% down: 2.80%
  - Add 0.20% if amortization is 26-30 years (first-time buyers/new builds eligible)
  Premium is added to the mortgage and amortized with your payments.
  (Source: cmhc-schl.gc.ca/mortgage-loan-insurance-cost)
- **Land Transfer Tax:** Varies by province. First-time buyer rebates available in some:
  - Ontario: up to $4,000 rebate for first-time buyers
  - Toronto: additional municipal LTT (up to $4,475 rebate for first-time buyers)
  - BC: exemption on homes up to $500,000 (partial up to $525,000) for first-time buyers

### Tax Implications & Deadlines
- FHSA contributions are tax-deductible (like RRSP). Can carry forward unused deductions.
- HBP withdrawal is tax-free but must be repaid over 15 years. Missed repayments = taxable income.
- FHSA must be open for at least 1 year before qualifying withdrawal — open it now even if
  you're not buying soon.
- Land transfer tax is due on closing (not tax-deductible for primary residence).
- Mortgage interest is NOT tax-deductible in Canada (unlike the US). This is a common misconception.
- Property tax is deductible only if you have rental income from the property.

### Impact on Existing Plan
- **Budget:** Major change. Add: mortgage, property tax, home insurance, maintenance (1-3% of
  home value/year), utilities, condo fees if applicable. Remove: rent.
- **Registered accounts:** FHSA emptied for down payment. RRSP reduced if using HBP (must
  repay). TFSA may be used for additional down payment.
- **Insurance:** Need home insurance. Review life insurance (mortgage payoff coverage).
- **Projections:** Home equity becomes a significant asset. Mortgage is a long-term liability.
  Recalculate net worth and retirement projections.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. **Not opening an FHSA immediately.** The 1-year minimum holding period means every day you
   wait is a day closer to needing the money but not being able to access it.
2. **Using HBP without a repayment plan.** Many people forget to repay and get hit with
   unexpected tax bills year after year. Set up automatic payments.
3. **Not budgeting for closing costs.** On a $600K home, closing costs can be $15,000-$25,000
   (land transfer tax alone in Toronto can be $8,000+).
4. **Buying at the top of your qualification.** Just because you qualify for $700K doesn't mean
   you should spend $700K. The stress test exists because rates can go up.
5. **Skipping the home inspection.** A $500 inspection can save you from a $50,000 foundation
   problem.
6. **Not comparing mortgage rates.** A 0.25% difference on a $500K mortgage over 25 years is
   ~$17,000 in interest. Use a broker or get at least 3 quotes.
7. **Forgetting that mortgage interest is NOT deductible in Canada.** Don't compare Canadian
   mortgage costs to US advice that assumes deductibility.

### When to Get Professional Help
- **Mortgage broker:** Free to you (paid by the lender). Shops multiple lenders for the best rate.
- **Real estate lawyer:** Required for closing ($1,000-$2,000). Can review the purchase agreement.
- **Home inspector:** Required for peace of mind ($400-$600). Non-negotiable.

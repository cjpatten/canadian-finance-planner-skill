## 2. Rent vs Buy Analysis Framework

This is one of the biggest financial decisions Canadians face. Don't answer with opinion —
show the math.

### Total Cost of Ownership (Monthly)
Calculate ALL costs of buying, not just the mortgage:

```
Monthly mortgage payment (principal + interest)
+ Property tax (annual / 12)
+ Home insurance (~$100-$250/month)
+ Maintenance reserve (1-3% of home value / 12)
+ Utilities (if not currently paying as renter)
+ Condo fees (if applicable)
- Principal portion of mortgage payment (this is forced savings, not a cost)
= True monthly cost of ownership
```

Compare this to: rent + tenant insurance + investing the down payment at expected returns.

### Opportunity Cost of Down Payment
The down payment is the hidden cost of buying. If you invest $100,000 instead of using it
as a down payment:
- At 7% average return: ~$197K after 10 years, ~$387K after 20 years
- This growth is the "cost" of tying that money up in a house
- However, home equity also grows (historically 3-5% in Canada, varies enormously by market)

### Break-Even Timeline
At current rates (2026), the break-even point where buying beats renting is typically
**4-6 years** in most Canadian markets. (Source: WOWA.ca, CPA Canada — verify)

Factors that shorten break-even: low interest rates, fast-appreciation market, low rent alternative
Factors that lengthen break-even: high interest rates, expensive market, cheap rent alternative

### Price-to-Rent Ratio
A quick shortcut: divide the home price by the annual rent for a similar place.
- **Below 15:** Buying strongly favored
- **15-20:** Buying slightly favored
- **20-25:** Roughly equal — depends on personal factors
- **Above 25:** Renting likely better financially (common in Vancouver, Toronto)

### When Renting Wins
- Staying less than 5 years (transaction costs eat the equity gains)
- Expensive market with high price-to-rent ratio
- No down payment saved (CMHC insurance adds 2.8-4% to the mortgage)
- Career mobility needed (may need to move for work)
- Debt that should be paid off first
- Want to invest the down payment in higher-return assets

### When Buying Wins
- Staying 5+ years
- Have 10-20%+ down payment
- Stable career and location
- Building forced savings through equity
- Want control over the space (renovations, pets, stability)
- Local market has reasonable price-to-rent ratio
- Emotional value of homeownership (this is real and valid)

### How to Present This to the User
Run the numbers for THEIR specific situation. Don't default to "buying is always better" —
that's Canadian cultural bias, not financial analysis. Show both scenarios with their actual
numbers and let them decide.

## 3. Mortgage Fundamentals

### Fixed vs Variable Rates
| Feature | Fixed Rate | Variable Rate |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| Rate (March 2026) | ~3.89-4.5% (verify) | ~3.35-3.70% (verify) |
| Rate changes | Locked for the term | Moves with Bank of Canada rate |
| Payment | Same every month | Payment may change (or stays same but allocation shifts) |
| Penalty to break | Greater of 3 months interest OR IRD (can be very expensive) | 3 months interest only |
| Best for | Risk-averse, tight budget, stable rates | Comfortable with risk, historically saves money |

**Historical note:** Variable rates have been cheaper than fixed rates about 80% of the time
over the last 25 years. But the 2022-2023 rate spike showed the downside — some variable-rate
borrowers saw payments jump 40%+. (Source: Bank of Canada historical data)

### Amortization Impact
On a $400,000 mortgage at 5%:

| Amortization | Monthly Payment | Total Interest | Interest Saved vs 25yr |
|-------------|----------------|---------------|----------------------|
| 15 years | $3,163 | $169,400 | $202,800 |
| 20 years | $2,639 | $233,400 | $138,800 |
| 25 years | $2,338 | $301,300 | — |
| 30 years | $2,147 | $372,200 | -$70,900 more |

(2026 — verify with current rates. These illustrate the relationship, not exact numbers.)

**30-year amortization:** Available for first-time buyers and new construction purchases with
insured mortgages (as of Dec 2024). Adds 0.20% to CMHC insurance premium. Lower payments
but significantly more interest over the life of the mortgage.

### Open vs Closed
- **Closed:** Lower rate, but penalties for breaking the mortgage early. Most common.
- **Open:** Higher rate (usually 1-2% more), but can pay off anytime without penalty. Useful
  if you expect to sell soon or receive a windfall.
- **Convertible:** Start open, convert to closed at any time. Some lenders offer this.

### Insured vs Uninsured
- **Insured (< 20% down):** CMHC mortgage insurance required. Insurance premium: 2.80-4.00%
  of the mortgage amount (added to the loan). Insured mortgages often get slightly better
  interest rates because the lender's risk is lower.
- **Uninsured (20%+ down):** No insurance required. Slightly higher rates in some cases.
  Maximum amortization is 25 years (30-year only available for insured).

### Stress Test
All borrowers must qualify at the **higher of:**
- Their contract interest rate + 2%, OR
- The benchmark qualifying rate (currently 5.25% — verify)

This means if your rate is 4%, you must prove you can afford payments at 6%. This limits
how much you can borrow and is designed to protect against rate increases.

**GDS (Gross Debt Service):** Housing costs (mortgage + property tax + heating + 50% of condo
fees) must be ≤ 32% of gross household income.

**TDS (Total Debt Service):** Housing costs + all other debt payments must be ≤ 40% of gross
household income.

### Mortgage Broker vs Bank
- **Broker:** Shops 30+ lenders for you. Free to the borrower (paid by the lender). Often
  gets better rates than walking into a bank. No obligation.
- **Bank:** Limited to their own products. May offer perks (rate discounts for bundling accounts).
  Relationship matters if you have complex finances.
- **Recommendation:** Always get a broker quote AND a bank quote. Use whichever is better.

(Source: canada.ca/FCAC, ratehub.ca)

## 4. Prepayment & Acceleration Strategies

### Bi-Weekly vs Monthly Payments
- Monthly: 12 payments/year
- Bi-weekly: 26 payments/year = equivalent of 13 monthly payments
- **Impact:** On a $400K mortgage at 5%, bi-weekly payments save ~$30,000 in interest and
  pay off the mortgage ~3 years early. This costs nothing — just changes payment frequency.
- **Accelerated bi-weekly:** Even better — takes the monthly payment, divides by 2, pays that
  every 2 weeks. Slightly larger than true bi-weekly. Most impactful free strategy.

### Lump Sum Prepayments
Most closed mortgages allow 10-20% of the original principal as an annual lump sum prepayment
without penalty. Check your mortgage contract.
- Example: $400K mortgage, 15% prepayment privilege = $60,000/year you can put down penalty-free.
- Best timing: make lump sum payments early in the year (or early in the mortgage) when the
  balance is highest — more interest savings.
- Sources: tax refunds, bonuses, inheritance, selling assets.

### Increasing Regular Payments
Most mortgages allow you to increase your regular payment by 10-20% once per year.
- Example: $2,338/month → increase by 10% to $2,572/month = $234/month extra toward principal.
- This is "set and forget" — more effective than relying on annual lump sums because it's automatic.

### The Math on Extra Payments
Extra $200/month on a $400K mortgage at 5%, 25-year amortization:
- **Saves ~$45,000 in interest**
- **Pays off 4+ years early**
- This is one of the highest-impact, lowest-risk financial moves a homeowner can make.

### Prepayment Penalties (If Breaking the Mortgage)

**Variable rate:** Penalty = **3 months of interest.** Usually manageable.
- Example: $400K balance at 5% → 3 months interest = $5,000.

**Fixed rate:** Penalty = **Greater of 3 months interest OR the Interest Rate Differential (IRD).**
The IRD can be massive.
- IRD = balance × (your rate - current comparable rate) × remaining term
- Example: $300K balance, your rate 5%, current 3-year rate 3%, 3 years remaining
  → IRD = $300K × 2% × 3 = **$18,000**
- This is why breaking a fixed-rate mortgage can cost $10,000-$30,000+.
- Before breaking: calculate whether refinancing savings exceed the penalty over the new term.

(Source: canada.ca/FCAC mortgage fees, ratehub.ca)

## 5. Mortgage Renewal & Negotiation

### The Renewal Process
- Lender sends a renewal notice **~120 days** before your term ends.
- The notice offers a new rate and term. **DO NOT just sign and return it.**
- If you do nothing, the bank auto-renews at their **posted rate** — which is almost always
  higher than what you could negotiate or find elsewhere.

### Rate Shopping Strategy
1. **Start 30-60 days before renewal.** Get quotes from at least 3 sources: your current
   lender, a mortgage broker, and one other bank/credit union.
2. **Present competing offers** to your current lender. Most will match or beat to keep you.
3. **Ask about the "retention rate"** — the best rate your current lender offers to keep you.
   This is usually not the first offer they make.
4. **Consider switching lenders.** At renewal, there's NO penalty to switch (your term has ended).
   If switching an insured mortgage, there's no new stress test required.

### Renewal Shock (2026)
Many Canadians who locked in at 2-3% during 2020-2022 are renewing at 4-5% in 2026.
This can mean a **~20% increase in monthly payments.** (Source: mortgagesandbox.com — verify)

Prepare the user: calculate their new payment at current rates and show the budget impact.
If the increase is unaffordable, options include:
- Extending the amortization (back to 25 or 30 years) to reduce payments
- Switching to variable rate if it's lower
- Making lifestyle adjustments to absorb the increase
- In extreme cases: consider selling if the home is no longer affordable

### Refinancing vs Renewal
| | Renewal | Refinancing |
|--|---------|-------------|
| When | At the end of your term | Anytime (penalties apply if mid-term) |
| Stress test | Not required if staying with same lender (insured) | Required |
| Can change amortization | Sometimes (lender discretion) | Yes |
| Can access equity | No | Yes (borrow more against home value) |
| Penalty | None (term has ended) | Yes (3 months or IRD if mid-term) |

(Source: ratehub.ca, FCAC)

## 6. Home Equity Strategies

### HELOC (Home Equity Line of Credit)
- **Rate:** Typically prime + 0.5% to prime + 2% (variable). Currently ~5-7% (verify).
- **Maximum:** Can borrow up to 65% of home value via HELOC. Total borrowing (mortgage + HELOC)
  capped at 80% of home value.
- **Payments:** Interest-only minimum payments. Principal repayment at your pace.
- **Revolving:** Borrow, repay, re-borrow — like a giant credit card secured by your home.

**Good uses:**
- Emergency fund backup (have it available, don't use it unless needed)
- Renovations that increase home value
- Debt consolidation (moving 20% CC debt to 6% HELOC — saves significant interest)
- Bridge financing (buying new home before selling old one)

**Bad uses:**
- Funding lifestyle spending (vacations, cars, consumer goods)
- Investing with borrowed money unless you understand the risk (Smith Manoeuvre)
- Using it because "it's cheap money" without a repayment plan

**Critical warning:** A HELOC converts unsecured debt to **secured debt backed by your home.**
If you consolidate $30K in credit card debt into a HELOC and can't repay, you could lose
your home. With credit cards, you'd damage your credit score but keep your house.

### Refinancing Decision Tree
1. Calculate the penalty to break your current mortgage (3 months interest or IRD)
2. Calculate monthly savings with the new rate × remaining months
3. If savings > penalty → refinancing makes sense
4. Also consider: accessing equity, consolidating debt, changing amortization

**Break-even formula:** Penalty ÷ monthly savings = months to recover.
If break-even is less than your new term length, refinancing is worthwhile.

### Reverse Mortgage (CHIP by HomeEquity Bank)
For Canadians **55 and older** who want to access home equity without selling.

- **How it works:** Borrow up to 55% of home value. No regular payments required. Loan is
  repaid when you sell, move, or pass away.
- **Rates:** ~7.24-7.36% fixed, ~7.36-7.80% variable (2025 — verify). Higher than traditional
  mortgages because of the no-payment feature.
- **Closing fee:** ~$1,795 (deducted from proceeds, not out-of-pocket).
- **No negative equity guarantee:** If home value drops below loan balance, HomeEquity Bank
  absorbs the difference. You (or your estate) never owe more than the home is worth.
- **Eligibility:** 55+, primary residence, home worth $200K+ (some areas $250K+), property
  taxes up to date, home in good condition.

**Best for:** Seniors who are house-rich but cash-poor, want to age in place, and don't want
to sell or take on monthly payments.

**Consider alternatives first:**
- HELOC (lower rate, but requires monthly payments)
- Downsizing (sell, buy smaller, invest the difference)
- Renting out part of the home (basement suite income)
- Government programs (GIS top-up, provincial benefits)

**Caution:** At 7%+ compounding with no payments, the loan balance grows fast. A $200K reverse
mortgage at 7% becomes $394K after 10 years. Show this math to the user.

(Source: chip.ca, HomeEquity Bank)

## 7. Investment Property

### Cap Rate (Capitalization Rate)
Cap Rate = (Net Operating Income / Property Value) × 100

- **Above 5%:** Generally considered a good investment
- **3-5%:** Acceptable in expensive markets (Toronto, Vancouver)
- **Below 3%:** Likely not worth the risk and hassle

Net Operating Income = Annual rental income - operating expenses (property tax, insurance,
maintenance, vacancy, property management). Does NOT include mortgage payments.

### Monthly Cash Flow Analysis
```
Gross rental income
- Vacancy allowance (5-10% of gross rent — properties are not 100% occupied)
- Property management (8-12% of gross rent, or $0 if self-managing)
- Property tax
- Insurance
- Maintenance/repairs (budget 1-2% of property value/year)
- Utilities (if landlord pays)
- Mortgage payment (principal + interest)
= Monthly cash flow (positive = good, negative = "feeding the beast")
```

**Negative cash flow** means you're paying out of pocket every month to own the property.
This can work if the property is appreciating fast enough, but it's risky and ties up cash.

### Tax Treatment of Rental Income (Source: canada.ca T4036 guide)

**Deductible expenses (against rental income):**
- Mortgage **interest** (not principal repayment)
- Property tax
- Insurance
- Repairs and maintenance (not capital improvements)
- Advertising for tenants
- Property management fees
- Accounting and legal fees
- Travel to the property (at CRA mileage rate)
- Utilities (if landlord pays)
- Condo fees (if applicable)

**NOT deductible:**
- Mortgage principal repayment (this is paying down your asset, not an expense)
- Capital improvements (new roof, renovation — add to ACB instead)
- Your own labour

### Capital Cost Allowance (CCA)
- Rental buildings are Class 1 property: **4% declining balance** CCA rate.
- You CAN claim CCA to reduce rental income to zero.
- You CANNOT use CCA to create or increase a rental loss.
- **CCA recapture on sale:** If you claimed CCA and sell for more than the depreciated value,
  the recapture amount is added to your income as **100% taxable** (not at the 50% capital
  gains inclusion rate). This is a common surprise.
- **Strategic choice:** Many advisors recommend NOT claiming CCA on residential rental property
  to avoid the recapture problem on sale. The tax deferral benefit is usually not worth the
  future recapture hit unless you plan to hold indefinitely.

### Capital Gains on Sale
- Investment property does NOT qualify for the principal residence exemption.
- Capital gain = sale price - ACB (original purchase price + capital improvements - CCA claimed)
  - closing costs.
- 50% of the gain is included in your taxable income (verify — capital gains inclusion rate
  has been debated in recent budgets).
- If you change the use of a property (from rental to personal or vice versa), it's a
  **deemed disposition** — you calculate the capital gain at that point as if you sold it.

### When to Incorporate
- For 1-2 rental properties: usually NOT worth incorporating. Corporate tax on passive rental
  income is high (~50% in most provinces). You lose the personal tax integration benefit.
- For 3+ properties generating significant income: incorporation may make sense for liability
  protection and income splitting. Consult a CPA.

(Source: canada.ca/rental-income, T4036 guide)

## 8. Condo vs Freehold

### Condo Fees
- **Range:** $300-$1,000+/month depending on building age, amenities, and size.
- **What fees cover:** Building insurance, common area maintenance (lobby, hallways, elevator,
  parking garage, landscaping), reserve fund contribution, sometimes water/heat.
- **What fees DON'T cover:** Your unit's interior maintenance, your contents/liability insurance,
  in-unit appliance replacement, upgrades you want.
- **Fees increase:** Typically 2-5% per year. Older buildings = higher increases due to
  deferred maintenance.

### Special Assessments
- When the reserve fund is insufficient for a major repair (e.g., parking garage waterproofing,
  elevator replacement, building envelope repair), the condo board levies a special assessment.
- **Can range from $5,000 to $50,000+ per unit.** Some buildings have had $100K+ assessments.
- **Due on short notice** (30-90 days typically). Must be paid — it's not optional.
- **How to protect yourself:** Review the reserve fund study before buying. Well-funded
  reserve = lower risk of surprise assessments.

### Reserve Fund Study
- Required every 3 years in most provinces (Ontario, BC).
- Shows: current reserve fund balance, projected major expenses (30-year plan), whether the
  fund is adequately funded, and recommended contribution increases.
- **Red flags:** reserve fund less than 25% of projected needs, large upcoming expenses not
  funded, board recommendations to increase fees significantly.

### Status Certificate (Ontario) / Form B (BC) / Information Certificate
- **Must review before buying a condo.** Your lawyer should examine this.
- **Cost:** ~$100
- **What it shows:** Reserve fund balance and study, financial statements, pending litigation,
  special assessments (past and planned), insurance coverage, rules and bylaws, minutes of
  recent board meetings.
- **Red flags:** low reserve fund, pending lawsuits against the corporation, recent large
  special assessments, high unit owner arrears, insurance claims history.

### Freehold Comparison
| Factor | Condo | Freehold |
|--------|-------|----------|
| Monthly fees | $300-$1,000+ | $0 (but you pay all maintenance directly) |
| Maintenance responsibility | Common areas handled by board | 100% yours |
| Special assessments | Possible | N/A (but surprise repairs happen too) |
| Board approval | Needed for renovations, pets, rentals (depends on rules) | Full control |
| Amenities | Pool, gym, concierge (included in fees) | Only what you build/buy |
| Insurance | Building covered by corp, you cover unit | All on you |
| Best for | Low-maintenance lifestyle, urban, first-time buyers | Families, control, space |

## 9. Principal Residence Exemption (PRE)

### What It Is
Your **primary home** is exempt from capital gains tax when you sell it. This is one of the
most valuable tax benefits in Canada — on a home that appreciated $300K, this saves $22,500+
in tax (at 50% inclusion rate and ~30% marginal rate).

### Rules
- Only **ONE property per family** (you + spouse/common-law partner) can be designated as
  principal residence per year.
- You must "ordinarily inhabit" the property during the year (no minimum days, but must be
  your actual home).
- Applies to: houses, condos, cottages, mobile homes, even houseboats if ordinarily inhabited.
- Does NOT apply to: investment/rental properties, vacant land (unless it's the land under
  your home), properties you've never lived in.

### Reporting & Election
- **Form T2091** (Designation of a Property as a Principal Residence): Not always required on
  sale, but CRA requires reporting the sale of your principal residence on your tax return
  (Schedule 3). Failing to report can result in the exemption being denied.
- If you own multiple properties (home + cottage), you choose which property to designate for
  each year to minimize total capital gains across both.

### Change of Use
- If you **start renting** your home, it's treated as a deemed disposition (you're considered
  to have sold it at fair market value and immediately reacquired it as a rental property).
  This triggers a capital gain calculation.
- **Section 45(2) election:** You can elect to defer the change-of-use deemed disposition
  for up to **4 years** (continue to treat it as your principal residence even though you've
  moved out and are renting it). Conditions: you don't claim CCA on it, and you don't
  designate another property as your principal residence during this period.
- If you **stop renting** and move back in, similar deemed disposition rules apply (with a
  section 45(3) election available).

### Partial Business Use
- If you use part of your home for business (home office), the PRE generally still applies to
  the **entire** property — as long as:
  - The business-use portion is not the dominant use (CRA generally accepts up to ~50%)
  - You do NOT claim CCA (capital cost allowance) on the business portion
  - There's no structural change to accommodate the business
- If you claim CCA on the home office portion, **that portion permanently loses the PRE.**
  This is a common and expensive mistake.

### Cottage / Vacation Property
- You can designate your cottage as your principal residence for some years (and your home
  for the remaining years) to split the exemption and minimize total capital gains.
- This requires careful year-by-year calculation. The formula considers the gain on each
  property and the optimal allocation of designation years.
- **Complex situation — recommend consulting a CPA** for optimal designation strategy.

(Source: canada.ca, T2091, Income Tax Act section 54)

## 10. Home Maintenance & Insurance

### Maintenance Budgeting
- **Rule of thumb:** Budget 1-3% of home value per year for maintenance.
- $500,000 home = $5,000-$15,000/year ($417-$1,250/month)
- New homes (< 5 years): budget 1%. Older homes (20+ years): budget 2-3%.

### Major System Lifespans & Costs (2025 — verify)
| System | Lifespan | Replacement Cost |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Asphalt roof | 20-25 years | $8,000-$15,000 |
| Furnace | 15-20 years | $3,000-$7,000 |
| Air conditioning | 15-20 years | $3,000-$6,000 |
| Water heater | 10-15 years | $1,000-$3,000 |
| Windows (full house) | 15-20 years | $8,000-$20,000 |
| Exterior siding | 25-40 years | $10,000-$25,000 |
| Deck | 15-25 years | $5,000-$15,000 |
| Driveway (asphalt) | 15-20 years | $3,000-$8,000 |
| Kitchen renovation | N/A | $15,000-$50,000 |
| Bathroom renovation | N/A | $10,000-$30,000 |

### Seasonal Maintenance Calendar
**Spring:** Roof inspection, clean gutters, check foundation for cracks, inspect exterior
(siding, paint, caulking), service AC, check sump pump.

**Summer:** Deck maintenance, exterior painting if needed, landscaping.

**Fall:** Furnace service/filter, winterize exterior (disconnect hoses, close outdoor taps),
clean gutters again, check weatherstripping, drain water heater.

**Winter:** Monitor for ice dams, check attic for condensation, test smoke/CO detectors.

**Annually:** Replace HVAC filters (every 3 months), test smoke detectors, clean dryer vent,
inspect plumbing for leaks.

### Property Insurance

**Homeowner's insurance:**
- **Dwelling coverage:** Based on replacement cost (NOT market value). The cost to rebuild
  your home if destroyed. Get an appraisal — many people are underinsured.
- **Contents coverage:** Your belongings. Typically 50-70% of dwelling coverage. Keep an
  inventory with photos.
- **Liability coverage:** Protects if someone is injured on your property. Minimum $1 million
  recommended, $2 million better.
- **Loss of use:** Covers temporary living expenses if your home is uninhabitable.

**Condo owner's insurance:**
- The condo corporation's policy covers the building structure and common areas.
- YOU need a policy for: your unit's improvements/upgrades, contents, liability, and your
  share of the building's deductible (can be $25,000-$100,000+).
- **Condo deductible assignment:** If a claim originates in your unit (e.g., water leak), the
  building's deductible may be assigned to you. Make sure your policy covers this.

**Tenant insurance:**
- Covers: your personal belongings, liability, and additional living expenses if displaced.
- Cost: ~$20-$40/month. Often required by landlord.
- **Strongly recommend even if landlord doesn't require it.** Landlord's insurance covers the
  building, not your stuff.

**Saving on premiums:**
- Bundle home + auto with same insurer (10-15% discount typical)
- Higher deductible = lower premium (but more out-of-pocket per claim)
- Claims-free discount (ask about loyalty discounts at renewal)
- Smart home devices (water leak detectors, security systems) may qualify for discounts

## 11. Downsizing Strategy

### When to Consider Downsizing
- **Retirement:** Lower housing costs free up cash for retirement income.
- **Empty nest:** Kids moved out, maintaining a large home is unnecessary.
- **Health/mobility:** Single-level living, closer to amenities/healthcare.
- **Cost reduction:** Lower property tax, utilities, maintenance, insurance.
- **Equity access:** Sell high, buy low, invest the difference.

### Financial Analysis
```
Current home sale price
- Realtor commission (typically 5% of sale price)
- Legal fees ($1,000-$2,000)
- Staging/repairs for sale ($2,000-$10,000)
- Mortgage payout (if any) + discharge fee ($200-$400)
= Net proceeds from sale

New home purchase price
+ Land transfer tax (varies by province — see Section 12)
+ Legal fees ($1,000-$2,000)
+ Home inspection ($400-$600)
+ Moving costs ($2,000-$5,000)
= Total cost of new home

Net proceeds - Total cost of new home = Freed-up equity
```

### What to Do with Freed-Up Equity
If downsizing frees $200K in equity:
- Fill TFSA ($7,000/year each, ~$14,000/year for a couple)
- Top up RRSP if there's room
- Invest the remainder in a non-registered account (low-cost ETF portfolio)
- At 5% return, $200K generates ~$10,000/year in investment income — a meaningful retirement boost

### Renting After Downsizing
Increasingly popular among retirees:
- **Pros:** Zero maintenance, flexibility to move, invest ALL equity, predictable costs
- **Cons:** No equity building, rent increases (though provincial limits apply in most provinces),
  less control over space, potential for landlord issues
- Financial analysis often favors renting if the freed equity is invested

### Timing
- Spring/early summer: typically best selling season (families want to move before school starts)
- Tax year planning: if you're selling a property that's NOT your principal residence, capital
  gains timing matters (may want to sell in a low-income year)
- Market conditions: work with a realtor to time the listing

### Emotional Considerations
- Attachment to the family home is real. Acknowledge it.
- Community ties, proximity to grandchildren, familiar neighbourhood.
- Frame it as gaining freedom, not losing a home.
- Some people benefit from renting in the new area first before buying.

## 12. Provincial Housing Rules

### Land Transfer Tax (LTT) Summary
**(2025 — verify all thresholds. Provinces update these.)**

**Ontario:**
- 0.5% on first $55,000
- 1.0% on $55,001-$250,000
- 1.5% on $250,001-$400,000
- 2.0% on $400,001-$2,000,000
- 2.5% above $2,000,000
- **Toronto Municipal LTT:** Same rates stacked on top (Toronto buyers pay double)
- **First-time buyer rebate:** Up to $4,000 provincial + $4,475 Toronto municipal
- Example: $700K home in Toronto = ~$11,475 provincial + ~$11,475 municipal = ~$22,950 total
  (minus rebates if first-time buyer)

**British Columbia:**
- 1% on first $200,000
- 2% on $200,001-$2,000,000
- 3% above $2,000,000
- **First-time buyer exemption:** Full exemption up to $500,000, partial to $525,000
- **Foreign buyer tax:** 20% in designated areas (Metro Vancouver, etc.) — verify if still active
- **Speculation and vacancy tax:** 0.5-2% for non-residents and satellite families

**Alberta:**
- **No land transfer tax.** Registration fees only (~$200-$400).
- This is a significant cost advantage for Alberta buyers.

**Quebec:**
- "Welcome tax" (droits de mutation):
  - 0.5% on first $58,900
  - 1.0% on $58,901-$294,600
  - 1.5% on $294,601-$500,000
  - 3.0% above $500,000
  (2025 thresholds — verify)
- Montreal imposes an additional transfer tax above $500,000

**Saskatchewan:** No land transfer tax. Registration fees only.

**Manitoba:**
- 0.5% on first $30,000
- 1.0% on $30,001-$90,000
- 1.5% on $90,001-$150,000
- 2.0% above $150,000

**Atlantic Provinces:** Generally no LTT or minimal. Nova Scotia has a deed transfer tax
(typically 1.5% of sale price). New Brunswick has a land transfer tax of 1%.

### Foreign Buyer Restrictions
- **Federal:** Prohibition on the Purchase of Residential Property by Non-Canadians Act
  (originally until Jan 2027 — verify if extended). Restricts foreign buyers from purchasing
  residential property in Census Metropolitan Areas and Census Agglomerations.
- **BC foreign buyer tax:** 20% in designated areas
- **Ontario Non-Resident Speculation Tax:** 25% (verify if still active)
- Exceptions exist for permanent residents, refugees, and certain work permit holders.

### Property Tax Rates
- Property tax rates vary dramatically by **municipality**, not by province.
- Typical residential rates: 0.5-1.5% of assessed property value per year.
- Assessment: done by provincial assessment authority (MPAC in Ontario, BC Assessment, etc.).
  Not the same as market value — can lag significantly.
- Property tax appeals: possible if you believe your assessment is too high.

(Source: provincial government websites, taxtips.ca)
